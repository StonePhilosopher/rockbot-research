## Species: Pharmacolite

### Identity
- **Formula:** CaHAsO₄·2H₂O
- **Crystal system:** Monoclinic
- **Mineral group:** Phosphate/Arsenate (hydrated calcium arsenate)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 2–2.5
- **Specific gravity:** 2.64–2.73
- **Cleavage:** {010} perfect, {001} distinct
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal to uneven
- **Luster:** Vitreous to pearly
- **Streak:** White
- **Tenacity:** Brittle; very soft

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Colorless, white, pale gray, pale yellow; may be tinted pinkish or greenish by impurities
- **Color causes:** Trace Fe (yellowish tint), Mn (pinkish), or Cu (greenish); pure material is colorless
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Delicate radiating or stellate crystal aggregates; acicular to fibrous habit. Often forms powdery or earthy coatings on arsenic-rich rocks. Efflorescent — loses water and crumbles in dry air. Very soft and fragile.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Acicular (needle-like) to prismatic; radiating aggregates and stellate groups
- **Common forms/faces:** {010}, {001}, {110} — tabular and prismatic forms common in monoclinic system
- **Twin laws:** Not common; contact twins on {100} reported but rare
- **Varieties:** None recognized — pharmacolite is a distinct species
- **Special morphologies:**
  - **Radiating aggregates:** Like starbursts of white needles
  - **Stalactitic:** Cavity fillings with concentric layers
  - **Earthy/powdery:** Massive, dull, friable material coating rock surfaces
  - **Encrustations:** Thin coatings on oxidized arsenic minerals

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 10–50°C (supergene/weathering zone)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 15–35°C (ambient surface temperatures)
- **Decomposition temperature:** Loses water at ~150°C, decomposes at higher temperatures; in nature, effloresces (loses H₂O) in arid conditions
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Lower temperatures favor acicular/radiating habits; warmer conditions may produce more equant crystals

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Ca (minimum ~100 ppm), As (minimum ~50 ppm)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - P (trace to 10 ppm) — may substitute for As in structure (forms transition to brushite/hureaulite family)
  - Fe (trace) — may cause yellowish tint
  - Cu (trace) — may cause greenish tint
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Pb (favors mimetite/pyromorphite instead); high Cu (favors olivenite/conichalcite); high Zn (favors adamite)
- **Required pH range:** Neutral to mildly acidic (pH 5.5–7.5)
- **Required Eh range:** Oxidizing (As must be in +5 oxidation state — arsenate)
- **Required O₂ range:** High (oxidizing environment required)

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No direct releases; but formation consumes H⁺ (slightly raises pH)
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Minor acidity neutralization
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Releases As (toxic) and Ca upon dissolution or heating

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Fast (for a supergene mineral)
- **Maximum crystal size:** Up to 2 cm (rare); typical 1–5 mm
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–5 mm (small, delicate)
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — growth is faster at higher temperatures but dehydration also accelerates
- **Competes with:** Haidingerite (CaHAsO₄·H₂O, the monohydrate — same formula minus one water); conichalcite (CaCu(AsO₄)(OH), when Cu is present); arseniosiderite (Ca₂Fe₃(AsO₄)₃O₂·3H₂O, when Fe is abundant)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes — loses water (dehydrates to haidingerite at ~80–100°C, then to anhydrous calcium arsenate at ~150°C)
- **Breaks down in light?** No
- **Dissolves in water?** Sparingly soluble (slightly soluble in water; more soluble in acid)
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — dissolves readily in dilute HCl and HNO₃
- **Oxidizes?** No — already fully oxidized (As⁵⁺)
- **Dehydrates?** Yes — efflorescent; loses water in dry air, converting to haidingerite or powdery anhydrous material
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Generally stable; may show weak radioactivity if associated with U/Th minerals

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Primary arsenides (loellingite, arsenopyrite, native arsenic) or sulfarsenides must oxidize first to release As
- **Forms BEFORE:** More stable arsenates like arseniosiderite (Fe-rich), conichalcite (Cu-rich), or annabergite (Ni-rich) depending on available cations
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - **Arsenic source minerals:** arsenopyrite, loellingite, native arsenic, cobaltite
  - **Co-precipitates:** haidingerite (monohydrate), arseniosiderite, conichalcite, olivenite
  - **Carbonates:** calcite, dolomite, aragonite (Ca source)
  - **Iron oxides:** goethite, limonite (oxidation products)
- **Zone:** Supergene/oxidation zone — forms at or near the surface in oxidized arsenic deposits
- **Geological environment:**
  1. **Oxidized arsenic vein deposits:** Weathering of arsenopyrite/loellingite veins in granitic or metamorphic rocks
  2. **Cobalt mining districts:** Associated with cobaltite, erythrite, and other Co-As minerals
  3. **Arid to semi-arid climates:** Efflorescent crusts on outcrops in dry conditions
  4. **Historical mine dumps:** Secondary mineralization on waste rock

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1: Jáchymov (Joachimsthal), Czech Republic** — Historic silver and cobalt mining district. Type locality for pharmacolite (described in 1800s). Found in oxidized veins with erythrite, annabergite, and native silver. Famous for secondary arsenate mineral suite.
- **Classic locality 2: Cobalt, Ontario, Canada** — The type locality for cobaltite and a classic Co-Ag district. Pharmacolite occurs in oxidized portions of veins with erythrite, annabergite, and haidingerite.
- **Classic locality 3: Schneeberg, Saxony, Germany** — Historic silver-cobalt-arsenic mining district. Beautiful acicular pharmacolite in radiating aggregates associated with native bismuth and cobaltite.
- **Classic locality 4: Lavrion, Greece** — Ancient mining district with extensive oxidation zone. Pharmacolite found in complex secondary arsenate assemblages.
- **Notable specimens:**
  - Radiating spray specimens from Jáchymov — white needle clusters to 2 cm
  - Cobalt district specimens with pinkish tint from Mn
  - Greek specimens with exceptional transparency

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally no
- **SW (255nm) color:** None (or very weak creamy white if any)
- **MW (310nm) color:** None
- **LW (365nm) color:** None
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** None confirmed; very weak response may be from trace impurities
- **Quenched by:** N/A

### Flavor Text

> **The Poison Flower**
>
> Pharmacolite is what happens when arsenic meets calcium in the presence of oxygen and water. It is a supergene mineral — born not in the deep earth's heat but at the surface, in the chemical weathering zone where primary sulfides and arsenides surrender their elements to the atmosphere. The result is delicate: white, needle-fine crystals that radiate from rock surfaces like frost or coral, so fragile they crumble at a touch.
>
> The name comes from the Greek *pharmakon* — poison — because arsenic is its essential component. Pharmacolite does not hide its nature. It forms on the oxidized margins of arsenopyrite veins, on the waste piles of old cobalt mines, in the dry air of mineral districts where the groundwater carries dissolved arsenate. Handle it with care: the arsenic is locked in the crystal structure, but dissolution or crushing can release it.
>
> In the vugg, pharmacolite would be a late-stage oxidation product, forming only after the primary arsenic minerals have had time to weather. It needs calcium — from calcite, from dolomite, from the groundwater itself — and it needs the arsenic to be fully oxidized to the +5 state. The pH must be near-neutral; too acidic and the calcium dissolves, too alkaline and the arsenic precipitates as different compounds.
>
> The crystals are evanescent. In humid conditions they hold their two waters of hydration and maintain their form. In dry air they effloresce, losing water, turning powdery, crumbling back to earth. They are a mineral of the moment, not the ages — here today, altered tomorrow, teaching the lesson that not all crystallization is permanent. Some minerals exist only in the narrow window between dissolution and dehydration, between oxidation and leaching. Pharmacolite is one of them: a poison flower, blooming briefly in the chemical no-man's-land of the oxidation zone.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:**
  - `Ca` (calcium) — **CRITICAL:** Not currently in trace system. Pharmacolite cannot be implemented without Ca.
  - Arsenic oxidation tracking (As³⁺ → As⁵⁺) — currently implicit in game; needs explicit state for arsenate minerals
- **New events needed:**
  - Arsenic oxidation event — conversion of arsenide/sulfarsenide to arsenate
  - Efflorescence event — dehydration in arid conditions
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF Ca > 100 ppm
  AND As > 50 ppm
  AND As_oxidation_state = +5
  AND temperature < 50°C
  AND temperature > 5°C
  AND pH > 5.5
  AND pH < 7.5
  AND Eh > 0.3 (oxidizing)
  AND O2 > 10 ppm
  AND humidity > 60% (prevents efflorescence)
  AND NOT (vug already has pharmacolite_max)
THEN nucleate pharmacolite
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF pharmacolite exists
  AND Ca > 50 ppm
  AND As > 25 ppm
  AND As_oxidation_state = +5
  AND temperature < 40°C
  AND pH > 5.0
  AND humidity > 50%
THEN grow at rate = base_rate * (Ca_ppm / 200) * (As_ppm / 100)
  where growth accelerates in humid conditions
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - High humidity + slow growth: acicular/radiating (classic pharmacolite)
  - Low humidity: efflorescent/powdery (degraded)
  - High Ca/As ratio: larger, more equant crystals
- **Decomposition products:**
  - Heat/dehydration → haidingerite (CaHAsO₄·H₂O) + water
  - Further heat → anhydrous calcium arsenate + water
  - Acid dissolution → Ca²⁺ + HAsO₄²⁻ + H₂O

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Radiating Pharmacolite** — Classic white needle clusters in stellate aggregates. Forms under stable humid conditions. Most collectible form.
- **Variant 2: Efflorescent Crust** — Powdery, friable, dull white coating. Forms in arid conditions or after dehydration. Lower value, represents degradation.
- **Variant 3: Stalactitic** — Concentric layered growth in cavities. Rare but visually striking. Represents prolonged, slow growth in protected vugs.

## Species: Beryl

### Identity
- **Formula:** Be₃Al₂Si₆O₁₈
- **Crystal system:** Hexagonal (dihexagonal dipyramidal class, 6/mmm)
- **Mineral group:** Cyclosilicate (silicate with six-membered SiO₄ rings)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 7.5–8.0
- **Specific gravity:** 2.63–2.92
- **Cleavage:** Imperfect on {0001} (basal)
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal to irregular
- **Luster:** Vitreous to resinous
- **Streak:** White
- **Tenacity:** Brittle
- **Optical:** Uniaxial (−); nω = 1.564–1.595, nε = 1.568–1.602; birefringence δ = 0.004–0.007

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Colorless when pure (goshenite). Green (emerald), blue to bluish-green (aquamarine), pink to peach (morganite), yellow to golden (heliodor/golden beryl), red (red beryl/bixbite)
- **Color causes:**
  - **Emerald green:** Cr³⁺ and/or V³⁺ substituting for Al³⁺ in octahedral sites
  - **Aquamarine blue:** Fe²⁺ in channel sites (pale blue); Fe²⁺ + Fe³⁺ together → deeper blue (maxixe)
  - **Heliodor yellow:** Fe³⁺ in channel sites
  - **Morganite pink:** Mn²⁺ substituting for Al³⁺ (and possibly Cs⁺ in channels)
  - **Red beryl:** Mn³⁺ (extremely rare, topaz-bearing rhyolites only)
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent; gemmy transparent varieties highly valued
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Hexagonal prism cross-section with flat basal pinacoid terminations (pyramidal terminations rare). Striated lengthwise on prism faces. Can show weak pleochroism. Cat's-eye chatoyancy rare but known.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Long prismatic hexagonal crystals, often striated parallel to c-axis. Terminated crystals have flat basal pinacoid {0001}; pyramidal terminations extremely rare.
- **Common forms/faces:** {1010} prism, {0001} basal pinacoid. Occasionally short and stubby.
- **Twin laws:** Rare. Contact twins on {0001} or {1010} reported but uncommon.
- **Varieties (named by color):**
  - **Emerald:** Deep green, Cr³⁺/V³⁺-colored. Most valuable gem variety.
  - **Aquamarine:** Greenish-blue to blue, Fe²⁺-colored. Heat-treatable to sky-blue.
  - **Morganite:** Pink to peach, Mn²⁺-colored. Surged in popularity since ~2010.
  - **Heliodor / Golden Beryl:** Yellow to golden-yellow, Fe³⁺-colored.
  - **Goshenite:** Colorless or white. Named after Goshen, Massachusetts.
  - **Red Beryl (Bixbite):** Deep red, Mn³⁺-colored. Extremely rare; only two localities in Utah (Wah Wah Mountains, Thomas Range).
  - **Maxixe:** Deep blue, unstable; color fades in light (both Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺ in channels).
  - **Green Beryl:** Pale green, lighter than emerald. Sometimes grouped with heliodor.
- **Special morphologies:** Massive, granular, columnar aggregates. Drusy coatings rare. Etched crystals common in pocket environments. Some specimens reach enormous size — largest known natural crystal of any mineral is beryl.

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 450–700°C (magmatic pegmatite); 300–450°C (hydrothermal veins/pockets)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 380 ± 80°C for gem-quality miarolitic pocket beryl (fluid inclusion data from Luumäki, Finland)
- **Decomposition temperature:** >800°C (beryl melts incongruently; breakdown to chrysoberyl + quartz at very high T)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - High-T magmatic (500–700°C): common beryl, often opaque, associated with quartz + K-feldspar + albite + muscovite in pegmatite intermediate zones
  - Lower-T hydrothermal (300–450°C): gem-quality beryl in miarolitic cavities and fractures; larger, clearer crystals, lower alkali content
  - Very low-T (rhyolite cavities): red beryl forms at ~650°C but under low pressure, near-surface miarolitic cavities

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:**
  - **Be:** minimum ~10 ppm (beryllium is extremely rare in crust; average 2.8 g/ton in crustal rock)
  - **Al:** significant concentration (8% of crust, readily available)
  - **Si:** abundant (27.7% of crust)
  - **O:** ubiquitous
  - **Optional chromophores for color varieties:**
    - Cr³⁺ (for emerald): ~100–1000 ppm
    - V³⁺ (for emerald): ~100–500 ppm
    - Fe²⁺ (for aquamarine): ~500–3000 ppm
    - Fe³⁺ (for heliodor): ~500–2000 ppm
    - Mn²⁺ (for morganite): ~200–1000 ppm
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - **Cs, Na, K, Li:** enter channel sites; alkali-rich beryl associated with more evolved pegmatites. Cs content correlates with pegmatite fractionation degree.
  - **Sc:** can substitute for Al → bazzite (separate species)
  - **Mg, Fe (non-chromophore):** structural substitutions
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - **High Ca:** competes for Al; beryl does not tolerate much Ca
  - **High Ti:** tends to form rutile/sphene instead of entering beryl
  - **Very high Fe²⁺ (>>1%):** can suppress transparency, make beryl cloudy or opaque
- **Required pH range:** Slightly acidic to neutral (pH 5–7 typical for pegmatite fluids)
- **Required Eh range:** Generally oxidizing for Fe³⁺/Mn³⁺ varieties; reducing for Fe²⁺ (aquamarine)
- **Required O₂ range:** Moderate; not strongly controlling

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No significant byproducts. Beryl crystallizes from silicate-rich fluid/melt.
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Consumes Be, Al, Si from fluid; may locally deplete these elements
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** At very high T (>800°C), decomposes to chrysoberyl (BeAl₂O₄) + quartz + fluid

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Slow (compared to quartz). Beryl is a late-stage mineral in most environments.
- **Maximum crystal size:** Extraordinary. Largest known natural crystal of any mineral: beryl from Malakialina, Madagascar — 18 m (59 ft) long, 3.5 m (11 ft) diameter, 380,000 kg (840,000 lb). New England pegmatites produced crystals to 8.2 m (27 ft), 26 tonnes.
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** Gem pockets: 1–20 cm. Common pegmatite beryl: up to 1 m or more.
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes. Higher T = faster growth but more inclusion-rich, less gemmy. Lower T = slower, clearer, more perfect crystals.
- **Competes with:** Quartz (for Si), feldspar (for Al), muscovite (for Al + K), tourmaline (for B + Al in boron-rich systems)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes, above ~800°C decomposes to chrysoberyl + quartz
- **Breaks down in light?** Maxixe (deep blue) fades in sunlight/UV; other varieties stable
- **Dissolves in water?** Very low solubility; essentially insoluble
- **Dissolves in acid?** Insoluble in acids
- **Oxidizes?** No; beryl is chemically inert
- **Dehydrates?** No water in structure (channel sites may contain H₂O or alkali ions but not structurally bound)
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Generally stable. Maxixe color can be affected by radiation (both natural and artificial).

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Early pegmatite minerals — quartz, K-feldspar, albite, muscovite, biotite. Beryl is a late-stage or miarolitic-cavity mineral.
- **Forms BEFORE:** Very late-stage minerals — lepidolite, cookeite, pollucite, petalite, spodumene (in Li-rich pegmatites); secondary alteration minerals.
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - Pegmatite: quartz, K-feldspar (orthoclase/microcline), albite, muscovite, biotite, tourmaline, garnet, columbite-tantalite, spodumene, lepidolite
  - Hydrothermal veins: quartz, muscovite, topaz (in rhyolites for red beryl)
  - Metamorphic: mica schist with quartz, feldspar, muscovite
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene (pegmatite, miarolitic cavities), some hydrothermal
- **Geological environment:**
  - **Granite pegmatites:** most common; especially LCT (Li-Cs-Ta) and NYF (Nb-Y-F) type pegmatites
  - **Miarolitic cavities:** pocket beryl, often gem-quality
  - **High-T hydrothermal veins:** associated with Sn-W deposits
  - **Metamorphic rocks:** mica schists (Ural Mountains, Russia)
  - **Rhyolite cavities:** red beryl only (topaz-bearing rhyolites, Utah)

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1:** **Bumpus Quarry, Albany, Maine, USA** — produced some of the largest beryl crystals ever found (up to 8.2 m / 27 ft, 26 tonnes). New Hampshire's state mineral. Operated 1927–1960s.
- **Classic locality 2:** **Malakialina, Madagascar** — world's largest known naturally occurring crystal of any mineral: 18 m × 3.5 m, 380,000 kg beryl. Discovered 1999.
- **Classic locality 3:** **Shigar Valley, northern Pakistan** — spectacular gem aquamarine crystals, vivid blue, hexagonal prisms with perfect terminations. Modern classic.
- **Notable specimens:**
  - **Emerald:** Muzo mine, Colombia (finest emeralds in the world). Also Zambia, Brazil, Russia (Ural Mountains).
  - **Aquamarine:** Santa Maria mine, Brazil (deep blue "Santa Maria" aquamarine); Pakistan; Madagascar.
  - **Morganite:** Pala, California; Madagascar; Brazil.
  - **Red Beryl:** Wah Wah Mountains, Utah (only viable source; crystals rarely >2 cm, extremely valuable).
  - **Goshenite:** Goshen, Massachusetts (type locality for colorless variety).

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally **no** for pure beryl. Some varieties show weak fluorescence.
  - **Morganite:** Weak violet fluorescence under LW UV
  - **Emerald:** Essentially non-fluorescent (fracture-filling oils/resins may fluoresce, but stone itself does not)
  - **Aquamarine:** Non-fluorescent
  - **Heliodor:** Non-fluorescent
  - **Goshenite:** Non-fluorescent
  - Some common beryl may show weak yellow, light blue, pink, or red fluorescence depending on trace elements
- **SW (255nm):** Generally none
- **MW (310nm):** Generally none; morganite may show weak violet
- **LW (365nm):** Generally none; morganite weak violet
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** Mn²⁺ in morganite (weak violet). Some fracture-filling materials fluoresce but are not native to the mineral.
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺ suppresses any potential fluorescence (common in many beryl varieties)

- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF trace_Be > 10 ppm
AND trace_Al > 1000 ppm  // or inferred from SiO₂ system
AND SiO2 > 600  // high silica environment
AND temperature BETWEEN 300 AND 700
AND pH BETWEEN 5 AND 7
AND (pegmatite_fractionation_event OR miarolitic_cavity_event)
→ nucleate beryl (max 1 per pocket — slow, exclusive mineral)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF beryl exists
AND trace_Be > 5 ppm
AND trace_Al > 500 ppm
AND SiO2 > 400
AND temperature BETWEEN 300 AND 700
AND NOT (temperature > 800)  // decomposes to chrysoberyl + quartz
→ grow at rate = SLOW (0.3× quartz baseline)
  // slower at lower T, faster but less gemmy at higher T
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - If T > 500°C and growth rate > moderate → short, stubby, opaque common beryl
  - If T 300–450°C and growth rate slow → long prismatic, gemmy, transparent
  - If miarolitic_cavity and slow growth → large hexagonal prism with flat basal termination
  - If hydrothermal vein → smaller, often etched crystals
- **Decomposition products:**
  - At T > 800°C: chrysoberyl (BeAl₂O₄) + quartz + fluid
  - No low-T breakdown products

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Goshenite (Colorless)** — Pure beryl, no chromophores. Forms when Be/Al/Si available but no Cr/V/Fe/Mn. Transparent, colorless, hexagonal prism. Classic pegmatite environment.
- **Variant 2: Aquamarine (Blue)** — Fe²⁺ in channels. Requires Fe > 500 ppm, slightly reducing conditions. Pale blue to deep blue (maxixe if Fe²⁺+Fe³⁺ both present). Heat treatment stable (unlike maxixe).
- **Variant 3: Emerald (Green)** — Cr³⁺ and/or V³⁺ substituting for Al³⁺. Requires Cr > 100 ppm or V > 100 ppm. Most valuable gem variety. Typically forms in schist-hosted or black shale-hosted deposits (Colombia) rather than classic pegmatites — separate geological environment.
- **Variant 4: Morganite (Pink)** — Mn²⁺ substituting for Al³⁺. Requires Mn > 200 ppm, oxidizing conditions. Soft peach to vivid pink. Modern popular gem.
- **Variant 5: Heliodor (Golden)** — Fe³⁺ in channels. Requires Fe > 500 ppm, oxidizing conditions. Yellow to golden-yellow. "Gift of the sun" — named from Greek helios (sun) + doron (gift).
- **Variant 6: Red Beryl (Bixbite)** — Mn³⁺ in topaz-bearing rhyolite cavities, low pressure, high T (~650°C). Extremely rare. Only viable from Utah, USA. Separate nucleation rule: requires rhyolite environment.

## Linked Paragenetic Sequences

Beryl does **not** belong to a simple oxidation/supergene paragenetic sequence like the copper minerals. Instead, it is part of the **pegmatite fractionation sequence**:

1. **Early magmatic:** Quartz + K-feldspar + albite + muscovite + biotite
2. **Intermediate:** Common beryl (opaque, often alkali-rich) + tourmaline + garnet
3. **Late magmatic / miarolitic:** Gem beryl (transparent, lower alkali) + lepidolite + spodumene + pollucite + cookeite
4. **Very late hydrothermal:** Secondary phosphates, clay minerals, alteration products

Within the beryl family itself, color varieties do **not** form a temporal sequence — they form based on available chromophores in the local fluid, not on progressive oxidation. Emerald (Cr/V) and aquamarine (Fe) can form simultaneously in different parts of the same deposit if fluid chemistry varies.

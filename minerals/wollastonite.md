# Wollastonite

## Identity
- **Formula:** CaSiO₃ (calcium metasilicate)
- **Crystal system:** Triclinic (wollastonite-1T); monoclinic polymorph exists (parawollastonite / wollastonite-2M, rare)
- **Mineral group:** Inosilicate (chain silicate) — wollastonite group
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 4.5–5.0
- **Specific gravity:** 2.86–3.09
- **Cleavage:** Perfect in two directions at near 90°; good {100} and {001} cleavage sets
- **Fracture:** Splintery to uneven
- **Luster:** Vitreous to dull, pearly on cleavage surfaces
- **Tenacity:** Brittle; may contain small amounts of Fe, Mg, Mn substituting for Ca (ferrowollastonite when Fe-bearing)

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** White, colorless, or gray; ferrowollastonite = gray to brown
- **Color causes:** Pure end-member is white/colorless; Fe²⁺/Mn²⁺ substitution → gray, brown, or pale green tints
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Cleavage fragments are bladed or lath-like with pearly luster. Aggregates may be fibrous, radiating, or compact masses. Under UV, fluoresces vivid orange-yellow to deep orange (Franklin/Sterling Hill specimens).

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Rare as isolated tabular crystals; commonly massive in lamellar, radiating, compact, or fibrous aggregates
- **Common forms/faces:** Tabular when crystalline; lath-like cleavage fragments; fibrous and acicular in skarns
- **Twin laws:** Common twinning (polysynthetic)
- **Varieties:**
  - **Ferrowollastonite:** Fe-substituted, gray to brown
- **Special morphologies:** Fibrous (asbestos-like) in some skarns; radiating blades in contact metamorphic rocks; compact granular in marble

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 400–800°C (contact metamorphic)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 500–700°C
- **Decomposition temperature:** 1540°C (melting point)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Higher T favors coarse bladed crystals; lower T in retrograde veins favors fibrous or acicular forms

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Ca (≥2000 ppm), Si (≥2000 ppm)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Fe (≤500 ppm for ferrowollastonite), Mn (≤200 ppm), Mg (≤300 ppm)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High CO₂ (stabilizes calcite over wollastonite); high Al (diverts to grossular or other skarn garnets); high Na (favors pectolite at lower T)
- **Required pH range:** 6.0–8.5 (neutral to mildly alkaline)
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing to mildly oxidizing
- **Required O₂ range:** Low to moderate

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Yes — decarbonation reaction: calcite + quartz → wollastonite + CO₂↑
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** CO₂ release (important in skarn systems — drives fluid flow)
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Releases Ca²⁺, silicic acid; may release CO₂ if re-forming calcite in retrograde conditions

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Slow — requires sustained high T and siliceous carbonate host
- **Maximum crystal size in nature:** Several cm (bladed crystals in skarns); massive deposits are industrial-scale
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** Rare in vugs; more commonly in veins or skarn masses. Where found in vugs: 2–10 mm bladed crystals
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — retrograde solubility means wollastonite is more soluble at lower T; thus growth is favored during prograde heating, not cooling
- **Competes with:** Calcite (if CO₂ present), diopside (if Mg present), grossular (if Al present), quartz (for Si)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Melts at 1540°C (very stable); begins to break down in presence of water above ~600°C to form idocrase or other skarn minerals
- **Breaks down in light?** No — stable under light
- **Dissolves in water?** Very low solubility (~0.095 g/L at neutral pH)
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — soluble in HCl and mineral acids; attacked by formic, acetic, citric, and lactic acids
- **Oxidizes?** No — stable to oxidation; Fe-bearing varieties may show minor surface staining
- **Dehydrates?** No water of crystallization; structurally anhydrous
- **Radiation sensitivity:** No known effect

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Calcite or dolomite host rock + silica-bearing fluid intrusion (contact metamorphism); requires pre-existing carbonate
- **Forms BEFORE:** Retrograde minerals (serpentine, talc, chlorite) may replace wollastonite during cooling and hydration
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Calcite, quartz, diopside, grossular, vesuvianite (idocrase), scapolite, plagioclase, epidote, magnetite (in skarns); garnet, pyroxene
- **Zone:** Contact metamorphic aureole; skarn deposits; some high-T veins in carbonate rocks
- **Geological environment:** Skarns (most important), contact-metamorphosed limestones/dolomites, some carbonatites, rare alpine veins

### Famous Localities
- **Willsboro / Lewis, Essex County, New York, USA:** World's largest wollastonite producer. Skarn deposits in Grenville marble. Industrial and fluorescent mineral locality. Coarse bladed crystals with diopside and garnet.
- **Franklin & Sterling Hill, New Jersey, USA:** Famous fluorescent wollastonite — vivid orange-yellow to deep orange under SW UV. Associated with franklinite, willemite, calcite. Some of the most spectacular fluorescent mineral specimens in the world.
- **Ratschach, Styria, Austria:** Historic European locality — white radiating aggregates in marble
- **Notable specimens:** Franklin wollastonite under SW UV is a classic fluorescent display piece; Willsboro specimens show coarse bladed crystals to several cm intergrown with olive-green diopside and red-brown garnet.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Yes (variable; location-dependent)
- **SW (255nm) color:** Orange-yellow to deep orange (Franklin/Sterling Hill); may also appear bluish-white, yellowish-white, or yellow (other localities)
- **MW (310nm) color:** Yellow-orange to orange
- **LW (365nm) color:** Weak to moderate yellow-orange
- **Phosphorescent?** Not typically
- **Activator:** Mn²⁺ (trace substitution in Ca site); REE in some localities
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺ (quenches fluorescence in ferrowollastonite)

### Flavor Text

> Wollastonite is what happens when limestone decides to stop being a sediment and become a silicate instead. A marble meets a granite intrusion, the heat rips CO₂ from calcite, and what remains recombines with silica into blades of calcium metasilicate — thin, pearly, and surprisingly tough. In the dark of a skarn, wollastonite grows as laths and fibers, sometimes fluorescent orange under UV like a secret only ultraviolet eyes can read. The Franklin wollastonites are famous for this: ordinary white rock by day, blazing orange suns by night under shortwave lamp. A mineral that keeps its best color hidden until you look at it the right way.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** None — uses existing Ca, Si system
- **New events needed:** `event_skarn_contact` — high-T metamorphic event requiring carbonate host + siliceous fluid
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF temperature >= 400°C AND temperature <= 800°C
AND Ca >= 2000 ppm
AND Si >= 2000 ppm
AND carbonate_host == true (calcite or dolomite present)
AND pH >= 6.0 AND pH <= 8.5
AND CO2 < 1000 ppm (decarbonation favors wollastonite)
→ nucleate wollastonite
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF temperature >= 500°C AND temperature <= 700°C
AND Ca and Si available
AND carbonate_host present
AND no high-Al competition (suppress if Al > 3000 ppm)
→ grow at rate 2 (slow, bladed/fibrous)
IF Fe >= 200 ppm → chance for ferrowollastonite variant (gray-brown)
IF Mn >= 100 ppm → enhanced fluorescence (orange SW)
```
- **Habit selection logic:** Bladed/lamellar default in skarns; fibrous if retrograde (cooling & hydration); rare tabular crystals only in exceptional conditions
- **Decomposition products:** Melting at 1540°C → Ca-Si glass; in acid → dissolved Ca²⁺ + silicic acid; in retrograde hydration → idocrase/serpentine/talc depending on available Mg/Fe/Al

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Skarn Blade** — standard white bladed wollastonite, pearly luster. Most common.
- **Variant 2: Fluorescent Franklin** — Mn-activated orange-yellow fluorescence under SW UV. Franklin/Sterling Hill signature. Higher value.
- **Variant 3: Ferrowollastonite** — Fe-substituted gray to brown variety, duller luster, non-fluorescent. Lower value, industrial grade.

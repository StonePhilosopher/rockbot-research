# Prehnite

## Identity
- **Formula:** Ca₂Al(AlSi₃O₁₀)(OH)₂
- **Crystal system:** Orthorhombic
- **Mineral group:** Inosilicate transitional to phyllosilicate (structurally ambiguous; Nickel-Strunz: inosilicate; Dana: phyllosilicate)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 6–6.5
- **Specific gravity:** 2.8–2.95
- **Cleavage:** Distinct on {001}
- **Fracture:** Brittle; conchoidal to uneven on aggregates
- **Luster:** Vitreous to pearly

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Pale green, yellow-green, apple green, mint green; less often colorless, blue, pink, or white
- **Color causes:** Green from Fe³⁺ substituting for Al (limited substitution, up to ~10%); colorless when pure; yellow-green from trace Ti; brownish from iron oxidation
- **Transparency:** Semi-transparent to translucent
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Most often forms as botryoidal, reniform, globular, or stalactitic aggregates — "grape clusters" or "cabbage heads." Individual crystal faces are tiny and rare; the aggregate surface is bubbly and rounded. Famous for **epimorphs** — hollow casts after dissolved anhydrite, glauberite, or laumontite crystals.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Botryoidal, reniform, globular, stalactitic aggregates; rare individual crystals are small, curved, or composite
- **Common forms/faces:** Tiny pyramidal faces on aggregate surfaces; curved and wedge-shaped crystals
- **Twin laws:** Fine lamellar twinning (common in aggregates)
- **Varieties:**
  - **Ferriprehnite:** Fe³⁺-dominant analogue (rare)
  - **Epimorph prehnite:** hollow casts after dissolved crystals — most visually distinctive form
- **Special morphologies:** Finger-like growths; bubbly crystal masses; epimorphs (hollow casts); "cabbage head" botryoidal clusters; stalactitic drapes in cavities

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 150–400°C (prehnite-pumpellyite facies)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 200–350°C
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~600°C (dehydration and structural breakdown to Ca-Al pyroxene/garnet)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Lower T (150–250°C) favors botryoidal and epimorph forms; higher T (250–400°C) favors more crystalline, bladed, or columnar forms

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Ca (≥1500 ppm), Al (≥1000 ppm), Si (≥2000 ppm)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Fe³⁺ (≤500 ppm, for green color), Ti (≤100 ppm, yellow-green tint), Mg (≤200 ppm, may substitute slightly)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Na (favors pectolite or zeolites); high K (favors K-feldspar or muscovite); high CO₂ (favors calcite); very high T >400°C (favors epidote or grossular)
- **Required pH range:** 6.5–8.5 (near-neutral to mildly alkaline)
- **Required Eh range:** Mildly oxidizing (Fe³⁺ stable) to near-neutral
- **Required O₂ range:** Low to moderate

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No significant release
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Consumes Ca²⁺, Al³⁺, SiO₂(aq), and OH⁻ from fluid
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Releases Ca²⁺, Al³⁺, silicic acid; above 600°C releases H₂O from OH groups

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Slow to moderate — slower than zeolites, comparable to epidote at lower T
- **Maximum crystal size in nature:** Aggregates to 10+ cm; individual crystals rarely >5 mm
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 2–15 mm botryoidal clusters; epimorphs may preserve shapes of original crystals (anhydrite to 3 cm)
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — growth accelerates 200–350°C; below 150°C zeolites outcompete; above 400°C epidote takes over
- **Competes with:** Zeolites (at lower T), epidote (at higher T), calcite (if CO₂ present), datolite, pectolite (if Na present)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes, >600°C dehydration and structural rearrangement to pyroxene/grossular assemblage
- **Breaks down in light?** No — stable under light
- **Dissolves in water?** Very low solubility
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — attacked by strong acids (HCl), especially the Ca component; slower than calcite
- **Oxidizes?** Fe³⁺ is already oxidized; further oxidation may darken color but doesn't destroy structure
- **Dehydrates?** Yes, above ~600°C (structural OH loss)
- **Radiation sensitivity:** No known effect

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Plagioclase feldspar alteration in basalt; often after laumontite or other early zeolites in vesicles
- **Forms BEFORE:** Epidote (at higher T), quartz, calcite (in later veins)
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Epidote, calcite, datolite, pectolite, zeolites (laumontite, stilbite, heulandite), chlorite, quartz, copper minerals (in some localities)
- **Zone:** Prehnite-pumpellyite facies (transitional between zeolite facies and greenschist facies); basalt vesicles and vugs; hydrothermal veins in mafic rocks; some granitic pegmatites and alpine clefts
- **Geological environment:** Basalt cavities (classic), hydrothermal alteration of mafic rocks, contact metamorphic aureoles (rare), some granites and syenites, alpine clefts in gneiss

### Famous Localities
- **Upper New Street Quarry / Prospect Park, Paterson, New Jersey, USA:** World-class botryoidal prehnite on basalt — bright apple-green "grape clusters" and epimorphs after anhydrite. The Paterson prehnites are arguably the most famous in the world for crystal quality.
- **Kayes Region, Mali:** Stunning bright green botryoidal prehnite balls on dark epidote — some of the most aesthetic prehnite specimens. Deep saturated green, high translucency.
- **Karoo dolerites, Cape of Good Hope, South Africa:** Type locality — discovered 1774, named 1788 after Colonel Hendrik von Prehn. Historic significance as first mineral named after a person.
- **Notable specimens:** Paterson epimorphs after anhydrite — hollow prehnite casts of perfectly formed anhydrite crystals, ghostly and beautiful. Mali "prehnite balls" on epidote are cabinet-grade display pieces. Sowerbutt Quarry (Prospect Park) epimorphs after laumontite are particularly fine.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Yes (variable)
- **SW (255nm) color:** Blue-white to mild peach
- **MW (310nm) color:** Blue-white
- **LW (365nm) color:** Yellow to yellow-green (most distinctive under LW)
- **Phosphorescent?** Not typically
- **Activator:** Uranyl? (U trace substitution); REE; possibly Mn²⁺ in some localities
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺ (suppresses in Fe-rich specimens)

### Flavor Text

> Prehnite doesn't grow crystals so much as it grows *clusters* — rounded, bubbly, botryoidal masses that look like bunches of green grapes frozen in stone. Named in 1788 for Colonel Hendrik von Prehn, making it the first mineral ever named after a person, it has spent the last two centuries proving that aggregates can be more beautiful than single crystals. The Paterson, New Jersey localities are legendary: apple-green botryoidal heads so perfect they look cultivated, and epimorphs — hollow prehnite shells that preserve the shape of dissolved anhydrite crystals like ghost architecture. It's the champion of second chances: a mineral that builds beautiful things from the empty spaces other minerals leave behind.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** None — uses existing Ca, Al, Si, OH system
- **New events needed:** `event_prehnite_vein` — low-grade metamorphic/hydrothermal event in mafic rocks; `event_anhydrite_dissolution` — precursor mineral dissolves, leaving cavity for prehnite epimorph
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF temperature >= 150°C AND temperature <= 400°C
AND Ca >= 1500 ppm
AND Al >= 1000 ppm
AND Si >= 2000 ppm
AND pH >= 6.5 AND pH <= 8.5
AND mafic_host == true (basalt, gabbro, diabase)
AND Na < 1500 ppm (suppress if Na too high — zeolites win)
→ nucleate prehnite (botryoidal habit)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF temperature >= 200°C AND temperature <= 350°C
AND Ca, Al, Si available
AND mafic_host present
AND no high-Na competition
→ grow at rate 3 (moderate, botryoidal/globular)
IF Fe >= 200 ppm → green coloration (apple-green to yellow-green)
IF anhydrite_dissolved == true AND prehnite_nucleated nearby
→ form epimorph variant (hollow cast, higher value)
IF temperature > 350°C → suppress growth (epidote outcompetes)
```
- **Habit selection logic:** Botryoidal/globular default in vugs and vesicles; stalactitic if growing from cavity roof; epimorph if anhydrite/glauberite/laumontite dissolved nearby; rare bladed/columnar only in high-T alpine veins
- **Decomposition products:** Above 600°C → grossular + pyroxene + H₂O; in acid → dissolved Ca²⁺ + Al³⁺ + silicic acid

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Apple Green Botryoid** — standard prehnite, pale to apple green, botryoidal clusters. Most common.
- **Variant 2: Epimorph Ghost** — hollow cast after dissolved anhydrite crystal, preserving original crystal shape. Higher value, rare. Only forms after anhydrite dissolution event.
- **Variant 3: Mali Deep Green** — Fe³⁺-rich saturated green variety from Mali-type localities. Higher translucency, higher value.

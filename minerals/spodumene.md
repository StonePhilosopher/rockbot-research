## Species: Spodumene

### Identity
- **Formula:** LiAlSi₂O₆ (lithium aluminium inosilicate)
- **Crystal system:** Monoclinic (α-spodumene, low-T form); Tetragonal (β-spodumene, high-T polymorph >900°C)
- **Mineral group:** Inosilicate, Pyroxene group (clinopyroxene subgroup)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 6.5–7
- **Specific gravity:** 3.03–3.23
- **Cleavage:** Perfect prismatic, two directions {110} ∧ {110} at 87° (near 90°, like true pyroxene)
- **Fracture:** Uneven to subconchoidal
- **Luster:** Vitreous, pearly on cleavage surfaces
- **Streak:** White
- **Tenacity:** Brittle

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Highly variable: colorless, white, gray, yellow, pink, lilac, violet, green, emerald-green; may be bicolored
- **Color causes:**
  - **Kunzite (pink→lilac→violet):** Mn³⁺ substituting for Al in the octahedral site. Low Fe/Mn ratio essential — Fe suppresses the pink color.
  - **Hiddenite (green→emerald-green):** Cr³⁺ (and/or V³⁺) substituting for Al. True hiddenite requires Cr; not all green spodumene is Cr-colored.
  - **Triphane (yellow):** Trace Fe or natural irradiation of colorless material.
  - **Colorless/white:** Pure spodumene without chromophores.
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent (gem varieties); opaque in massive material
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:**
  - **Strong pleochroism:** Kunzite — deep purple along c-axis, nearly colorless perpendicular; Hiddenite — deep green along c-axis, yellowish-green perpendicular. Visible to the unaided eye.
  - **Tenebrescence:** Kunzite can fade in strong sunlight (UV bleaches Mn³⁺ color centers) and regain color in the dark or under X-rays/irradiation. Hiddenite does not tenebresce.
  - **Chatoyancy:** Rare; cat's-eye kunzite from fine needle inclusions.
  - **Striations:** Dense vertical striations parallel to {100} on prism faces.
  - **Crystal faces:** Often etched and pitted with triangular markings.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Prismatic, elongated along [001] (c-axis), often flattened on {100}; commonly massive
- **Common forms/faces:** {100}, {110}, {010} — striated prisms with steep pyramidal or pinacoidal terminations
- **Twin laws:** Common on {100} — simple contact twins, sometimes repeated producing a fishtail or butterfly form
- **Varieties:**
  - **Kunzite:** Pink to lilac to violet, Mn-colored. Named 1902 after George Frederick Kunz (Tiffany's gemologist). Strong pleochroism, tenebrescent.
  - **Hiddenite:** Emerald-green to yellowish-green, Cr-colored. Named after A.E. Hidden (discovered 1879, North Carolina). Not all green spodumene is true hiddenite.
  - **Triphane:** Yellow to colorless spodumene. Historical term, less common in modern usage.
  - **Colorless/White spodumene:** The dominant industrial lithium ore form.
- **Special morphologies:**
  - **Giant crystals:** Spodumene forms some of the largest crystals known — up to 14.3 m (47 ft) long and 90 tons at the Etta Mine, Black Hills, South Dakota.
  - **Cleavable masses:** Industrial spodumene is typically massive, cleavable, and coarse-grained.

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 450–725°C (LCT pegmatite crystallization)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 550–650°C in zoned pegmatites
- **Decomposition temperature:** α→β polymorphic inversion at ~900°C (β-spodumene is the high-T tetragonal form, industrially produced by roasting)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - Higher temperatures (>600°C): larger, more euhedral prismatic crystals; core-zone spodumene can be meters long
  - Lower temperatures (450–550°C): smaller, more flattened crystals; wall-zone habit
  - Hydrothermal replacement (<400°C): spodumene alters to cookeite, eucryptite, or lepidolite

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Li (minimum ~100 ppm for nucleation; higher for growth)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - Mn (1–50 ppm) — essential for kunzite color; must be accompanied by LOW Fe
  - Cr (1–20 ppm) — essential for hiddenite color
  - Fe (trace to 1%) — common impurity; suppresses kunzite color if Fe/Mn > ~1:1
  - V (trace) — may contribute to green color in some hiddenite
  - Na, K (trace) — common substituents in late-stage hydrothermal alteration
  - Mg, Ca (trace) — minor substituents for Li
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Fe relative to Mn (prevents pink color); high Mg (favors other pyroxenes)
- **Required pH range:** Mildly acidic to neutral (pH 5.0–7.0) for primary crystallization
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing to mildly oxidizing (Mn exists as Mn²⁺ in melt but Mn³⁺ needed for color; Cr as Cr³⁺)
- **Required O₂ range:** Low to moderate (pegmatite-forming conditions)

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Spodumene crystallization from pegmatite melt releases SiO₂, Al₂O₃, and other components into residual fluid, enriching late-stage fluids in incompatible elements (Cs, Rb, Ta)
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Enrichment of residual fluid in fluxing elements (H₂O, F, B) enabling growth of large crystals
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Alteration to cookeite releases SiO₂ (as quartz) and Li into hydrothermal fluid; conversion to eucryptite + quartz releases Al

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate (faster than beryl in pegmatites, slower than quartz)
- **Maximum crystal size:** 14.3 m long (Etta Mine, South Dakota); gem crystals to 1 m (Afghanistan)
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 5–50 mm (pegmatite pocket crystals are well-formed but dwarfed by core-zone massive spodumene)
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — growth accelerates above 600°C but crystal quality decreases; optimal gem-quality growth occurs at 500–600°C in slowly cooled pockets
- **Competes with:** Petalite (LiAlSi₄O₁₀) for Li and Al in high-Si environments; lepidolite for late-stage Li; quartz and feldspar for space and Si/Al

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** α→β inversion at ~900°C (structure change from monoclinic to tetragonal); industrial roasting at 1075–1250°C for lithium extraction
- **Breaks down in light?** Kunzite: YES — tenebrescence causes fading in strong sunlight/UV; color recovers in darkness or by irradiation. This is NOT decomposition but a reversible color-center effect.
- **Dissolves in water?** Insoluble
- **Dissolves in acid?** Slowly attacked by hot concentrated acids; industrial extraction uses H₂SO₄ roasting
- **Oxidizes?** No — Li⁺, Al³⁺, Si⁴⁺ are all in their highest stable oxidation states
- **Dehydrates?** N/A — anhydrous; but alteration to cookeite (hydrous) in late hydrothermal stage effectively "hydrates" the mineral
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Kunzite color darkens under X-ray or gamma irradiation (reversible); long-term UV exposure fades color

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Early pegmatite crystallization of quartz, K-feldspar, albite, muscovite; spodumene is an intermediate-to-late phase in zoned pegmatites
- **Forms BEFORE:** Late hydrothermal minerals — lepidolite, cookeite, eucryptite, late phosphates, gem pocket tourmaline
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - **LCT pegmatites:** quartz, albite, K-feldspar, muscovite, lepidolite, beryl (especially morganite), tourmaline (elbaite), columbite-tantalite, petalite, eucryptite, amblygonite-montebrasite, cassiterite, topaz, fluorite
  - **Specific associations:**
    - Kunzite: often with pink tourmaline, morganite, cleavelandite (albite)
    - Hiddenite: with emerald, chromian dravite, quartz in alpine-type veins
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene (magmatic — LCT pegmatites, especially intermediate zones and cores)
- **Geological environment:**
  1. **LCT pegmatites:** Fractionated granitic pegmatites enriched in Li, Cs, Ta. Spodumene appears in intermediate zones and cores.
  2. **Alpine clefts:** Rare; hiddenite-type green spodumene in fractures associated with emeralds (North Carolina, Austria)
  3. **Aplitic dikes:** Fine-grained spodumene in Li-enriched aplite margins
  4. **Hydrothermal alteration zones:** Spodumene alters to cookeite + quartz + lepidolite in late-stage fluids

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1: Pala District, San Diego County, California, USA** — The birthplace of kunzite. Discovered in 1902; named after George Frederick Kunz. Produces magnificent pink to lilac kunzite crystals in gem pockets with tourmaline, morganite, and blue topaz. The Pala Chief Mine, Tourmaline Queen, and Stewart Mines are legendary. Crystals to 30+ cm. Bicolor kunzite (pink + green) found here.
- **Classic locality 2: Hiddenite, Alexander County, North Carolina, USA** — The type locality for hiddenite. Discovered 1879 by A.E. Hidden. Emerald-green Cr-spodumene in alpine-type veins with emerald, quartz, and chromian dravite. The North American Emerald Mine and Rist/Ellis Tracts still produce hiddenite today. Only true Cr-colored green spodumene from this district is considered "hiddenite."
- **Classic locality 3: Nuristan (Konar Valley, Paprok, Mawi) and Laghman Province, Afghanistan** — Currently the world's premier source of gem kunzite. Produces the finest, most saturated pink-to-violet crystals with exceptional transparency. Crystals to 1 m. Strong pleochroism and orange LW fluorescence. Dara-i-Pech region particularly noted.
- **Classic locality 4: Minas Gerais, Brazil** — Major source of kunzite, yellow spodumene, and some green material (note: Brazilian green spodumene is typically Fe-colored, NOT true Cr-hiddenite). Produces large, clean crystals.
- **Classic locality 5: Etta Mine, Keystone, Pennington County, South Dakota, USA** — Home to the largest spodumene crystal ever found: 14.3 m (47 ft) long, 90 tons. Industrial spodumene, not gem quality, but awe-inspiring in scale. The Black Hills are a historic lithium district.
- **Classic locality 6: Bikita, Zimbabwe / Tanco Mine, Bernic Lake, Manitoba, Canada / Greenbushes, Western Australia** — Major industrial spodumene deposits. Massive spodumene in giant LCT pegmatites. Tanco is famous for both petalite and spodumene.
- **Notable specimens:**
  - A 130 × 30 × 5 mm kunzite crystal from Dara-i-Pech, Afghanistan, showing dramatic pleochroism and orange LW fluorescence
  - 56.65-ct cut kunzite from Pala Chief Mine, California
  - The "Big Kahuna" — one of the largest kunzite crystals from Afghanistan, reportedly over 1 m long
  - 5.14-ct hiddenite from North Carolina — classic emerald-green color
  - Etta Mine "log" — 14.3-meter single crystal, one of the largest mineral crystals on Earth

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** YES — primarily kunzite (pink/lilac variety)
- **SW (255nm) color:** Weak to moderate creamy white to pale orange (Mn²⁺/Mn³⁺ emission)
- **MW (310nm) color:** Blue to bluish-white — attributed to oxygen defects in silicate groups and possibly [TiO₆]⁸⁻ centers (similar mechanism to benitoite/dumortierite)
- **LW (365nm) color:** Strong orange to salmon-pink — diagnostic for Mn-bearing kunzite. The Convoy S2+ 365nm lamp produces vivid orange fluorescence.
- **Phosphorescent?** No confirmed phosphorescence
- **Activator:** Mn³⁺ (orange LW); oxygen vacancies + [TiO₆]⁸⁻ (blue MW)
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺ (suppresses both fluorescence and color); high Fe/Mn ratio
- **Note:** Hiddenite (Cr-bearing) does NOT typically fluoresce under standard UV lamps. The Cr³⁺ produces R-line luminescence in spectroscopy but not visible UV fluorescence. Colorless/triphane spodumene is generally non-fluorescent.

### Flavor Text

> **The Giant That Hides Gems**
>
> Spodumene is a mineral of impossible scale. In the Black Hills of South Dakota, a single crystal grew to fourteen meters — longer than a city bus, heavier than a loaded truck. It was not beautiful. It was industrial spodumene, coarse and gray, mined for lithium. But it proves what this mineral can do when given a pocket large enough and time enough. Spodumene grows big because it grows last — after the feldspar and quartz have crystallized, after the melt has fractionated into something so enriched in lithium that nothing else can compete.
>
> And yet the same mineral, in a different pocket, produces kunzite: a crystal of such delicate pink that it seems borrowed from a sunset. Kunzite was discovered in 1902 in Pala, California, and named for George Frederick Kunz — the man who, more than anyone else, taught America to love gemstones. He recognized it immediately: a new jewel, pleochroic, glowing with internal fire, and already fading in the sunlight. For kunzite is tenebrescent. Leave it in the sun and it bleaches to palest lilac. Keep it in darkness and the color returns, as if the crystal breathes light in and out. It is the only major gemstone that asks you to put it away in a drawer.
>
> Hiddenite, the emerald-green variety, is even rarer. Named for the town in North Carolina where it was found, it requires chromium — the same element that colors emerald — in a lithium pyroxene structure that has no business holding Cr. True hiddenite is a geological accident: the right trace of chromium in a lithium-rich fluid, at the right moment, in a fracture in the earth. Most "green spodumene" is not hiddenite. It is Fe-colored, pale, unremarkable. True hiddenite, deep green, is one of the rarest gem varieties on Earth.
>
> In the vugg, spodumene would be a late-stage prize: large, prismatic, striated, appearing only after the pegmatite has fully fractionated. It would compete with lepidolite for lithium, with petalite for the same elements. And if the trace chemistry is just right — low Fe, just enough Mn — it might emerge not as gray industrial ore but as kunzite, pink and glowing, a crystal that fades in sunlight and returns in darkness, teaching the patience of the dark.

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Kunzite** — Pink to lilac prismatic crystals, strongly pleochroic. Forms when Mn > 1 ppm and Fe/Mn < 1. Tenebrescent: fades in light, recovers in darkness. Orange LW fluorescence. High gem value. Named for George Frederick Kunz.
- **Variant 2: Hiddenite** — Emerald-green prismatic crystals, Cr-colored. Strong green/colorless pleochroism. Does NOT tenebresce. Rare and valuable. True hiddenite requires Cr > 1 ppm. Named for A.E. Hidden and Hiddenite, NC.
- **Variant 3: Giant Spodumene** — Massive gray-white cleavable crystals, meters long. Industrial form, no gem value but impressive scale bonus. Forms in large vugs at high Li + low chromophores. Reference to Etta Mine crystal.

**Formation sequence in LCT pegmatites (after Černý, 1991; London, 2008):**
1. **Border zone:** Fine-grained muscovite + quartz + albite (wall rock contact)
2. **Wall zone:** Coarser muscovite + K-feldspar + quartz + albite + tourmaline
3. **Intermediate zone:** **beryl + columbite-tantalite + spodumene** + K-feldspar + quartz
4. **Core margin:** Massive quartz + spodumene + petalite (if high Si)
5. **Core:** Massive quartz + K-feldspar (spodumene may project into core)
6. **Late-stage vugs:** gem tourmaline + kunzite + hiddenite + lepidolite + cleavelandite
7. **Hydrothermal alteration:** spodumene → cookeite + quartz; or → eucryptite + quartz

**Key paragenetic relationship — Spodumene vs. Lepidolite:**
In the most fractionated LCT pegmatites, spodumene and lepidolite are mutually exclusive or sequential. Spodumene crystallizes at higher temperatures and is the primary Li sink in moderate-F pegmatites. Lepidolite replaces spodumene in late hydrothermal fluids or forms directly in highly evolved, F-rich systems. The transition spodumene → lepidolite represents the pegmatite's final surrender of lithium to the fluid phase.

**Key paragenetic relationship — Spodumene vs. Petalite:**
Petalite (LiAlSi₄O₁₀) is the high-Si alternative to spodumene. The stability boundary is pressure-dependent: at >300 MPa, petalite is favored over spodumene. The two rarely coexist; spodumene replaces petalite during cooling or decompression. This is analogous to the albite/K-feldspar relationship.

## Individual Flavor Texts for Variants

### Kunzite Flavor Text

> **The Stone That Breathes Light**
>
> Kunzite was born in a California gem pocket in 1902 and named for the man who recognized it: George Frederick Kunz, Tiffany's gemologist, the man who taught America that stones could be more than investment — they could be love. Kunzite is pink spodumene, colored by manganese, and it carries a secret no other major gemstone shares: it fades in sunlight. Leave a kunzite ring on a windowsill and in weeks it will bleach to a ghost of itself. Put it in a drawer and the color returns, slowly, like memory.
>
> Gemologists call this tenebrescence. I call it patience. Kunzite teaches that beauty is not constant — that some things must be kept in darkness to keep their color. The pleochroism is violent: look down the long axis of the crystal and it is deep violet; turn it sideways and it is nearly colorless. It is a mineral that changes depending on how you look at it, when you look at it, how much light you give it.
>
> The finest kunzite comes from Afghanistan now — crystals a meter long, transparent as glass, pink as the inside of a shell. But Pala, California, was first. And in the vugg, kunzite would be the reward for patience: a late-stage crystal, Mn-colored, appearing only after the Fe has been sequestered and the Li has concentrated. It would ask to be kept in shadow. And when the simulator's light events pass, it would fade — and wait for darkness to become itself again.

### Hiddenite Flavor Text

> **The Green That Shouldn't Exist**
>
> Hiddenite is the emerald-green variety of spodumene, and it should not exist. Spodumene is a lithium aluminum pyroxene; its structure has an octahedral site that prefers Al. Chromium — the element that colors emerald, that demands a beryl structure with its larger site — somehow wedges itself into spodumene's tighter lattice and turns it green. Not pale green. Emerald green. The color of forests in stone.
>
> It was found in 1879 in Alexander County, North Carolina, by a man named Hidden — A.E. Hidden, a mining agent who was looking for emeralds and found something stranger. True hiddenite requires chromium. Most green spodumene does not have Cr; it is Fe-colored, yellowish, weak. True hiddenite is rarer than emerald and far less known. The North American Emerald Mine still produces it, deep in the same fractures that hold emerald, quartz, and chromian tourmaline.
>
> Hiddenite does not fade. Unlike kunzite, it is stable in light, stable in time, stable in memory. Its pleochroism is equally strong — deep green along the c-axis, yellowish-green across it — but it does not ask to be hidden away. It simply is green, defiantly, in a mineral that has no right to be. In the vugg, hiddenite would be the rarest of the spodumene variants: a Cr-colored miracle, appearing only when chromium and lithium meet in the same fractionated fluid, at the same moment, in the same pocket. The odds are low. The color, when it happens, is unforgettable.

### Triphane / Colorless Spodumene Flavor Text

> **The Invisible Giant**
>
> Most spodumene is not kunzite and not hiddenite. It is colorless, white, or pale yellow — the mineral in its unadorned form, growing to sizes that make other crystals seem like dust. The Etta Mine crystal was fourteen meters of gray spodumene, ninety tons of lithium ore. No color. No gem value. Just scale.
>
> This is spodumene as industry: the source of half the world's lithium, roasted to β-spodumene, leached for the element that powers phones and electric cars. It is the mineral that makes the energy transition possible, buried in pegmatites in Australia, Zimbabwe, Canada, the Congo. The Greenbushes mine in Western Australia is the largest spodumene deposit on Earth — not beautiful, but essential.
>
> In the vugg, colorless spodumene would be the common form: prismatic, striated, perhaps a meter long if the vug is large enough. It would not glow, would not change color, would not ask for darkness. It would simply grow, patient and enormous, the backbone of the lithium paragenesis. And when the vug finally opens, the collector would see not a gem but a giant: a crystal that grew because it had space, time, and enough lithium to outlast everything else.

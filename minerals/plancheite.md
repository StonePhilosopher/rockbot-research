# Research: Plancheite — Cu₈(Si₄O₁₁)₂(OH)₄·H₂O

## Species: Plancheite

### Identity
- **Formula:** Cu₈(Si₄O₁₁)₂(OH)₄·H₂O (also written Cu₈Si₈O₂₂(OH)₄·H₂O)
- **Crystal system:** Orthorhombic
- **Mineral group:** Inosilicate (chain silicate) — amphibole-type double chains
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 5.5–6.0
- **Specific gravity:** 3.6–3.8
- **Cleavage:** None observed
- **Fracture:** Fibrous
- **Luster:** Vitreous to silky; adamantine on crystal faces

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Pale turquoise-blue, light blue, greenish-blue; rarely darker blue
- **Color causes:** Cu²⁺ in square planar and distorted octahedral coordination within the silicate framework — same chromophore as other copper silicates but with slightly different coordination environment producing the characteristic turquoise tone
- **Transparency:** Translucent to opaque
- **Streak:** Pale blue
- **Notable visual features:** Strong pleochroism — X = colorless or pale blue, Y = colorless or blue, Z = blue or dark blue. Fibrous aggregates have a silky luster. Individual tiny crystals may show adamantine luster. The almost turquoise-like color is distinctive among copper silicates.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Radial sprays and spherulites of acicular (needle-like) or fibrous crystals; fibers extended parallel to the c crystal axis (along the silicate chains)
- **Common forms/faces:** Individual crystals are rare; typically fibrous or acicular aggregates. Tiny tabular or platy crystals reported in some specimens.
- **Twin laws:** Not commonly reported
- **Varieties:** None formally recognized; color varies from pale turquoise to darker blue depending on locality and Cu²⁺ concentration
- **Special morphologies:**
  - Spherulites of radiating acicular crystals
  - Massive and mammillary (breast-like) aggregates
  - Fibrous crusts lining vug walls
  - Chatoyant crusts when viewed on edge (parallel crystals growing perpendicular to matrix)

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 20–100°C (supergene, low-temperature)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 40–80°C
- **Decomposition temperature:** Not applicable at Earth-surface conditions; stable to ~250°C
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Higher temperatures favor larger, more compact spherulitic masses; lower temperatures favor delicate fibrous sprays and crusts

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:**
  - Cu: >45 ppm (oxidized to Cu²⁺)
  - SiO₂: >25 ppm (requires alkaline pH for silica mobilization, same as other copper silicates)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - Ca: may promote silicate mobilization and buffer pH
  - Al: traces may substitute into structure
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - High Fe: competes for Si, favors iron oxides
  - Strongly acidic pH (<6.5): suppresses silica solubility
  - Very high carbonate: may favor malachite/azurite instead
- **Required pH range:** 7.5–9.5 (alkaline to mildly alkaline — similar to shattuckite, slightly broader than dioptase)
- **Required Eh range:** Oxidizing (+0.4 to +0.7 V)
- **Required O₂ range:** Moderate to high

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No significant byproducts
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Consumes Cu²⁺ and SiO₂ from solution
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Dissolves in acid to release Cu²⁺ (toxic)

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate — comparable to shattuckite, faster than dioptase, slower than chrysocolla
- **Maximum crystal size:** Individual crystals rarely exceed 3–5 mm; fibrous aggregates and crusts up to several cm
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–3 mm acicular crystals in radial sprays; crusts 1–5 mm thick
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — growth rate increases with temperature up to ~80°C
- **Competes with:** Shattuckite (very close competitor — same elements, similar conditions), dioptase (for Cu and Si at higher pH), chrysocolla (for Cu and Si across broader pH), malachite (for Cu)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Stable to ~250°C; gradual decomposition above
- **Breaks down in light?** No — color stable
- **Dissolves in water?** Very slightly; more soluble in acidic water
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — dissolves in HCl and H₂SO₄, releasing Cu²⁺
- **Oxidizes?** No — Cu already in +2 state
- **Dehydrates?** The structural H₂O is loosely bound; dehydration begins above ~200°C
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Not notably radiation-sensitive

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Primary copper sulfides oxidize to release Cu²⁺; silica becomes mobilized in alkaline conditions
- **Forms BEFORE:** May be coated by later chrysocolla, malachite, or dioptase; may alter to chrysocolla in late-stage low-Si fluids
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Chrysocolla, dioptase, malachite, conichalcite, tenorite, shattuckite, quartz, cerussite, limonite, brochantite, "bisbeeite" (a chrysocolla variety)
- **Zone:** Supergene/oxidation zone — silica-rich microenvironments within the oxidation zone
- **Geological environment:** Copper deposits in arid/semi-arid regions where silica is mobilized by alkaline groundwater. Type locality (Mindouli, Republic of Congo) and African copperbelt localities are classic.

### Famous Localities
- **Sanda Mine, Mindouli, Pool Region, Republic of Congo:** Type locality (1908). Named after J. Planche who brought specimens from Africa. Blue, chatoyant crusts of plancheite lining cavities.
- **Kimbedi and Mbumba, Mindouli District, Pool Region, Republic of Congo:** Significant locality with fine radial sprays and chatoyant crusts. Classic African copperbelt material.
- **Tantara, Kambowe, and M'Sesa, Katanga (Shaba) Province, DR Congo:** Fine material from the Congo copperbelt. Often associated with dioptase, malachite, and chrysocolla.
- **Mashamba West Mine, Kolwezi, Katanga, DR Congo:** Open vugs lined with individual plancheite crystals to 3 mm — exceptional for this usually fibrous mineral.
- **Tsumeb Mine, Namibia:** Occurs on and below 30 Level, associated with dioptase. The chemically related mineral plancheite appears in the same oxidation zone as dioptase at this world-famous locality.
- **Milpillas Mine, Sonora, Mexico:** Important recent locality. Plancheite occurs with primary malachite (unusual — suggests it can form in slightly different conditions than typical supergene). Also noted in Mineralogical Record 39-6 (2008).
- **Table Mountain Mine, Arizona, USA:** Significant US locality.
- **Driggith Mine, Caldbeck Fells, Cumbria, England:** Rare European occurrence.
- **Rioja, Argentina:** Noted in Evans & Mrose 1977 crystal chemistry study.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** No — non-fluorescent
- **SW (255nm) color:** None
- **MW (310nm) color:** None
- **LW (365nm) color:** None
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** N/A
- **Quenched by:** N/A

### Flavor Text
> In the oxidation zone, where copper weathers free of its sulfide bonds and meets silica in alkaline water, four copper silicates compete for the same atoms. Dioptase claims the emerald green. Shattuckite takes the azure blue. Chrysocolla accepts whatever is left, amorphous and forgiving. And plancheite — pale turquoise, almost forgettable beside its vivid cousins — holds a secret in its chains. Where shattuckite built pyroxene-like single chains and dioptase closed hers into rings, plancheite grew amphibole-like double chains: the same architecture that carries tremolite through mountain ranges. A mineral of the weathered surface, wearing the bones of the deep earth. Named for the man who carried it out of Africa, it remains rare enough that most collectors have only seen it in photographs — radial sprays of turquoise needles, or chatoyant crusts that flash like cat's-eye when turned to the light.

- **Variant 2: Tsumeb Spray** — Delicate radial sprays of acicular crystals, associated with dioptase; requires stable, slow-growing conditions and slightly higher silica; the "prize" variant for collectors

## Paragenetic Note: Plancheite in the Copper Silicate Family

Plancheite is the fourth member of the copper silicate subgroup, alongside chrysocolla, shattuckite, and dioptase. It shares the same fundamental requirements (oxidized Cu + mobilized silica + alkaline pH) but occupies a distinct structural and chemical niche:

| Parameter | Chrysocolla | Shattuckite | Plancheite | Dioptase |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|------------|----------|
| Formula | Cu₂₋ₓAlₓ(H₂₋ₓSi₂O₅)(OH)₄·nH₂O | Cu₅(SiO₃)₄(OH)₂ | Cu₈(Si₄O₁₁)₂(OH)₄·H₂O | Cu₆Si₆O₁₈·6H₂O |
| Cu:Si ratio | Variable (~1:1) | 5:4 (1.25:1) | 1:1 | 1:1 |
| Crystal system | Amorphous/orthorhombic | Orthorhombic | Orthorhombic | Trigonal |
| Structure type | Amorphous/phyllo | Inosilicate (pyroxene chains) | Inosilicate (amphibole double chains) | Cyclosilicate (rings) |
| Color | Blue-green/cyan | Azure blue | Turquoise-blue | Emerald green |
| pH preference | 6.5–9.5 (broad) | 7.5–9.5 | 7.5–9.5 | 8.5–10.5 (strict) |
| Hardness | 2.5–3.5 | 3.5 | 5.5–6.0 | 5 |
| Habit | Botryoidal/crust | Botryoidal/fibrous | Radial/fibrous spray | Rhombohedral prism |

**Key structural insight (Evans & Mrose 1977):** Shattuckite contains silicate chains similar to pyroxene (single chains, SiO₃), while plancheite contains silicate chains similar to amphibole (double chains, Si₄O₁₁). This is the same structural progression found in the major rock-forming silicates: pyroxene → amphibole → mica. Plancheite has adopted the double-chain architecture of the deep-earth minerals, even though it forms at the surface.

**Paragenetic sequence within the copper silicate subgroup:**

```
Primary sulfide oxidation
        │
        ▼
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  pH 6.5–7.5      pH 7.5–8.5        pH 8.5–9.5      pH 8.5–10.5 │
  │     │                 │                  │              │       │
  │     ▼                 ▼                  ▼              ▼       │
  │  Chrysocolla      Shattuckite      Plancheite       Dioptase   │
  │  (amorphous)      (pyroxene        (amphibole       (cyclo-    │
  │  ├─ broad pH        chains)          double           silicate) │
  │  ├─ low Si          ├─ mod. pH       chains)         ├─ strict  │
  │  └─ most common     ├─ mod. Si       ├─ mod. pH       pH        │
  │                     └─ moderate      ├─ 1:1 Cu:Si     ├─ high Si│
  │                       hardness       └─ hardest       └─ gemmy  │
  │                                      (5.5–6)          (H=5)    │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Plancheite's position is between shattuckite and dioptase in terms of pH, but its 1:1 Cu:Si ratio means it competes directly with dioptase for stoichiometry while sharing shattuckite's pH tolerance. Its higher hardness (5.5–6) is unique among copper silicates — the amphibole-like double chains create a more robust structure than the pyroxene chains of shattuckite or the rings of dioptase.

**Fluid evolution path:**
```
Early oxidation (acidic, high CO₂):
  Azurite → Malachite
        │
        ▼
Carbonate buffered, silica mobilized (mildly alkaline, pH 7.5–8.5):
  Shattuckite nucleates first (broadest pH tolerance among crystalline Cu-silicates)
        │
        ▼
Silica increases, pH stabilizes (pH 8.0–9.5):
  Plancheite nucleates (1:1 Cu:Si, amphibole chains)
        │
        ▼
Strongly alkaline, high silica (pH 8.5–10.5):
  Dioptase nucleates (cyclosilicate rings, requires most silica)
        │
        ▼
Late-stage silica flooding or pH drop:
  Chrysocolla (amorphous, fills remaining porosity)
  OR pseudomorph replacement of earlier minerals
```

In the simulator, if any copper silicate nucleates, all others in the subgroup should have elevated nucleation probability (same microenvironment). The presence of plancheite specifically suggests moderate pH, moderate silica, and relatively stable conditions — it's less demanding than dioptase but more crystalline and structured than chrysocolla.

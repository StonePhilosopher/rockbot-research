## Species: Morganite

### Identity
- **Formula:** Be₃Al₂Si₆O₁₈ with Mn²⁺ (and sometimes Cs⁺) in structural channels and octahedral sites
- **Crystal system:** Hexagonal (6/mmm)
- **Mineral group:** Cyclosilicate — color variety of beryl
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 7.5–8.0
- **Specific gravity:** 2.71–2.90 (slightly higher than aquamarine due to Mn + Cs content)
- **Cleavage:** Imperfect on {0001}
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal to irregular
- **Luster:** Vitreous to resinous; pearly on basal faces
- **Streak:** White
- **Optical:** Uniaxial (−); nω ≈ 1.58–1.60, nε ≈ 1.572–1.592; birefringence ~0.008–0.010 (slightly higher than other beryl varieties)

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Soft pink to peach, salmon, rose; rarely violet-pink ("rose morganite")
- **Color causes:**
  - **Primary chromophore:** Mn²⁺ in octahedral sites (substituting for Al³⁺) and/or in channel sites
  - **Mn²⁺ alone:** very pale pink (Mn²⁺ d-d transitions are Laporte-forbidden, inherently weak)
  - **Mn²⁺ + natural irradiation:** Deepens pink. Natural irradiation from host granite (⁴⁰K, U, Th decay) oxidizes some Mn²⁺ → Mn³⁺, intensifying color. This is why morganite from Madagascar (high natural radiation) often shows deeper peach-pink than Brazilian material.
  - **Heat treatment:** Most commercial morganite is heat-treated at 400–500°C to remove yellow/orange component and stabilize uniform pink. Heat treatment converts any Mn³⁺ back to Mn²⁺ and may affect Fe/Mn charge transfer bands.
  - **Cs content:** Morganite from highly evolved pegmatites can contain 1–3% Cs₂O. Cesium contributes to the "warm" tone and slightly elevates refractive index and specific gravity. Cs-rich morganite tends toward peach rather than pure pink.
  - **Fe interference:** Even trace Fe can shift morganite toward peach or salmon (Mn²⁺ pink + Fe³⁺ yellow = peach). Low-Fe morganite is purer pink.
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent; gem-quality is exceptionally clear
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Hexagonal prisms with flat basal pinacoid terminations. Often cleaner and clearer than aquamarine from same deposit. Stronger pleochroism than aquamarine: deep pink/colorless to pale peach. Can show cat's-eye chatoyancy (rare, from fine needle inclusions).

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Long prismatic hexagonal crystals, striated parallel to c-axis. Often more slender and elongated than aquamarine.
- **Common forms/faces:** {1010} prism, {0001} basal pinacoid
- **Twin laws:** Rare on {0001} or {1010}
- **Varieties:**
  - **Pink morganite:** Classic rose-pink, low Fe, moderate Mn. Most common.
  - **Peach/salmon morganite:** Higher Fe content, Mn²⁺ + Fe³⁺ charge transfer produces warm orange-pink. Madagascar material often shows this.
  - **"Rose" morganite:** Deeper, more saturated pink. Usually heat-treated or from high-radiation environment.
  - **Cat's-eye morganite:** Rare; fine parallel needle inclusions produce chatoyancy.
- **Special morphologies:** Etched crystals in pocket environments. Some crystals reach 30+ cm in highly evolved pegmatites. Often associated with pink tourmaline (rubellite) in same pockets.

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 350–650°C (pegmatite, especially late-stage)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 400–550°C for gem-quality morganite in miarolitic cavities
- **Decomposition temperature:** >800°C (beryl melts incongruently)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - Higher T (500–650°C): shorter, slightly more opaque; common in intermediate zones
  - Lower T (350–500°C): longer, clearer, more gemmy; miarolitic cavities
  - Morganite is generally a late-stage mineral, appearing after early quartz and feldspar

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:**
  - **Be:** ~10+ ppm (beryllium essential)
  - **Al:** significant (for beryl structure)
  - **Si:** abundant
  - **Mn²⁺:** ~200–1000+ ppm (essential for pink color; higher = deeper pink)
  - **Cs (optional):** 0–3% Cs₂O — not required for color but common in evolved pegmatites
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - **Fe:** trace only; suppresses pure pink, shifts toward peach/salmon. Low Fe/Mn ratio essential for pure pink.
  - **Na, Li, K:** in channel sites; common in late-stage pegmatite fluids
  - **Rb:** often accompanies Cs in evolved pegmatites
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - **High Fe (>200 ppm):** shifts color toward peach, suppresses pure pink
  - **High Cr or V:** would produce emerald green, not pink
  - **High Fe²⁺ in channels:** would produce aquamarine blue
  - **Very low Mn:** colorless goshenite forms instead
- **Required pH range:** 5–7 (slightly acidic to neutral)
- **Required Eh range:** Mildly oxidizing to neutral (Mn²⁺ must be stable; some natural oxidation to Mn³⁺ by irradiation deepens color but too much oxidation destroys the beryl structure)
- **Required O₂ range:** Low to moderate

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release chemicals when forming?** No significant byproducts
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Consumes Be, Al, Si, Mn from fluid. Late-stage crystallization enriches residual fluid in even more incompatible elements (Cs, Rb, Ta)
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** >800°C → chrysoberyl + quartz

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Slow (0.3× quartz baseline)
- **Maximum crystal size:** 30+ cm in highly evolved pegmatites; gem crystals to 10+ cm common
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 2–15 cm for gem pockets
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — slower at lower T but clearer and more gemmy
- **Competes with:** Rubellite (pink tourmaline) for Mn in late-stage pegmatites. Morganite and rubellite often coexist in same pockets but use Mn in different structures.

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes, >800°C
- **Breaks down in light?** No — morganite is stable in light (unlike realgar or maxixe-type beryl). However, prolonged strong UV may very slightly affect Mn color centers over decades.
- **Dissolves in water?** Insoluble
- **Dissolves in acid?** Insoluble
- **Oxidizes?** No — Mn²⁺ is stable in beryl structure under normal conditions
- **Dehydrates?** No water in structure
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Natural irradiation from host granite DEEPENS pink color (Mn²⁺ → Mn³⁺ oxidation). This is a beneficial effect, not damage. Artificial irradiation can also deepen color but may produce unstable color centers.

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Early pegmatite minerals — quartz, K-feldspar, albite, muscovite, early schorl
- **Forms BEFORE:** Very late-stage — lepidolite, cookeite, pollucite, petalite, late phosphates
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - **Quartz, K-feldspar, albite, muscovite** — host pegmatite minerals
  - **Rubellite (pink tourmaline)** — the classic morganite companion. Both are Mn-colored, both form in late pegmatite pockets. The pocket that produces one often produces the other.
  - **Cleavelandite (albite)** — platy, late-stage albite often encrusts morganite crystals
  - **Lepidolite** — lithium mica in same late-stage environment
  - **Spodumene (kunzite)** — in LCT pegmatites, kunzite and morganite can coexist
  - **Topaz, garnet** — occasional associates
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene (late pegmatite, miarolitic cavities)
- **Geological environment:**
  - **Highly evolved LCT or NYF pegmatites** — fractionated to the point where Mn, Cs, Li, and other incompatible elements are concentrated
  - **Miarolitic cavities** — gem-quality morganite requires open cavities for slow, unimpeded growth
  - **Pegmatite core margins** — where late-stage fluid has accumulated incompatible elements

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1:** **Pala, San Diego County, California, USA** — The original source of gem morganite. Discovered in 1902 by George Frederick Kunz (same man who identified kunzite). Named after J.P. Morgan, the financier and gem collector, by Kunz himself. Pala Chief Mine, Tourmaline Queen Mine, and Stewart Lithia Mine produced magnificent pink crystals to 30+ cm, often associated with rubellite and blue topaz.
- **Classic locality 2:** **Madagascar (Manjaka, Sahatany Valley, and other localities)** — Currently one of the premier sources. Madagascar morganite tends toward peach and salmon due to higher Fe content and natural irradiation effects. Also produces "rose morganite" — deeper, more saturated pink. Associated with spectacular liddicoatite (triangular color-zoned tourmaline).
- **Classic locality 3:** **Minas Gerais, Brazil** — Major source of clean pink morganite, often lower in Fe than Madagascar material, producing purer pink. Associated with aquamarine and helidor in same pegmatite districts. The Golconda mine and Urucum mine are notable.
- **Notable specimens:**
  - **The "Maltese Cross":** A 584 ct rough crystal from Madagascar, named for its cross-shaped twin formation. Extraordinary clarity.
  - **Pala Chief Mine specimens:** Crystals to 30 cm, transparent pink, associated with rubellite, blue topaz, and cleavelandite. Collector's classics.
  - **Morganite in jewelry:** The "Moon of Baroda" is a famous pink diamond, but morganite has become the premier pink gem for engagement rings since ~2010 — a more affordable, ethical alternative to pink diamond.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Weakly — some specimens show faint violet or pinkish glow
- **SW (255nm):** Very weak pink to violet (Mn²⁺ emission, but Fe suppresses)
- **MW (310nm):** Weak violet to pinkish
- **LW (365nm):** Weak violet — the most common fluorescence color for morganite. Mn²⁺ in beryl structure can produce weak violet LW fluorescence, but Fe content usually quenches it.
- **Phosphorescent?** No confirmed phosphorescence
- **Activator:** Mn²⁺ (weak violet/pink)
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺ (common in morganite, suppresses fluorescence). Low-Fe morganite may show stronger fluorescence.
- **Note:** Morganite fluorescence is weak and unreliable as an identification tool. Unlike kunzite (strong orange), morganite does not glow dramatically.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:**
  - **Be, Al, Si:** Same blockers as base beryl
  - **Mn oxidation state / availability:** Critical for morganite. Need Mn > 200 ppm and Fe/Mn ratio < 1.0 for pure pink.
  - **Fe/Mn ratio tracking:** Essential for color determination. High Fe → peach; low Fe → pink.
  - **Cs tracking (optional):** Could affect "warmth" of color — higher Cs → peachier tone.
  - **Natural irradiation event:** Could deepen color in some environments (Madagascar-type)
  - **Heat treatment event:** `event_morganite_heat_treat` — removes yellow component, stabilizes uniform pink
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF trace_Be > 10 ppm
AND trace_Al > 1000 ppm
AND SiO2 > 600
AND temperature BETWEEN 350 AND 650
AND pH BETWEEN 5 AND 7
AND (pegmatite_fractionation_event OR miarolitic_cavity_event)
AND Mn > 200 ppm
AND Fe/Mn < 1.0  /* low iron essential for pink */
→ nucleate morganite (max 1 per pocket)
  IF Fe/Mn < 0.2 AND Mn > 500
    → variant = "Rose Morganite" (deep pure pink)
  ELSE IF Fe/Mn < 0.5
    → variant = "Pink Morganite" (standard pink)
  ELSE IF Fe/Mn > 0.5 AND Fe > 100
    → variant = "Peach Morganite" (warm salmon)
  IF Cs > 1000 ppm
    → warmth_bonus = "Cesium warmth" (slight peach shift)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF morganite exists
AND trace_Be > 5 ppm
AND trace_Al > 500 ppm
AND SiO2 > 400
AND temperature BETWEEN 350 AND 650
AND NOT (temperature > 800)
→ grow at rate = SLOW (0.3× quartz baseline)
  color_intensity += Mn_ppm / 1000 * (1 - Fe/Mn)
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - T 350–450°C + slow growth → long slender prismatic, gemmy
  - T 450–550°C → shorter, more robust prisms
  - Miarolitic cavity → exceptional clarity, flat basal termination
  - Late-stage pocket → often etched or modified crystal faces
- **Decomposition products:** Same as beryl: >800°C → chrysoberyl + quartz

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Pink Morganite** — Standard rose-pink. Mn > 200 ppm, Fe/Mn < 0.5. Clear, hexagonal, gemmy. Classic late-pegmatite prize.
- **Variant 2: Rose Morganite** — Deep, saturated pure pink. Mn > 500 ppm, Fe/Mn < 0.2. Rare. Premium gem value. Requires highly evolved, Fe-depleted fluid.
- **Variant 3: Peach / Salmon Morganite** — Warm peach-orange-pink. Mn present but Fe/Mn > 0.5. Common in Madagascar-type deposits. Lower per-carat value but distinctive color.

## Linked Paragenetic Sequence

Morganite belongs to the **beryl family paragenetic sequence** within pegmatites. See `memory/research-beryl.md` for the full sequence.

**Specific to morganite:**

1. **Iron sequestration phase:** Early pegmatite crystallization of biotite, schorl, magnetite, etc., removes Fe from fluid
2. **Manganese concentration phase:** As crystallization proceeds, incompatible Mn is enriched in residual fluid
3. **Morganite nucleation:** When Mn reaches 200+ ppm and Fe is depleted (Fe/Mn < 1), morganite becomes possible
4. **Rubellite competition:** In same fluid, Mn may also nucleate rubellite (pink tourmaline). Both can coexist.
5. **Very late stage:** Cs, Rb, Ta enrichment → pollucite, lepidolite, cookeite

**Color zoning in natural morganite:** Some crystals show color gradients from peach (core, higher Fe) to pink (rim, lower Fe), documenting changing fluid chemistry during growth. This could be implemented in the simulator as a "growth ring" mechanic.

## Research Notes

### Kunz and Morgan: A Naming Story
George Frederick Kunz (1856–1932) was Tiffany & Co.'s chief gemologist and the most influential American gemologist of his era. He:
- Discovered/named kunzite (1902)
- Identified morganite (1910, named for J.P. Morgan)
- Wrote "Gems and Precious Stones of North America" (1890)
- Was the first to systematically document American gem deposits

Kunz had a genius for naming — he understood that a gemstone needs a story, not just a chemical formula. "Morganite" gave pink beryl the prestige of finance; "kunzite" gave it the prestige of science. Both names stuck.

### Heat Treatment in Trade
Most commercial morganite is heat-treated at 400–500°C to:
- Remove yellow/orange component (from Fe)
- Stabilize uniform pink color
- Improve clarity by reducing color zoning

Heat treatment is permanent, stable, and accepted by the trade. It is undetectable without advanced spectroscopy. Natural-color deep pink morganite commands a premium.

In the simulator: heat treatment could be an optional post-formation event.

### Cesium-Rich Morganite
Some pegmatite morganite contains 1–3% Cs₂O. This is diagnostic of highly evolved pegmatites (the " pollucite zone" level of fractionation). Cs:
- Increases specific gravity and refractive index slightly
- May contribute to "warm" peach tone
- Is a marker for extreme fractionation — the fluid that made this morganite was among the last to crystallize in the pegmatite

Cs tracking is optional for simulator implementation but adds geological realism.

## Comparison: Morganite vs. Kunzite (Pink Spodumene)

Both are pink gemstones from LCT pegmatites. Both are Mn-colored. Both were named by George Frederick Kunz. But they differ critically:

| Feature | Morganite | Kunzite |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Structure | Beryl (hexagonal, rings) | Pyroxene (monoclinic, chains) |
| Mn site | Octahedral (Al replacement) + channels | Octahedral (Al replacement) |
| Hardness | 7.5–8 | 6.5–7 |
| Pleochroism | Weak (pink/colorless) | Strong (violet/colorless) |
| Tenebrescence | No | **Yes** — fades in light |
| Fluorescence | Weak violet LW | Strong orange LW |
| Stability | Stable | Fades in sunlight |
| Crystal habit | Hexagonal prism | Monoclinic prism, striated |
| Fe sensitivity | High (shifts to peach) | High (suppresses pink) |

In the simulator: finding a pink crystal in a late-stage pegmatite pocket could be EITHER morganite or kunzite (or rubellite tourmaline). The player would need to examine properties to distinguish them — a perfect identification puzzle.

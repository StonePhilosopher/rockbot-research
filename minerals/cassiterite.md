## Species: Cassiterite

### Identity
- **Formula:** SnO₂
- **Crystal system:** Tetragonal
- **Mineral group:** Oxide (Rutile group)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 6–7
- **Specific gravity:** 6.98–7.1 (very heavy — nearly twice galena's density)
- **Cleavage:** {100} imperfect, {110} indistinct
- **Fracture:** Subconchoidal to uneven
- **Luster:** Adamantine to adamantine-metallic, splendent; greasy on fracture surfaces
- **Streak:** White to brownish
- **Tenacity:** Brittle

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Black, brownish black, reddish brown, brown, red, yellow, gray, white; rarely colorless
- **Color causes:** Trace Fe³⁺, Nb⁵⁺, Ta⁵⁺ substitutions into the Sn⁴⁺ site; natural radiation; structural defects
- **Transparency:** Transparent in thin crystals and light-colored material; opaque in dark, massive specimens
- **Streak:** White to pale brown
- **Notable visual features:** High adamantine luster produces exceptional fire and brilliance when faceted as a gemstone. Crystals often show concentric color zoning. "Wood tin" variety has a botryoidal/reniform habit with concentric banding resembling tree rings. Twinning produces characteristic "elbow" or "knee" twins bent at ~60°.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Short to long prismatic [001] with {110} and {100} dominant; terminated by steep pyramidal forms {111}, {101}
- **Common forms/faces:** {110}, {100}, {111}, {101} — dipyramidal tetragonal crystals are classic
- **Twin laws:** Very common on {011} — contact twins, penetration twins, and geniculated ("elbow") twins. The elbow twin is diagnostic: two crystals joined at ~60° resembling a bent knee.
- **Varieties:**
  - **Wood tin:** Botryoidal/reniform masses with concentric fibrous banding (looks like petrified wood cross-sections)
  - **Stream tin:** Water-worn pebbles and grains from placer deposits
  - **Needle tin:** Acicular crystals, rare
- **Special morphologies:** Coarse to fine granular, massive, radially fibrous, concretionary

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 400–700°C (pegmatitic); 300–500°C (hydrothermal veins); 200–400°C (greisen)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 450–600°C for primary magmatic-hydrothermal crystals
- **Decomposition temperature:** None under normal geological conditions — cassiterite is extraordinarily stable
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - High-T (>500°C): prismatic, well-developed pyramidal terminations, larger crystals
  - Medium-T (300–500°C): stubbier, more equant forms
  - Low-T (<300°C): fine-grained, often colloform/botryoidal (wood tin)

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Sn (minimum ~50 ppm for nucleation; higher for significant growth)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - Fe (10–1000 ppm) — common substituent, darkens color
  - Nb, Ta (trace to 1%) — coupled substitution with Fe: 2(Nb,Ta)⁵⁺ + Fe²⁺ ↔ 3Sn⁴⁺
  - Ti (trace to 2%) — particularly in hydrothermal vein cassiterite
  - W, Mn, Sc, In (trace) — various substitution mechanisms
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Ca, Mg (suppresses Sn mobility in favor of carbonates/silicates)
- **Required pH range:** Acidic to mildly acidic (pH 2–5 optimal for hydrothermal transport)
- **Required Eh range:** Oxidizing (Sn²⁺ → Sn⁴⁺ required for precipitation as oxide)
- **Required O₂ range:** High — cassiterite is the fully oxidized form of tin

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No direct releases; but cassiterite precipitation from hydrothermal fluids typically follows acid alteration of wall rocks (greisenization), consuming feldspars and releasing SiO₂, K⁺, Na⁺ into fluid.
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Acidic fluid (pH drops) due to hydrolysis reactions during wall-rock alteration
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Extremely resistant to chemical weathering; essentially no dissolution products in normal geological conditions. Mechanical breakdown only.

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Slow to moderate (slower than quartz, faster than wolframite)
- **Maximum crystal size:** Up to 10 cm individual crystals; massive aggregates to meters
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–20 mm (vug crystals are usually well-formed; pegmatite vugs host the largest)
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — higher temperatures promote faster growth and larger crystals; retrograde cooling in greisens causes episodic growth with color/concentration zoning
- **Competes with:** Stannite (Cu₂FeSnS₄) for Sn in sulfide-rich systems; wolframite for space in greisen veins; topaz/fluorite/tourmaline for vug space in pegmatites

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** No — melts at 1630°C (far above any geological temperature)
- **Breaks down in light?** No
- **Dissolves in water?** Essentially insoluble (solubility << 1 mg/L)
- **Dissolves in acid?** Resistant to most acids; slowly attacked by hot concentrated HCl or H₂SO₄
- **Oxidizes?** Already fully oxidized (Sn⁴⁺); no further oxidation possible
- **Dehydrates?** N/A — anhydrous
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Transparent/light-colored varieties may darken under prolonged UV or radiation exposure due to defect center formation; dark varieties unaffected

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Granite solidification and fractionation (Sn is incompatible and concentrates in late melts/fluids); early formed minerals in greisen include muscovite, topaz, fluorite, tourmaline
- **Forms BEFORE:** Late sulfides (stannite, chalcopyrite, sphalerite, galena in lower-temperature veins); supergene alteration products (none for cassiterite — it's the terminal weathering product)
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - **Pegmatite:** beryl, columbite-tantalite, topaz, fluorite, apatite, muscovite, lepidolite, tourmaline
  - **Greisen:** wolframite, molybdenite, fluorite, topaz, muscovite, lepidolite, arsenopyrite
  - **Hydrothermal veins:** quartz, wolframite, arsenopyrite, chalcopyrite, sphalerite, galena, stannite
  - **Placer:** quartz, tourmaline, magnetite, garnet, gold (rarely)
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene (magmatic-hydrothermal, greisen, pegmatite); supergene (placer concentrations)
- **Geological environment:**
  1. **Granite pegmatites:** Late-stage magmatic differentiates, LCT-type (lithium-cesium-tantalum) family
  2. **Greisen deposits:** Hydrothermally altered granite margins, quartz-topaz-muscovite-Sn assemblage
  3. **Hydrothermal veins:** High-temperature quartz-cassiterite ± wolframite veins in or near granites
  4. **Skarns:** Contact metasomatic deposits in carbonate wall rocks
  5. **Placer deposits:** Secondary concentrations in river gravels and beach sands (cassiterite's density and hardness make it ideal placer mineral)

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1: Viloco Mine (Araca), La Paz Department, Bolivia** — The world's finest cassiterite crystals. Gemmy, transparent honey-brown to black prismatic crystals to 10 cm, often with perfect pyramidal terminations and elbow twins. Found in hydrothermal veins in granite. Famous since the 19th century.
- **Classic locality 2: Cornwall and Devon, England** — Historic tin district, mined for ~4000 years. Greisen and vein deposits. The "Streaming" method (hydraulic mining) was developed here. Crystals typically smaller, often in quartz veins with tourmaline and wolframite.
- **Classic locality 3: Xuebaoding, Pingwu, Sichuan, China** — Exceptional gem-quality cassiterite crystals in alpine cleft-type veins in granite. Transparent to translucent golden-brown to colorless crystals, often associated with muscovite, topaz, and fluorite. Recent production of world-class specimens.
- **Notable specimens:**
  - A 1128g specimen from Xuebaoding (100 × 95 mm) surrounded by muscovite is one of the finest known
  - Bolivian crystals routinely exceed 5 cm and are prized by collectors
  - "Wood tin" from Durango, Mexico, and Cornwall shows spectacular concentric banding

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally no — cassiterite is not known for fluorescence
- **SW (255nm) color:** None
- **MW (310nm) color:** None
- **LW (365nm) color:** None
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** N/A
- **Quenched by:** N/A
- **Note:** Some Fe-rich or Nb/Ta-bearing cassiterites show weak cathodoluminescence (CL) in SEM — yellow to orange emission bands related to trace element substitutions. This is not observable under standard UV lamps.

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Prismatic Crystal** — Classic tetragonal dipyramid, transparent to translucent honey-brown. Forms at 450–600°C in vugs and veins. High luster, often twinned. Most collectible form.
- **Variant 2: Wood Tin** — Botryoidal/reniform masses with concentric fibrous banding. Forms at lower temperatures (<300°C) or in colloform deposits. Opaque, brown to black, resembles petrified wood.

4. Late sulfide: stannite + chalcopyrite + sphalerite + galena (200–350°C)
5. Supergene: placer concentration of cassiterite (mechanical only — no chemical change)

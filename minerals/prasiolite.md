## Identity

- **Formula:** SiO₂ (quartz) with interstitial Fe²⁺ color centers
- **Crystal system:** Trigonal (α-quartz, space group P3₂21 or P3₁21)
- **Mineral group:** Tectosilicate / oxide (quartz group)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 7
- **Specific gravity:** 2.65
- **Cleavage:** None
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal
- **Luster:** Vitreous

### Color & Appearance

- **Typical color:** Leek-green to grayish-green, pale mint-green
- **Color causes:** Fe²⁺–Fe³⁺ intervalence charge transfer (IVCT) centered at ~725 nm (13,660 cm⁻¹). Fe²⁺ occupies interstitial sites in the quartz channels (not substituting for Si⁴⁺ in the tetrahedral framework). Concentration: <20 ppm Fe typically sufficient.
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent
- **Streak:** White (none)
- **Notable visual features:** Color often pale and easily confused with prase (chlorite-included quartz) or chrysoprase (Ni-bearing chalcedony). True prasiolite is single-phase quartz with no inclusions causing the color.

### Crystal Habits

- **Primary habit:** Hexagonal prisms with rhombohedral terminations (same as all quartz)
- **Common forms/faces:** {10ī0}, {01ī1}, {10ī1} — standard quartz forms
- **Twin laws:** Brazil law (twins with one left-handed, one right-handed crystal; rare in prasiolite but possible), Dauphiné law (electrical twins), Japan law (rare, contact twins on {11ī2})
- **Varieties:** None within prasiolite itself, but color grades from pale gray-green to deep leek-green
- **Special morphologies:** Amethyst that has been heat-treated to green retains original crystal form; natural prasiolite forms in geodes and cavities

## Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

### Temperature

- **Nucleation temperature range:** 150–300°C (hydrothermal quartz typical)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 200–300°C for α-quartz; prasiolite color develops during post-growth heating
- **Color formation temperature:** 400–500°C (heat treatment of amethyst → green)
- **Color loss temperature:** >500°C (green fades to colorless/yellow; >600°C typically destroys prasiolite color)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Same as quartz — higher temps favor β-quartz (hexagonal, >573°C at 1 atm), but prasiolite only forms/colors in α-quartz stability field

### Chemistry Required

- **Required elements in broth:**
  - Si: dominant (as H₄SiO₄, >100 ppm)
  - Fe: trace (5–20 ppm as Fe²⁺ for color; higher concentrations make smoky quartz)
  - O: framework component
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - Al³⁺: common impurity in quartz, charge-compensates Fe-related defects
  - Li⁺, Na⁺, K⁺: interstitial alkalis can influence Fe speciation
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - Ti⁴⁺: causes rose/pink quartz (different color center)
  - Mn²⁺: can cause pink coloration
  - High Fe³⁺: creates yellow/citrine color, competes with green
  - Cr³⁺, V³⁺: would create emerald-like green (not prasiolite)
- **Required pH range:** 7–9 (mildly alkaline typical for quartz deposition)
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing to mildly oxidizing (Fe²⁺ must be preserved; strongly oxidizing conditions convert Fe²⁺→Fe³⁺, destroying prasiolite color)
- **Required O₂ range:** Low to moderate (enough for trace Fe³⁺ presence for IVCT, but not so high that all Fe oxidizes)

### Secondary Chemistry Release

- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No — quartz is chemically inert under most conditions
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** None
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Dissolves as H₄SiO₄ in alkaline solutions; releases Fe²⁺ if color centers break down

### Growth Characteristics

- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate (same as quartz baseline)
- **Maximum crystal size in nature:** Prasiolite crystals up to ~10 cm known from Thunder Bay geodes
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–5 cm
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — retrograde solubility of quartz means it precipitates as fluids cool
- **Competes with:** Other silica minerals (chalcedony, opal), feldspars (if Al/K/Na available), zeolites (low-temp silica competitors)

### Stability

- **Breaks down in heat?** Color fades above 500°C, lost by 600°C; crystal structure itself stable to >1470°C (melting)
- **Breaks down in light?** No — but color can fade under prolonged intense UV exposure (Fe²⁺ oxidation)
- **Dissolves in water?** Essentially insoluble; trace solubility at high pH
- **Dissolves in acid?** Resistant to most acids; attacked by HF
- **Oxidizes?** Fe²⁺→Fe³⁺ under oxidizing conditions, converting green prasiolite to colorless/yellow/smoky
- **Dehydrates?** N/A (anhydrous)
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Fe²⁺ can be oxidized by ionizing radiation, causing color fading or shift to smoky

## Paragenesis

- **Forms AFTER:** Volcanic rocks (basalt) or hydrothermal cavities; amethyst must form first for heat-treatment pathway
- **Forms BEFORE:** Late-stage low-temp minerals (calcite, zeolites, chalcedony)
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - Amethyst (precursor for heat-treated prasiolite)
  - Calcite, dolomite (geode linings)
  - Chalcedony, agate
  - Zeolites (heulandite, stilbite in basalt geodes)
  - Chlorite, epidote (green minerals that are NOT prasiolite — distinguish carefully)
- **Zone:** Secondary alteration zone (geodes, vugs), or primary hydrothermal
- **Geological environment:**
  - Basalt geodes (Thunder Bay, Poland, Brazil)
  - Hydrothermal veins and cavities
  - Pegmatite pockets (rare)

## Famous Localities

- **Płóczki Górne, Lower Silesia, Poland:** First discovered in early 1800s. Natural green quartz in volcanic rocks. The type locality.
- **Montezuma Mine, Minas Gerais, Brazil:** Source of heat-treated amethyst that becomes prasiolite. Commercial production since 1950s.
- **Thunder Bay, Ontario, Canada:** Natural prasiolite in amethyst geodes; basalt-hosted.
- **Farm Rooisand, Namibia:** Natural prasiolite reported.
- **Notable specimens:** Most "prasiolite" on market is heat-treated Brazilian amethyst. Natural material <1% of commercial supply.

## Simulator Implementation Notes

### Game Positioning

Prasiolite is a **color variety of quartz**, not a distinct species. However, it deserves its own entry because:
1. It has a unique formation pathway (heat treatment of amethyst)
2. The color center mechanism (Fe²⁺–Fe³⁺ IVCT) is distinct from other green quartz varieties
3. It teaches players about post-growth thermal alteration
4. It introduces instability — the color can be destroyed

### New Parameters Needed

- **Post-growth temperature events:** The simulator needs a mechanism for "geological events" that alter existing crystals without destroying them. Prasiolite requires this.
- **Fe speciation tracking:** Distinguish Fe²⁺ from Fe³⁺ in the broth, not just total Fe.

### Nucleation Rule Pseudocode

```
IF mineral == quartz AND
   Si > 100 ppm AND
   Fe²⁺ > 5 ppm AND
   Fe³⁺ > 1 ppm AND  // Need both for IVCT
   T < 500°C AND      // Must stay below color-loss temp
   pH 7-9 AND
   Eh in reducing-to-mildly-oxidizing band
THEN
   nucleate prasiolite (or convert existing amethyst to prasiolite)
```

### Growth Rule Pseudocode

```
IF prasiolite exists AND
   T < 500°C AND
   Fe²⁺ remains in broth
THEN
   grow at quartz standard rate
ELSE IF T > 500°C
   convert to colorless quartz (prasiolite → clear)
ELSE IF Eh becomes strongly oxidizing
   convert to smoky quartz or citrine (Fe²⁺ oxidizes)
```

### Habit Selection Logic

Same as quartz — hexagonal prisms with rhombohedral terminations. Inherits habit from precursor amethyst if formed by heat treatment.

### Decomposition Products

- **Heat >500°C:** Colorless quartz (Fe²⁺ oxidizes or color center destroyed)
- **Strong oxidation:** Smoky quartz or citrine (Fe³⁺ dominates)
- **Dissolution:** H₄SiO₄ in solution + released Fe²⁺

## Variants for Game

- **Variant 1: Natural Prasiolite**
  - Formed directly with Fe²⁺–Fe³⁺ IVCT during growth
  - Rare, pale green, found only in specific geode localities
  - Conditions: moderate Fe, mildly reducing, no prior amethyst needed

- **Variant 2: Heat-Treated Amethyst**
  - Requires amethyst to exist first, then temperature event 400–500°C
  - More common, deeper green possible
  - Conditions: amethyst + post-growth heat pulse

- **Variant 3: Faded Prasiolite**
  - Formerly green, now colorless or smoky
  - Teaches players about instability
  - Conditions: prasiolite + oxidizing event OR temperature >500°C

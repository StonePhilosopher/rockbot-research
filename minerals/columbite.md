## Species: Columbite ((Fe,Mn)(Nb,Ta)₂O₆)

### Identity
- **Formula:** (Fe,Mn)(Nb,Ta)₂O₆ (columbite-tantalite series, also called "coltan")
- **Crystal system:** Orthorhombic
- **Mineral group:** Oxide (columbite group)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 6 (columbite) – 6.5 (tantalite)
- **Specific gravity:** 5.2 (columbite) – 8.0 (tantalite) — density increases with Ta content
- **Cleavage:** {100} distinct, {010} indistinct, {001} indistinct
- **Fracture:** Subconchoidal to uneven
- **Luster:** Vitreous to submetallic, often resinous on fractures
- **Streak:** Brownish black to black
- **Tenacity:** Brittle

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Black, brownish black, dark brown, reddish brown; streak is reddish brown to black
- **Color causes:** Fe²⁺/Mn²⁺ ratio (Fe-rich = darker, Mn-rich = lighter); trace Ti, W, Sn
- **Transparency:** Opaque in hand specimen; thin fragments translucent to transparent (gemmy columbite-tantalite is extremely rare)
- **Streak:** Brownish black to black
- **Notable visual features:** Prismatic crystals with striated faces parallel to [001]; often embedded in pegmatite matrix. Some crystals show faint iridescence from surface alteration. The high specific gravity makes specimens surprisingly heavy for their size.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Short to long prismatic [001], often tabular on {010}; also massive, granular, in groups
- **Common forms/faces:** {010}, {100}, {001}, {110}, {101} — orthorhombic prisms with pyramidal terminations
- **Twin laws:** Rare — {021} contact twins reported but uncommon
- **Varieties:**
  - **Columbite-(Fe):** Fe > Mn, lower SG (~5.2–6.6)
  - **Columbite-(Mn):** Mn > Fe, lighter color, slightly lower SG
  - **Tantalite-(Fe):** Fe > Mn with Ta > Nb, high SG (~7.9–8.0)
  - **Tantalite-(Mn):** Mn > Fe with Ta > Nb
  - **Coltan:** Industrial/mining term for the mixed series
- **Special morphologies:** Massive, granular, columnar, rarely botryoidal crusts

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 500–700°C (pegmatitic); 450–600°C (granitic)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 550–650°C in LCT pegmatites
- **Decomposition temperature:** Stable to very high temperatures; does not decompose under geological conditions
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Higher temperatures favor larger, more euhedral prismatic crystals; lower temperatures produce granular/massive forms

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Nb and/or Ta (minimum ~10 ppm combined for nucleation; higher for growth)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - Fe (10–1000 ppm) — essential end-member component
  - Mn (10–1000 ppm) — essential end-member component
  - Ti (trace to 5%) — common substituent for Nb/Ta
  - W, Sn, Sc (trace) — minor substituents
  - Li (10–100 ppm) — associated with LCT pegmatite environment
  - F (10–50 ppm) — flux element enhancing mobility
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Ca, Mg (favor pyrochlore or perovskite instead)
- **Required pH range:** Mildly acidic to neutral (pH 4–7)
- **Required Eh range:** Oxidizing to mildly reducing (Nb⁵⁺/Ta⁵⁺ require oxidizing conditions; Fe²⁺/Mn²⁺ can tolerate mildly reducing)
- **Required O₂ range:** Moderate (O₂ > 5 ppm)

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No direct releases; but pegmatite crystallization releases H₂O, F, and other volatiles
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Enrichment of residual melt/fluid in Li, Cs, Ta (LCT signature)
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Essentially insoluble; no significant dissolution products

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Very slow (among the slowest crystallizing oxides)
- **Maximum crystal size:** Up to 25 cm in giant pegmatites (Bikita, Zimbabwe); typical 1–5 cm
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–10 mm (vug crystals are small but well-formed)
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — growth is extremely sluggish below 450°C; above 650°C, crystals may be larger but competition with feldspar/quartz reduces individual size
- **Competes with:** Pyrochlore (for Nb/Ta in carbonatites); fergusonite (for Nb in granites); cassiterite, beryl, spodumene (for space in pegmatite vugs)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** No — extremely refractory; melting point >1500°C
- **Breaks down in light?** No
- **Dissolves in water?** Essentially insoluble
- **Dissolves in acid?** Resistant; attacked only by HF or fusion with alkali
- **Oxidizes?** Already contains Nb⁵⁺/Ta⁵⁺; no further oxidation
- **Dehydrates?** N/A — anhydrous
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Generally stable; Ta-rich varieties may show weak metamictization from U/Th inclusions

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Feldspar, quartz, muscovite (early pegmatite crystallization); beryl may precede or co-crystallize
- **Forms BEFORE:** Lepidolite, spodumene, petalite (latest-stage Li minerals); cassiterite (in some sequences); tantalite may form after columbite in fractionated systems
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - **LCT pegmatites:** beryl (especially morganite, heliodor), spodumene, lepidolite, petalite, tourmaline, amblygonite, cassiterite, wolframite
  - **Nigerian-type pegmatites:** topaz, fluorite, monazite
  - **Granites:** muscovite, biotite, quartz, feldspar, zircon
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene (magmatic — LCT pegmatites and rare-metal granites)
- **Geological environment:**
  1. **LCT pegmatites:** The primary source. Fractionated granitic pegmatites enriched in Li, Cs, Ta. Often in cores or intermediate zones.
  2. **Rare-metal granites:** Albitized and greisenized granites with disseminated columbite.
  3. **Placer deposits:** Secondary concentrations in river gravels (due to high density and hardness)
  4. **Carbonatites:** Pyrochlore is more common, but columbite can occur in late-stage carbonatite dikes

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1: Bikita, Zimbabwe** — World-famous LCT pegmatite. Produced enormous crystals of spodumene, petalite, and columbite-tantalite. The type locality for petalite and eucryptite.
- **Classic locality 2: Minas Gerais, Brazil** — Extensive pegmatite district. Columbite-(Mn) and tantalite in complex zoned pegmatites. Associated with tourmaline, beryl, and topaz.
- **Classic locality 3: Kogi and Nasarawa States, Nigeria** — "Nigerian-type" rare-metal pegmatites. Columbite-tantalite + topaz + fluorite assemblage. Historically important source of tantalum.
- **Classic locality 4: Kenticha, Ethiopia** — One of the world's largest tantalite deposits. LCT pegmatite belt with significant Ta-rich columbite-tantalite.
- **Classic locality 5: Manono-Kitotolo, DRC** — Giant LCT pegmatite, historically one of the largest columbite-tantalite producers. Now largely exhausted but historically crucial.
- **Notable specimens:**
  - Crystals to 10 cm from Minas Gerais, often with perfect prismatic form
  - The "coltan" from eastern DRC that fueled the conflict mineral trade
  - Gem-quality transparent columbite-tantalite is virtually unknown (too opaque, too dark)

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** No
- **SW (255nm) color:** None
- **MW (310nm) color:** None
- **LW (365nm) color:** None
- **Phosphorescent?** No

### Flavor Text

> **The Conflict in Your Pocket**
>
> Columbite-tantalite — "coltan" to the miners — is the mineral that makes your phone work. The tantalum extracted from its Ta-rich end-member, tantalite, coats the capacitors inside every smartphone, every laptop, every game console. The niobium from its Nb-rich end-member, columbite, strengthens jet engine alloys and superconducting magnets. It is a mineral of the space age, disguised as ancient pegmatite.
>
> But the ore has a shadow. Eastern Congo's coltan mines have funded militias, fueled wars, and drawn the phrase "conflict mineral" into international law. The same high density that concentrates it in placers — the same hardness that lets it survive river transport — makes it profitable to mine by hand in remote, lawless valleys.
>
> Geologically, columbite-tantalite is a latecomer. It crystallizes only after a granite melt has fractionated so completely that the residual soup is enriched in elements the rest of the rock rejected: lithium, cesium, tantalum, niobium. It is a mineral of leftovers, of the dregs that became precious through sheer scarcity. In a zoned pegmatite, it appears in the intermediate zones — not the massive feldspar-quartz core, not the outer contact, but the transition where chemistry becomes weird.
>
> The crystals are often small, black, unremarkable to the untrained eye. But their density is a giveaway: a specimen the size of a walnut that weighs like a lead sinker. Prismatic faces show subtle striations. And the series itself is a gradient — from columbite (Nb > Ta, SG ~5.2) through manganocolumbite to tantalite (Ta > Nb, SG ~8.0) — a complete transition from one element to another while the structure stays the same. It is the mineralogical equivalent of a dial turned gradually from one setting to the next.
>
> In the vugg, columbite-tantalite would be a late-stage prize: small, dense, appearing only after the granite has given up its easier minerals. It does not glow. It does not form spectacular crystals. It simply exists, heavy and dark, waiting for the technology of a future age to need it.

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Columbite-(Fe)** — Black prismatic, Fe > Mn, Nb > Ta. Lower density (~5.5). Dark streak. Most common form in typical pegmatites.

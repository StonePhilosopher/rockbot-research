## Species: Turquoise

### Identity
- **Formula:** CuAl₆(PO₄)₄(OH)₈·4H₂O (hydrated copper aluminum phosphate)
- **Crystal system:** Triclinic — very rare in macroscopic crystals
- **Mineral group:** Phosphate (specifically, a hydrated copper-aluminum phosphate)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 5–6 (varies with porosity; dense gem-grade material ~6, porous chalky material ~4–5)
- **Specific gravity:** 2.60–2.83 (lower end for porous material, higher for dense nodules)
- **Cleavage:** None — fractures conchoidally or unevenly
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal to uneven, sometimes earthy/chalky in porous specimens
- **Luster:** Waxy to dull in massive material; vitreous in rare macro-crystals; earthy when dehydrated

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Sky blue to blue-green to apple green; the classic "Persian blue" is robin's-egg sky blue
- **Color causes:** Cu²⁺ in octahedral coordination → blue; increasing Fe³⁺ substitution → greener; dehydration shifts blue → green
- **Transparency:** Opaque (massive/nodular); transparent in extremely rare thin crystals or slices
- **Streak:** White to pale greenish-white; may show brown/black spots from iron oxide inclusions
- **Notable visual features:**
  - **Matrix/web patterns:** Dark spiderweb or dendritic veining of host rock (limonite, iron oxides) permeating the turquoise — the "matrix" is structurally the host rock's fracture network mineralized by turquoise
  - **Nodular/botryoidal habit:** Most natural turquoise forms as cryptocrystalline nodules or crusts, not visible crystals
  - **Porosity variation:** Dense, waxy "natural" turquoise vs. porous, chalky material that requires stabilization
  - **Zonation:** Some nodules show color banding from changing Cu/Fe ratios during growth

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Massive, cryptocrystalline, nodular, botryoidal, reniform, crustiform, vein-filling
- **Common forms/faces:** When crystals are visible (extremely rare), tiny triclinic prisms or thin blades — typically under 1 mm
- **Twin laws:** None reported — massive habit precludes twinning observation
- **Varieties:**
  - **"Persian" turquoise:** Dense, waxy, sky-blue with little or no matrix; from Nishapur, Iran
  - **"Sleeping Beauty":** Classic bright robin's-egg blue, little matrix; Globe, Arizona (mine now closed)
  - **"Bisbee" turquoise:** Rich blue with distinctive black or chocolate-brown spiderweb matrix
  - **"Morenci" turquoise:** Bright blue with pyrite inclusions creating a metallic "fool's gold" matrix
  - **"Green turquoise":** Fe-rich variety, often from Nevada or China; sometimes dehydrated blue turquoise
  - **"Chalk turquoise":** Very porous, pale, chalky material; often dyed or stabilized
- **Special morphologies:**
  - **Nodules:** Rounded, potato-like masses in clay seams or fracture fillings
  - **Crusts:** Thin coatings on fracture walls, sometimes with drusy quartz overgrowths
  - **Pseudomorphs:** After apatite (rare) or after feldspar grains in altered host rock
  - **Vein fillings:** Stringers and networks in altered volcanic host rock, often with chrysocolla

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 15–60°C (supergene — near-surface weathering)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 20–40°C (warm but not hot; arid climates favor concentration)
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~200°C (begins losing structural water; by 400°C fully decomposed to CuO + Al₂O₃ + P₂O₅ gases)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Lower temps + slower evaporation → larger, denser nodules. Higher temps + rapid drying → porous, chalky material with microfractures.

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:**
  - Cu: minimum ~50 ppm (from oxidized chalcopyrite, chalcocite, covellite, native copper)
  - Al: minimum ~200 ppm (from feldspar alteration, clay minerals, or aluminous host rock)
  - P: minimum ~20 ppm (from apatite weathering, organic decomposition, or phosphate-bearing sediments)
  - H₂O: abundant (hydrated mineral — 4 H₂O in formula, plus hydroxyl)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - Fe³⁺: shifts color blue → green; creates limonite matrix/web patterns
  - Zn: traces may shift color slightly; some Zn-rich turquoise localities known
  - SiO₂: excess silica may lead to chalcedony or chrysocolla formation instead of/in competition with turquoise
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - High Ca: competes for P → forms apatite or other Ca-phosphates instead
  - High SiO₂ + low Al: favors chalcedony/chrysocolla over turquoise
  - High Fe²⁺: may reduce Cu²⁺ or create reducing conditions unsuitable for supergene oxidation
  - High CO₃²⁻: competes for Cu → malachite/azurite carbonate formation
- **Required pH range:** 3.0–6.5 (acidic to mildly acidic; supergene fluids from oxidizing sulfides are typically pH 2–5)
- **Required Eh range:** Strongly oxidizing (Eh > +400 mV) — Cu must be in Cu²⁺ oxidation state
- **Required O₂ range:** High oxygen availability — open to atmosphere or near water table fluctuation zone

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** 
  - Nucleation consumes H⁺ (from acidic fluid) and releases OH⁻ into local environment, slightly raising pH in microenvironment
  - Precipitation removes Cu²⁺ and Al³⁺ from fluid, potentially allowing later minerals to nucleate from depleted fluid
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Slight acid neutralization in immediate vicinity
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** At 200–400°C, releases H₂O vapor, then at higher temps releases P₂O₅ (as phosphoric acid vapor) and leaves CuO + Al₂O₃ residue

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Very slow (compared to quartz as baseline ~1). Turquoise grows at geological scales — nodules may take 10,000–1,000,000 years to reach 2–3 cm
- **Maximum crystal size in nature:** Visible crystals to 1–2 mm (extremely rare); nodules to 10+ cm; crusts to several meters along veins
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** Microcrystalline crusts or nodules 1–10 mm; rare drusy coatings on fracture surfaces
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — arid conditions with seasonal wet/dry cycles create episodic growth: wet season dissolves and transports Cu/Al/P; dry season precipitates turquoise. This pulse-growth creates concentric banding in nodules.
- **Competes with:**
  - Malachite/azurite (for Cu)
  - Chrysocolla (for Cu + Si)
  - Brochantite/antlerite/chalcanthite (for Cu + S in oxidizing conditions)
  - Atacamite (for Cu + Cl in saline conditions)
  - Iron oxides/goethite/limonite (for Al and fracture space)
  - Apatite (for P — but apatite is primary, turquoise is secondary)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes. Begins losing water at ~150–200°C. Fully decomposes by 400°C to CuO (tenorite) + Al₂O₃ (corundum) + P₂O₅ gas.
- **Breaks down in light?** Yes — prolonged UV exposure and strong sunlight can shift color blue → green via oxidation/dehydration. Some specimens fade over decades of display.
- **Dissolves in water?** Very slightly soluble in pure water. More soluble in acidic water (pH < 5). Will etch in prolonged contact with acidic fluids.
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — soluble in HCl and HNO₃ with bubbling (carbonate inclusions) and color change. Phosphoric acid can actually stabilize it.
- **Oxidizes?** Cu is already in +2 state, but Fe²⁺ inclusions may oxidize to Fe³⁺, deepening green tones.
- **Dehydrates?** Yes — the 4 H₂O and 8 OH are structural. Dehydration causes color shift (blue → green → brown), shrinkage cracks, and loss of hardness. Rehydration partial but imperfect.
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Unknown/minimal — not typically radiation-damaged; not fluorescent from natural radiation effects.

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:**
  - Primary sulfides (chalcopyrite, bornite, chalcocite) must oxidize first to release Cu²⁺
  - Feldspar must alter to clay to release Al³⁺
  - Apatite or organic matter must weather to release PO₄³⁻
  - Iron oxides may precede or accompany turquoise, creating matrix
- **Forms BEFORE:**
  - Chrysocolla (if silica becomes available later)
  - Brochantite/antlerite/chalcanthite (if sulfate continues to build)
  - Atacamite (if chloride concentrates in evaporative environment)
  - Silica overgrowths (chalcedony, opal) may seal turquoise in later
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - Chalcopyrite, bornite, chalcocite (primary Cu sources — in unoxidized core)
  - Chrysocolla (Cu-silicate, often intergrown or adjacent)
  - Malachite, azurite (other Cu secondary minerals, deeper in oxidation zone)
  - Cuprite, native copper (intermediate oxidation products)
  - Limonite, goethite, hematite (iron oxides creating matrix)
  - Quartz, chalcedony (silica phases, may overgrow or underlie)
  - Kaolinite, montmorillonite (clay alteration products providing Al)
  - Pyrite (may persist in reduced pockets below water table)
  - Manganese oxides (sometimes in matrix)
- **Zone:** Supergene/oxidation zone — specifically the upper, near-surface portion where meteoric water percolates and evaporates
- **Geological environment:**
  - Porphyry copper deposit weathering profiles (most classic deposits)
  - Volcanic-hosted Cu deposits in arid climates
  - Altered felsic igneous rocks (trachyte, rhyolite, andesite) with phosphate access
  - Sedimentary-hosted deposits where Cu-rich fluids meet phosphate-bearing sediments (rare)

### Famous Localities
- **Nishapur District, Razavi Khorasan, Iran:** The classical source for 2,000+ years. Turquoise from weathered trachyte. "Persian blue" is the quality standard — dense, waxy, sky-blue, minimal matrix. Mines on Ali-mersai mountain.
- **Sleeping Beauty Mine, Globe, Arizona, USA:** Produced the iconic bright robin's-egg blue turquoise with little matrix. Mine closed in 2012; material now highly collectible. Porphyry copper system in altered volcanics.
- **Morenci / Bisbee, Arizona, USA:** Morenci = bright blue with pyrite matrix; Bisbee = rich blue to green with dark chocolate or black spiderweb matrix. Both from classic Arizona porphyry copper districts.
- **Other notable deposits:**
  - **Nevada, USA:** Tonopah, Goldfield, Royston — green to blue-green material
  - **New Mexico, USA:** Cerrillos — one of the oldest North American sources, mined by Pueblo peoples pre-Spanish
  - **Tibet / China:** Zhushan, Hubei Province — massive production, wide color range
  - **Sinai, Egypt:** Serabit el-Khadim — mined since ~3000 BCE, possibly the oldest known turquoise source
  - **Eilat, Israel:** Eilat stone (turquoise + chrysocolla + malachite mixture)
- **Notable specimens:**
  - The largest known nodules reach 10+ cm and several kg; most gem-quality material is under 5 cm
  - Persian material from Nishapur was traded across the ancient world; Tutankhamun's death mask includes turquoise inlay from Sinai
  - Sleeping Beauty turquoise set the modern color standard for the gem industry

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally **NO** — natural turquoise is not fluorescent
- **SW (255nm):** None
- **MW (310nm):** None
- **LW (365nm):** Occasionally very weak bluish-white in some specimens; usually non-fluorescent
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** None in natural material
- **Quenched by:** Fe (which is usually present) would quench any potential fluorescence
- **Special note:** **Stabilized or impregnated turquoise may fluoresce weakly** due to resins, waxes, or plastics used in treatment. This is diagnostically useful — if it glows under UV, it's likely been treated. In the Vugg Simulator, this could be a fun "authentication" mechanic: synthetic or treated turquoise glows; natural turquoise doesn't.

- **New parameters needed:** None — Cu, Al, P, pH, O₂, and H₂O are already tracked
- **New events needed:**
  - `event_arid_season`: Evaporation concentrate — all dissolved species increase in concentration; turquoise favored

- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF temp < 80°C
AND pH >= 3.0 AND pH <= 6.5
AND Cu >= 50 ppm
AND Al >= 200 ppm
AND P >= 20 ppm
AND O2 >= 0.05 (oxidizing)
AND H2O >= 0.3 (some moisture)
AND malachite_saturation < 1.0 (carbonate not dominant)
AND chrysocolla_saturation < 1.0 (silicate not dominant)
AND brochantite_saturation < 1.0 (sulfate not dominant)
AND random() < 0.15 (slow nucleation)
→ nucleate turquoise
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF turquoise exists
AND temp < 100°C
AND pH >= 3.0 AND pH <= 7.0
AND Cu >= 30 ppm
AND Al >= 100 ppm
AND P >= 10 ppm
AND O2 >= 0.03
AND H2O >= 0.2
AND NOT (temp > 200°C for >3 consecutive steps)
→ grow at rate = base_rate * min(Cu/50, Al/200, P/20) * (1 if H2O > 0.5 else 0.3 if arid)
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - IF porosity of host > 0.6 AND temp fluctuates wildly → nodular/botryoidal (most common)
  - IF fracture_width < 2 mm AND fluid is confined → vein-filling / crustiform
  - IF arid_cycles > 5 AND slow_evaporation → dense, waxy, gem-quality
  - IF Fe_in_fluid > 100 ppm → matrix/web pattern (iron oxide inclusions)
  - IF very_rare AND slow_growth AND supersaturation_low → microscopic triclinic crystals (extremely rare variant)
- **Decomposition products:**
  - At 200–400°C: releases H₂O vapor, then P₂O₅ gas
  - Solid residue: CuO (tenorite) + Al₂O₃ (corundum) + Fe₂O₃ (hematite, if Fe present)
  - If heated in oxidizing conditions with silica available: may form Cu-bearing aluminosilicate melt

### Variants for Game

- **Variant 1: "Persian Blue"**
  - What makes it different: Dense, waxy, minimal matrix, sky-blue color
  - Conditions: Slow growth in stable arid environment, low Fe, high Cu/Al ratio, minimal fracture intrusion of host rock
  - Rarity: Uncommon (requires stable, long-term evaporation without disruption)

- **Variant 2: "Spiderweb Matrix"**
  - What makes it different: Blue to blue-green turquoise permeated by dark brown/black iron oxide web patterns
  - Conditions: Moderate Fe in fluid (>100 ppm), growth in fractured host rock where iron oxides pre-cement fractures before turquoise fills them
  - Rarity: Common in Bisbee-type deposits
  - Visual: The matrix pattern is procedurally generated from the fracture network geometry

- **Variant 3: "Green Turquoise"**
  - What makes it different: Apple-green to teal color instead of blue
  - Conditions: High Fe³⁺/Cu ratio in fluid, or partial dehydration event during growth history
  - Rarity: Common in Nevada/Chinese deposits
  - Note: In-game, if temperature spikes above 100°C for even a few steps during growth, existing turquoise shifts toward green variant

- **Variant 4: "Chalk Turquoise"**
  - What makes it different: Very pale, porous, crumbly — low value, often dyed in real life
  - Conditions: Rapid growth, high porosity host, insufficient time for dense crystallization
  - Rarity: Common but low-value; might be a "failed growth" outcome in-game

- **Variant 5: "Crystal Turquoise"**
  - What makes it different: Actual visible triclinic crystals (tiny, under 1 mm) — the holy grail for collectors
  - Conditions: Extremely slow growth, perfectly stable chemistry, vug cavity (not fracture), supersaturation kept just barely above 1.0
  - Rarity: Extremely rare (<<1% chance even in perfect conditions)
  - Game mechanic: Achievement for growing this variant

## Paragenetic Context

Turquoise sits at a unique position in the copper oxidation zone. It is **not** the first secondary mineral to form (those are oxides: cuprite, native copper) nor the deepest carbonates (malachite, azurite). It forms where:
1. Sulfides have already oxidized to release Cu²⁺
2. Feldspars have altered to clays, releasing Al³⁺
3. Phosphate has become available (from apatite or organics)
4. The environment is **arid enough** for evaporation to concentrate the fluid
5. The pH is **acidic enough** to keep Cu and Al in solution but not so acidic that everything dissolves

This is a **late-stage, evaporative, phosphate-specific** mineral. In a porphyry copper scenario, it forms in the upper 0–200 meters of the oxidation profile, often above the water table fluctuation zone, where seasonal wetting and drying create the pulse-growth bands.

The **matrix** — those fracture-hosted web patterns — is diagnostic of how turquoise grows. It doesn't replace the host rock wholesale. It fills fractures and void spaces, and where iron oxides have already cemented fracture walls, the turquoise incorporates them as dark veins. The spiderweb is the rock's autobiography, written in healed fractures.

## Game Integration Notes

- Turquoise adds **phosphate chemistry** to the Cu oxidation sequence, giving players a third branch after carbonates (malachite/azurite) and sulfates (brochantite/antlerite/chalcanthite)
- The **arid/evaporative condition** is a new gameplay parameter — does the vug dry out periodically? If so, turquoise is favored. If it stays wet, chrysocolla or carbonates win.
- **Color grading** — blue vs. green vs. pale — gives visual feedback on growth history (Cu/Fe ratio, dehydration events)
- **Matrix patterns** — can be procedurally generated from fracture geometry, giving each specimen unique visual character
- **Stabilization mechanic** — could be a "crafting" option where porous turquoise + resin = more durable but UV-fluorescent (diagnostic fake-detection minigame?)
- **Archaeological resonance** — turquoise is the oldest mined gemstone (Sinai, ~3000 BCE). In a game about time and crystallization, this matters.

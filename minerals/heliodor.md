## Species: Heliodor

### Identity
- **Formula:** Be₃Al₂Si₆O₁₈ with Fe³⁺ substituting for Al³⁺ in octahedral sites
- **Crystal system:** Hexagonal (6/mmm)
- **Mineral group:** Cyclosilicate — color variety of beryl
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 7.5–8.0
- **Specific gravity:** 2.67–2.74 (similar to aquamarine)
- **Cleavage:** Imperfect on {0001}
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal to irregular
- **Luster:** Vitreous to resinous
- **Streak:** White
- **Optical:** Uniaxial (−); nω ≈ 1.57–1.58, nε ≈ 1.565–1.575; birefringence ~0.005

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Yellow to golden-yellow, greenish-yellow, pale yellow, honey-colored; occasionally greenish-tinged
- **Color causes:**
  - **Primary chromophore:** Fe³⁺ in octahedral sites (substituting for Al³⁺) — this is the critical distinction from aquamarine, where Fe²⁺ sits in channels
  - **Fe³⁺ concentration:** Higher Fe³⁺ → deeper golden color. At low concentrations, heliodor is pale lemon-yellow.
  - **Fe³⁺ + Fe²⁺ together:** Fe²⁺ in channels shifts color toward greenish-yellow or green ("green beryl")
  - **Uranium oxide:** Some greenish-yellow heliodor contains trace UO₂, which contributes to color. These specimens may show weak fluorescence.
  - **Heat treatment:** Unlike aquamarine, heat treatment does not improve heliodor color. Heating may actually shift Fe³⁺→Fe²⁺, producing blue tones (undesirable for heliodor).
  - **Irradiation:** Can deepen yellow or produce green tones; effects are variable and not standard in trade
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent; gem-quality is clear with warm "sunny" appearance
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Hexagonal prisms with flat basal pinacoid terminations. Longitudinal striations on prism faces. Pleochroism: yellow/colorless to pale greenish-yellow — weak, like aquamarine. Can form large crystals but rarely as enormous as aquamarine.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Long prismatic hexagonal crystals, striated parallel to c-axis
- **Common forms/faces:** {1010} prism, {0001} basal pinacoid
- **Twin laws:** Rare on {0001} or {1010}
- **Varieties:**
  - **Golden heliodor:** Deep saturated yellow to golden. High Fe³⁺ content. Rare and valuable.
  - **Lemon / pale yellow heliodor:** Low Fe³⁺. Common. Often marketed as simply "yellow beryl."
  - **Green beryl:** Fe³⁺ + Fe²⁺ together. Greenish-yellow to pale green. Intermediate between heliodor and aquamarine. Not emerald (which requires Cr/V).
  - **Rössing heliodor:** Specific locality type from Namibia, often with greenish tone and possible uranium content. The name "heliodor" originated as a trade name for this material.
- **Special morphologies:** Etched crystals in pocket environments. Crystals to 20+ cm known but less common than large aquamarine.

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 300–700°C (pegmatite/hydrothermal)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 400–600°C for gem-quality heliodor
- **Decomposition temperature:** >800°C (beryl melts incongruently)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - Higher T (500–700°C): shorter, slightly more opaque
  - Lower T (300–500°C): longer, clearer, more gemmy

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:**
  - **Be:** ~10+ ppm (beryllium essential)
  - **Al:** significant (for beryl structure)
  - **Si:** abundant
  - **Fe³⁺:** ~500–2000+ ppm (essential for yellow color; must be substitutional, not channel)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - **Fe²⁺ in channels:** Shifts color toward greenish-yellow (green beryl)
  - **Uranium (trace):** May contribute to greenish-yellow in some specimens
  - **Mn (trace):** Does not affect heliodor color directly
  - **Cs, Na, Li, K:** in channel sites; common in late-stage pegmatite fluids
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - **High Fe²⁺ in channels (>1000 ppm):** Shifts toward aquamarine blue or green beryl
  - **High Cr or V:** Produces emerald green
  - **High Mn:** Produces morganite pink
  - **Very low Fe:** Colorless goshenite forms instead
- **Required pH range:** 5–7
- **Required Eh range:** Oxidizing (Fe³⁺ must be stable). Reducing conditions would convert Fe³⁺→Fe²⁺, producing blue/green tones instead of yellow.
- **Required O₂ range:** Moderate to high (relative to other beryl varieties)

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release chemicals when forming?** No significant byproducts
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Consumes Be, Al, Si, Fe from fluid
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** >800°C → chrysoberyl + quartz

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Slow (0.3× quartz baseline)
- **Maximum crystal size:** 20+ cm known; less commonly enormous than aquamarine
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 3–15 cm
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — slower at lower T but clearer
- **Competes with:** Other beryl varieties for Be, Al, Si. If Fe³⁺ is present but Fe²⁺ also abundant, color may be intermediate (green beryl).

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes, >800°C
- **Breaks down in light?** No — heliodor is stable in light
- **Dissolves in water?** Insoluble
- **Dissolves in acid?** Insoluble
- **Oxidizes?** No — Fe³⁺ is already oxidized
- **Dehydrates?** No water in structure
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Generally stable. Trace uranium in some specimens may show weak fluorescence but does not affect color stability.

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Early pegmatite minerals — quartz, K-feldspar, albite, muscovite
- **Forms BEFORE:** Very late-stage — lepidolite, cookeite, pollucite
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - Quartz, K-feldspar, albite, muscovite
  - Schorl (black tourmaline)
  - Aquamarine (if Fe²⁺ is also present in same deposit)
  - Morganite (if Mn is present)
  - Dravite (brown tourmaline, Mg-rich)
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene (pegmatite, miarolitic cavities)
- **Geological environment:**
  - Granite pegmatites, especially miarolitic cavities
  - Hydrothermal veins with oxidizing fluids
  - Some metamorphic environments (less common)

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1:** **Rössing, Erongo Region, Namibia** — The original source of "heliodor" as a trade name. Greenish-yellow to golden crystals from a granite pegmatite associated with the Rössing uranium mine. Often contains trace uranium. Name "heliodor" coined from Greek *helios* (sun) + *doron* (gift).
- **Classic locality 2:** **Volodarsk-Volynskii (Khoroshiv), Zhytomyr Oblast, Ukraine** — Important source of fine golden heliodor. Crystals to 10+ cm, associated with aquamarine, topaz, and quartz in pegmatite cavities. Often etched and modified.
- **Classic locality 3:** **Minas Gerais, Brazil** — Produces yellow to golden heliodor, sometimes associated with aquamarine and morganite in same pegmatite districts. Not as famous as Brazilian aquamarine but significant source.
- **Notable specimens:**
  - **Rössing crystals:** Greenish-yellow heliodor with distinctive "sunny" luster and occasional uranium content. Historic significance as namesake locality.
  - **Ukrainian heliodor:** Golden crystals from Volodarsk-Volynskii, often with perfect terminations and exceptional clarity. Associated with gem topaz.
  - **Namibian "sunstone":** Not to be confused with orthoclase sunstone; some Namibian heliodor is marketed with solar-themed names due to its color.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally **no** for pure heliodor
- **SW (255nm):** None
- **MW (310nm):** None; very weak yellowish-green in some uranium-bearing specimens
- **LW (365nm):** None; very weak greenish-yellow in uranium-bearing specimens
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** Uranium (trace, in some specimens only)
- **Quenched by:** Fe³⁺ suppresses fluorescence
- **Note:** Most heliodor is non-fluorescent. The yellow color comes from Fe³⁺, which is a fluorescence quencher.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:**
  - **Be, Al, Si:** Same blockers as base beryl
  - **Fe oxidation state tracking:** Critical for heliodor. Fe³⁺ must be substitutional (octahedral), not channel. Need Fe³⁺ > 500 ppm and Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺ ratio > 2:1.
  - **Oxidation state (Eh) control:** Heliodor requires oxidizing conditions. This is the key differentiator from aquamarine (reducing) and green beryl (intermediate).
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF trace_Be > 10 ppm
AND trace_Al > 1000 ppm
AND SiO2 > 600
AND temperature BETWEEN 300 AND 700
AND pH BETWEEN 5 AND 7
AND (pegmatite_fractionation_event OR miarolitic_cavity_event)
AND Fe_total > 500 ppm
AND Fe3_ratio > 0.7  /* Fe must be mostly Fe³⁺ */
AND Eh > 0.2  /* oxidizing conditions */
→ nucleate heliodor (max 1 per pocket)
  IF Fe_total > 1500 AND Fe3_ratio > 0.9
    → variant = "Golden Heliodor" (deep gold)
  ELSE IF Fe3_ratio > 0.7
    → variant = "Heliodor" (standard yellow)
  ELSE IF Fe2_ratio > 0.3 AND Fe3_ratio > 0.3
    → variant = "Green Beryl" (intermediate)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF heliodor exists
AND trace_Be > 5 ppm
AND trace_Al > 500 ppm
AND SiO2 > 400
AND temperature BETWEEN 300 AND 700
AND NOT (temperature > 800)
AND Eh > 0.0  /* maintain oxidizing conditions */
→ grow at rate = SLOW (0.3× quartz baseline)
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - T 400–600°C + oxidizing fluid → standard prismatic heliodor
  - T 300–450°C + slow growth → gemmy, clear, long prisms
  - High Fe³⁺ → deeper color but possibly more included
- **Decomposition products:** Same as beryl: >800°C → chrysoberyl + quartz

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Heliodor (Yellow Beryl)** — Standard yellow to golden-yellow. Fe³⁺ dominant (>70%). Oxidizing conditions. Common in pegmatite districts with oxidized fluids.
- **Variant 2: Golden Heliodor** — Deep saturated gold. Fe³⁺ >90%, total Fe >1500 ppm. Rare. Premium gem value. Requires strongly oxidizing, iron-rich fluid.
- **Variant 3: Green Beryl** — Greenish-yellow to pale green. Mixed Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺. Intermediate Eh. Not emerald (no Cr/V). Forms when fluid is not fully oxidizing or reducing.

## Linked Paragenetic Sequence

Heliodor belongs to the **beryl family paragenetic sequence** within pegmatites. See `memory/research-beryl.md` for the full sequence.

**Specific to heliodor — The Iron Oxidation Story:**

In a pegmatite system, iron speciation is controlled by:
1. **Temperature:** Higher T favors Fe²⁺ (reducing, deeper in system)
2. **Oxygen fugacity (fO₂):** Higher fO₂ favors Fe³⁺ (oxidizing, shallower or more water-rich)
3. **pH:** Lower pH favors Fe²⁺ mobility; higher pH favors Fe³⁺ precipitation

**Fluid evolution model:**
- Early, deep, hot fluid → Fe²⁺ dominant → aquamarine (if Fe²⁺ enters channels) or colorless goshenite
- Intermediate fluid → mixed Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺ → green beryl
- Late, cooler, more oxidized fluid → Fe³⁺ dominant → heliodor
- Very late, Mn-enriched fluid → morganite

This creates a potential **zoning pattern** within a single pegmatite:
- Deep/core: aquamarine or goshenite
- Intermediate: green beryl or heliodor
- Late/rim: heliodor or morganite

In the simulator, this could be modeled as an **Eh gradient** within the vug, with different beryl varieties nucleating at different positions based on local oxidation state.

## Research Notes

### Fe²⁺ vs. Fe³⁺ in Beryl: Site Occupancy
This is one of the most elegant examples of crystal chemistry in gemology:

| Oxidation State | Site | Color | Variety |
|----------------|------|-------|---------|
| Fe²⁺ | Channel | Blue | Aquamarine |
| Fe²⁺ | Octahedral (Al replacement) | Weak green | Green beryl (minor contribution) |
| Fe³⁺ | Octahedral (Al replacement) | Yellow | Heliodor |
| Fe²⁺ + Fe³⁺ | Both sites | Deep blue | Maxixe-type (unstable) |
| Fe²⁺ + Fe³⁺ | Channel + octahedral | Greenish-blue | Green beryl |

The channel site in beryl is a ring of six SiO₄ tetrahedra with a central cavity ~5 Å in diameter. It can accommodate:
- H₂O molecules
- Alkali ions (Na⁺, K⁺, Cs⁺, Li⁺)
- Small transition metal ions (Fe²⁺, Mg²⁺)
- CO₃²⁻, NO₃⁻ anions (in maxixe)

The octahedral site is part of the main structural framework. Substitutions here are:
- Al³⁺ → Cr³⁺, V³⁺ (emerald)
- Al³⁺ → Fe³⁺, Mn³⁺ (heliodor, "red beryl")
- Al³⁺ → Mn²⁺ (morganite — but Mn²⁺ is much larger than Al³⁺, so limited substitution)
- Al³⁺ → Sc³⁺ (bazzite, a separate Be-Sc silicate species)

### The "Red Beryl" Connection
Red beryl (bixbite) is Be₃Al₂Si₆O₁₈ with Mn³⁺ in octahedral sites. Mn³⁺ is unusual because:
- Mn²⁺ is too large for comfortable octahedral substitution → weak color (morganite)
- Mn³⁺ is closer to Al³⁺ in size and can substitute more readily → intense red
- But Mn³⁺ requires oxidizing conditions (Mn²⁺ → Mn³⁺), which is why red beryl is so rare
- Red beryl also requires a specific geological environment: topaz-bearing rhyolites, low pressure, ~650°C, vapor-rich

This is NOT heliodor but is the Mn analog of heliodor's Fe³⁺ mechanism. It could be a future ultra-rare variant.

## Game Value Assessment

**High value for Vugg Simulator:**
- **Visual:** Hexagonal prisms, warm yellow to gold — sunny and distinctive
- **Gem connection:** Less famous than aquamarine or emerald but recognized by gem enthusiasts
- **Chemistry teaching:** Fe³⁺ vs. Fe²⁺ site occupancy is advanced but can be simplified to "oxidation state = color"
- **Oxidation state mechanic:** Heliodor requires oxidizing conditions — this creates interesting gameplay where fluid Eh controls which beryl variety forms
- **Eh gradient concept:** Could introduce the idea that different parts of the same vug have different oxidation states
- **Contrast with aquamarine:** Two iron-colored beryls, one blue (reducing), one yellow (oxidizing) — perfect teaching pair

**Blocker:** Requires Be + Al + Fe oxidation state tracking + Eh control. Same blockers as base beryl. Implementation priority: **Medium-High** — excellent when Be/Al are added. The oxidation-state contrast with aquamarine is a particularly strong gameplay mechanic.

## Species: Uvarovite

### Identity
- **Formula:** Ca₃Cr₂Si₃O₁₂
- **Crystal system:** Cubic (isometric)
- **Mineral group:** Garnet group (nesosilicate), ugrandite subgroup
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 6.5–7.5
- **Specific gravity:** 3.77–3.81
- **Cleavage:** None (garnets have no cleavage)
- **Fracture:** Uneven to conchoidal
- **Luster:** Vitreous to subadamantine
- **IMA symbol:** Uv
- **Space group:** Ia3d
- **Unit cell:** a = 11.99 Å; Z = 8

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Emerald green, vivid green, occasionally green-black
- **Color causes:** Cr³⁺ in octahedral coordination — the only consistently green garnet species. The chromium chromophore produces an intense emerald-green hue unmatched by other garnets.
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent; thin fragments and small crystals tend toward transparency
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Crystals are typically very small (sub-millimeter to a few millimeters), often forming sparkling drusy coatings on host rock. The contrast between dark matrix and brilliant green crystals is distinctive. Occurs in solid solution with grossular (Ca₃Al₂Si₃O₁₂) and andradite (Ca₃Fe₂Si₃O₁₂), producing color shifts toward yellow-green or brown-green.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Dodecahedral {110} and trapezohedral {211} crystals; euhedral when space permits
- **Common forms/faces:** Dodecahedra, trapezohedra; rarely icositetrahedra
- **Twin laws:** Twinning is rare in garnets; not a significant feature
- **Varieties:** 
  - Uvarovite-grossular series: lighter green, yellowish-green as Al substitutes for Cr
  - Uvarovite-andradite series: darker, brownish-green as Fe substitutes for Cr
- **Special morphologies:** Drusy coatings (most common), granular aggregates, massive; crystals rarely exceed 1–2 mm in natural occurrences. Museum-quality specimens from Outokumpu, Finland, are exceptional at 3–5 mm.

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 300–600°C (hydrothermal / skarn / serpentinite alteration)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 400–550°C (contact metamorphic skarns); lower temperatures (~200–350°C) in serpentinized ultramafic rocks
- **Decomposition temperature:** Stable to >1400°C at low pressure (uvarovite-grossular solid solutions stable to 1410°C). Will not thermally decompose under any plausible vugg scenario.
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Higher temperatures in skarns favor larger euhedral crystals; lower-temperature serpentinite alteration produces finer drusy coatings.

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Ca (major), Cr (major), Si (major)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Al (promotes uvarovite-grossular solid solution, shifts color toward yellow-green), Fe (promotes uvarovite-andradite solid solution, shifts toward brown-green)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Mg suppresses uvarovite in favor of chromite or serpentine minerals; high Mn may shift toward other garnet species
- **Required pH range:** Neutral to mildly alkaline (pH 7–9 in serpentinizing fluids); acidic conditions dissolve the carbonate source rock and release Ca but also mobilize Cr as chromate, which is unfavorable for garnet growth
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing to mildly oxidizing. Uvarovite requires Cr³⁺, not Cr⁶⁺ (chromate). Strongly oxidizing conditions convert Cr³⁺ to Cr⁶⁺ and prevent nucleation.
- **Required O₂ range:** Low O₂; serpentinization and skarn formation both occur in reduced environments

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Yes — serpentinization (the process that creates uvarovite's host rock) releases H₂ and CH₄ as byproducts through the reaction of olivine with water. In a vugg simulator context, this could be modeled as a H₂ pulse during nucleation.
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** H₂ from concurrent serpentinization; CO₂ if forming from carbonate skarn reactions
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Uvarovite is essentially insoluble in water and acid-resistant. Dissolution only occurs in strong HF. In the game, it would be among the most stable minerals.

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Very slow. Uvarovite crystals are among the smallest of any garnet species due to slow growth kinetics and limited element availability.
- **Maximum crystal size:** Exceptional museum specimens reach 3–5 mm; most natural crystals are <1 mm
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 0.1–1 mm drusy coatings; individual euhedral crystals up to 2–3 mm only in exceptional conditions
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — retrograde behavior. Uvarovite is more stable at lower temperatures in the presence of adequate Ca and Cr, but nucleation requires an initial high-temperature event (skarn formation or serpentinization) to mobilize Cr from chromite.
- **Competes with:** Grossular (for Ca and Si), andradite (for Ca and Si), chromite (for Cr), diopside, wollastonite, serpentine group minerals

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** No — stable to >1400°C. Will not thermally decompose.
- **Breaks down in light?** No.
- **Dissolves in water?** Essentially insoluble.
- **Dissolves in acid?** Resistant to HCl, HNO₃, H₂SO₄. Dissolves only in HF or prolonged boiling in concentrated HCl.
- **Oxidizes?** Cr³⁺ is the stable oxidation state in uvarovite. Strong oxidation converts surface Cr to Cr⁶⁺ (toxic chromate), but the mineral structure itself is resistant.
- **Dehydrates?** No — anhydrous garnet structure.
- **Radiation sensitivity:** No known radiation damage effects. Cr³⁺-bearing minerals are generally radiation-stable.

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Chromite must be present and accessible for Cr mobilization. Serpentinization of olivine/orthopyroxene must precede or coincide with uvarovite nucleation to provide host rock fractures and fluids.
- **Forms BEFORE:** In the weathering sequence, uvarovite is highly resistant and may outlast its host rock. In hydrothermal sequences, it may be overgrown by late-stage calcite, quartz, or sulfides.
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - **In serpentinites:** Chromite (source of Cr), serpentine (lizardite, antigorite), fuchsite (Cr-bearing muscovite), magnetite, brucite, talc
  - **In skarns:** Diopside, wollastonite, vesuvianite, grossular, andradite, calcite, dolomite, epidote
  - **In ophiolites:** Chromitite layers, podiform chromite, platinum group minerals
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene (skarn, serpentinization) and supergene/oxidation (as chromite weathers, uvarovite may persist as a resistant relic)
- **Geological environment:** Serpentinized ultramafic rocks (ophiolites, mantle wedges), contact metamorphic skarns (ultramafic intrusion into carbonate country rock), chromite deposits, rodingites

### Famous Localities
- **Outokumpu, Finland:** The most significant source historically. Now-closed copper mine produced the finest museum-quality specimens — brilliant emerald-green drusy coatings up to 5 mm. Found in chromite-bearing rocks associated with volcanogenic Cu-Co-Zn sulfide ores. The Outokumpu district has unusually high chromium content in its silicate assemblages.
- **Saranovskii Mine, Ural Mountains, Russia:** Type locality, discovered 1832 by Germain Henri Hess. Named after Count Sergei Semenovich Uvarov (1765–1855), Russian statesman and amateur mineral collector. Crystals associated with chromite in serpentinized peridotite.
- **Kip Dağlari, Turkey (Eskişehir Province):** Serpentinized peridotite with chromite; fine drusy coatings.
- **Other notable localities:** Røros, Norway; Val Malenco, Italy; Bushveld Igneous Complex, South Africa; Vumba Schist Belt, Botswana; Delight, Maryland and Lancaster County, Pennsylvania, USA; Serpentine Belt of the Klamath Mountains, California.
- **Notable specimens:** Outokumpu specimens command premium prices in the collector market due to their exceptional color and crystal size. A single 5 mm crystal on matrix from Outokumpu is considered a world-class specimen.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** YES — notably and characteristically fluorescent
- **SW (255nm) color:** Red
- **MW (310nm) color:** Red (reported)
- **LW (365nm) color:** Red
- **Phosphorescent:** No significant phosphorescence reported
- **Activator:** Cr³⁺ in the octahedral site of the garnet structure. The Cr³⁺ fluorescence is a characteristic emission in the red region (~700 nm) due to the ²E → ⁴A₂ transition.
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺ in solid solution (andradite component) can quench Cr³⁺ fluorescence. Pure uvarovite fluoresces most strongly; Fe-bearing varieties fluoresce weakly or not at all.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** Ca (calcium) major element; Si (silicon) major element. Cr is already tracked in the trace system. Serpentinization index (measure of ultramafic alteration progress).
- **New events needed:** `event_serpentinization` — releases H₂, raises pH, mobilizes Cr from chromite. `event_skarn_contact` — ultramafic intrusion meets carbonate rock, creates high-T Ca-Cr-Si fluid.
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF (temperature > 300°C AND temperature < 700°C) AND
   (Ca > 1000 ppm AND Cr > 500 ppm AND Si > 1000 ppm) AND
   (pH > 7 AND pH < 10) AND
   (Eh < 0.2 OR reducing) AND
   (chromite_present OR serpentinization_active OR skarn_contact_active) AND
   (vug_fill < 0.8)
→ nucleate uvarovite (max 1 per vug; rare spawn)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF (temperature > 250°C AND temperature < 600°C) AND
   (Ca > 500 ppm AND Cr > 200 ppm AND Si > 500 ppm) AND
   (pH > 6.5 AND pH < 10) AND
   (vug_fill < 0.95)
→ grow at rate 0.3× quartz_baseline (very slow)
→ habit: drusy coating (probability 0.7) OR dodecahedral crystal (probability 0.3)
→ IF Al > 200 ppm: shift toward uvarovite-grossular (lighter green)
→ IF Fe > 300 ppm: shift toward uvarovite-andradite (darker, brown-green)
```
- **Habit selection logic:** Small vug → drusy coating; large vug → euhedral dodecahedral crystals. Temperature >500°C favors larger crystals; <350°C favors drusy. High fluid turbulence promotes drusy; static fluids promote euhedral growth.
- **Decomposition products:** None — uvarovite does not thermally decompose under any plausible vugg scenario. It persists as a relic mineral even when all else weathers away.

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1 — Pure Uvarovite:** Classic emerald-green drusy coating on dark matrix. Forms in serpentinized ultramafic vugs. Requires chromite source + serpentinization event. Brightest red UV fluorescence. Most common variant.
- **Variant 2 — Skarn Uvarovite:** Larger euhedral dodecahedral crystals (up to 3 mm in game scale), lighter green with possible yellowish tint from Al substitution (uvarovite-grossular). Forms in skarn contact zones. Lower UV fluorescence due to Fe quenching. Rarer.
- **Variant 3 — Uvarovite Relic:** Weathered specimen where host rock has dissolved away, leaving only the resistant uvarovite crystals as a loose green sand or coating on altered rock. No associated chromite. Appears in late-game weathering zones. May be the only mineral surviving after everything else has dissolved.

## Chromian Clinochlore (Associated Mineral)

*Chromian clinochlore now has its own complete research file at `memory/research-chromian-clinochlore.md`. The brief notes below are retained for context; see the full file for simulator parameters, paragenetic sequence, and game variants.*

- **Formula:** (Mg,Fe²⁺)₆(Si,Al)₄O₁₀(OH)₈ with Cr³⁺ substituting for Al and/or Fe
- **Crystal system:** Monoclinic
- **Mineral group:** Chlorite group (phyllosilicate)
- **Hardness:** 2–2.5
- **Specific gravity:** ~2.65–2.78
- **Color:** Emerald green, grass green (Cr³⁺); variety kämmererite is pink to deep crimson
- **Formation:** Same environments as uvarovite — serpentinized ultramafic rocks, rodingites, chromite alteration zones. Forms at lower temperatures than uvarovite (150–400°C) as a retrograde alteration product.
- **Paragenesis:** Often intergrown with uvarovite, chromite, serpentine, and talc. Represents a later, cooler stage of the same hydrothermal system that produces uvarovite. Full vein sequence: clinochlore → uvarovite → chromian clinochlore → rutile → titanite → calcite.
- **Game role:** Three variants in game: Kämmererite (crimson leaf), Chromian Green (serpentinite cloak), Pseudomorph After Garnet (ghost crystal). See full research file for nucleation rules and pseudocode.

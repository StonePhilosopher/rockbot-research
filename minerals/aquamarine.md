## Species: Aquamarine

### Identity
- **Formula:** Be₃Al₂Si₆O₁₈ with Fe²⁺ in channel sites (and minor Fe³⁺)
- **Crystal system:** Hexagonal (6/mmm)
- **Mineral group:** Cyclosilicate — color variety of beryl
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 7.5–8.0
- **Specific gravity:** 2.68–2.74
- **Cleavage:** Imperfect on {0001}
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal to irregular
- **Luster:** Vitreous to resinous
- **Streak:** White
- **Optical:** Uniaxial (−); nω ≈ 1.57–1.58, nε ≈ 1.565–1.575; birefringence low (~0.005)

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Pale blue to bluish-green; deep blue specimens called "Santa Maria" aquamarine; greenish-blue "Maxixe-type" is unstable
- **Color causes:**
  - **Primary chromophore:** Fe²⁺ in structural channels (not substituting for Al in octahedral sites — this is critical distinction from heliodor)
  - **Fe²⁺ alone:** pale blue-green
  - **Fe²⁺ + Fe³⁺ together:** deeper blue (maxixe-type, but see stability note below)
  - **Heat treatment:** converts Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺, deepening blue and removing green component. Most commercial aquamarine is heat-treated.
  - **Natural vs. treated:** Difficult to distinguish; treated stones are stable and accepted in trade
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent; gem-quality is water-clear
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Hexagonal prisms with flat basal pinacoid terminations. Longitudinal striations on prism faces. Can form extraordinarily large crystals (meters long). Pleochroism: blue/colorless to pale green — weak compared to other beryl varieties.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Long prismatic hexagonal crystals, often striated parallel to c-axis
- **Common forms/faces:** {1010} prism, {0001} basal pinacoid
- **Twin laws:** Rare on {0001} or {1010}
- **Varieties:**
  - **Common aquamarine:** Pale blue to greenish-blue
  - **Santa Maria:** Deep, saturated blue — benchmark quality from Brazil (Santa Maria de Itabira mine). Now also from Mozambique, Madagascar.
  - **Espirito Santo:** Medium blue from Espírito Santo state, Brazil.
  - **Maxixe-type:** Deep blue from irradiation-induced color centers (CO₃²⁻ in channels), NOT stable Fe²⁺ color. Fades in light. Distinct from true aquamarine.
  - **Moss aquamarine:** Contains moss-like inclusions of hematite or other iron oxides.
- **Special morphologies:** Etched crystals common in pegmatite pockets. Water-clear gemy crystals to 1+ meters. Some Brazilian crystals weigh hundreds of kilograms.

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 300–700°C (pegmatite/hydrothermal)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 350–550°C for gem-quality aquamarine in miarolitic cavities
- **Decomposition temperature:** >800°C (beryl melts incongruently)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - Higher T (500–700°C): shorter, more opaque crystals; common in pegmatite intermediate zones
  - Lower T (300–500°C): longer, clearer, more gemmy; miarolitic cavities

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:**
  - **Be:** ~10+ ppm (beryllium essential)
  - **Al:** significant (for beryl structure)
  - **Si:** abundant
  - **Fe²⁺:** ~500–3000 ppm (the color source — must be present in channels, not just as impurity)
  - **Fe³⁺:** trace to moderate (contributes to green component; heat treatment converts to Fe²⁺)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - **Cs, Na, K, Li:** in channel sites; alkali content correlates with pegmatite fractionation
  - **Mn:** trace; does not affect color in aquamarine
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - **High Cr or V:** would push toward emerald green, not blue
  - **High Mn:** would push toward morganite pink
  - **Very high Fe³⁺ without Fe²⁺:** produces heliodor yellow, not aquamarine blue
- **Required pH range:** 5–7 (slightly acidic to neutral)
- **Required Eh range:** Slightly reducing to mildly oxidizing (Fe²⁺ must be present and stable in channels)
- **Required O₂ range:** Moderate; not strongly controlling

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release chemicals when forming?** No significant byproducts
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Consumes Be, Al, Si, Fe from fluid
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** At >800°C, decomposes to chrysoberyl + quartz

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Slow (0.3× quartz baseline)
- **Maximum crystal size:** Extraordinary — Brazilian crystals to 1.5+ m, 100+ kg
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 2–20 cm for gem pockets
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — slower at lower T but clearer; faster at higher T but more included
- **Competes with:** Quartz, feldspar, muscovite, other beryl varieties (emerald if Cr/V present, morganite if Mn present)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes, >800°C
- **Breaks down in light?** Standard aquamarine: **stable**. Maxixe-type: **unstable** — fades in sunlight (see Research Notes below)
- **Dissolves in water?** Insoluble
- **Dissolves in acid?** Insoluble
- **Oxidizes?** No
- **Dehydrates?** No water in structure
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Maxixe-type color is radiation-induced and fades; true aquamarine Fe²⁺ color is stable

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Early pegmatite minerals — quartz, K-feldspar, albite, muscovite
- **Forms BEFORE:** Very late-stage — lepidolite, cookeite, pollucite, gem tourmaline
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - Quartz, K-feldspar, albite, muscovite, biotite
  - Schorl (black tourmaline) in border zones
  - Dravite/elbaite (gem tourmaline) in late pockets
  - Columbite-tantalite in intermediate zones
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene (pegmatite, miarolitic cavities)
- **Geological environment:**
  - Granite pegmatites, especially miarolitic cavities
  - High-T hydrothermal veins associated with Sn-W deposits (some aquamarine)
  - Metamorphic mica schists (less common)

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1:** **Santa Maria de Itabira, Minas Gerais, Brazil** — The benchmark for deep blue aquamarine. "Santa Maria" is a color grade, not just a locality. Original mine produced the deepest saturated blues.
- **Classic locality 2:** **Shigar Valley, northern Pakistan** — Spectacular gem crystals, vivid blue, hexagonal prisms with perfect terminations. Modern classic. Crystals to 30+ cm, exceptionally transparent.
- **Classic locality 3:** **Marambaia, Minas Gerais, Brazil** — Historic source of enormous crystals. Dom Pedro crystal: 10,363 ct (2.07 kg), 35.4 cm tall, cut by Bernd Munsteiner into an obelisk. The largest cut aquamarine in the world.
- **Notable specimens:**
  - **Dom Pedro crystal and cut stone:** Found 1980s Brazil. Original crystal ~1 m long, 45 kg. Cut stone 35.4 cm, 10,363 ct. Smithsonian Institution.
  - **Roosevelt Aquamarine:** 1,847 ct, gifted to Eleanor Roosevelt by Brazilian government in 1936. Also at Smithsonian.
  - **The "Martha Rocha":** 134 lb (60.8 kg) crystal from Brazil, named after Miss Brazil 1954.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally **no** for pure aquamarine
- **SW (255nm):** None
- **MW (310nm):** None
- **LW (365nm):** None; occasionally very weak yellowish-green due to trace Fe
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** None in pure aquamarine
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺ suppresses any potential fluorescence
- **Note:** Some aquamarine may show weak greenish-yellow LW fluorescence from trace uranium or other impurities, but this is not diagnostic.

- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```

AND trace_Al > 1000 ppm
AND SiO2 > 600
AND temperature BETWEEN 300 AND 700
AND pH BETWEEN 5 AND 7
```

- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF aquamarine exists
AND trace_Be > 5 ppm
AND trace_Al > 500 ppm
AND SiO2 > 400
AND temperature BETWEEN 300 AND 700
AND NOT (temperature > 800)
→ grow at rate = SLOW (0.3× quartz baseline)
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - T 300–450°C + slow growth → long prismatic, gemmy, transparent

- **Decomposition products:** Same as beryl: >800°C → chrysoberyl + quartz

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Standard Aquamarine** — Pale blue to blue-green. Fe²⁺ dominant but some Fe³⁺ present. Common in most pegmatite pockets. May have slight green component.
- **Variant 2: Santa Maria Aquamarine** — Deep, saturated blue. High Fe²⁺ (>1500 ppm), high Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺ ratio (>2:1). Rare. Premium gem value. Named after Brazilian benchmark locality.
- **Variant 3: Maxixe-Type (Unstable Blue)** — Deep blue from irradiation-induced CO₃²⁻ color centers, NOT Fe²⁺. Unstable: fades in sunlight/heat. Distinct nucleation rule: requires natural radiation exposure event (from host granite ⁴⁰K). Color fades over game turns if exposed to "light" events. Can be restored by irradiation event. Educational about color center vs. chromophore chemistry.

## Linked Paragenetic Sequence

Aquamarine is part of the **beryl family paragenetic sequence** within pegmatites:

1. **Early magmatic:** Colorless goshenite (no chromophores) or pale green beryl
2. **Intermediate:** As fluid fractionates and trace elements concentrate:
   - **Fe²⁺-rich fluid** → aquamarine (blue)
   - **Fe³⁺-rich fluid** → heliodor (yellow)
   - **Mn²⁺-rich fluid** → morganite (pink)
   - **Cr³⁺/V³⁺-rich fluid** → emerald (green) — but emerald typically forms in different geological environments (schist-hosted), not classic pegmatites
3. **Late hydrothermal:** Gem-quality crystals in miarolitic cavities — all color varieties can form here depending on local fluid chemistry

**Key insight:** Color varieties are NOT a temporal sequence. They form based on local trace element availability, not progressive oxidation. A single pegmatite can produce aquamarine, heliodor, and morganite in different pockets simultaneously if fluid chemistry varies spatially.

## Research Notes

### Maxixe vs. Aquamarine Stability
This is a critical distinction often confused in gemology:

| Feature | True Aquamarine | Maxixe-Type |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| Color source | Fe²⁺ in channels | Irradiation-induced CO₃²⁻ color centers |
| Stability | **Stable in light** | **Fades in sunlight** |
| Heat treatment | Deepens blue (accepted) | Destroys color |
| Fe content | Present | May or may not be present |
| Cs/Li content | Variable | Often high Cs₂O (~2.8%), Li₂O (~1%) |
| First described | Ancient | 1917, Maxixe mine, Brazil |

Maxixe-type color can be induced in ANY beryl (including colorless goshenite) by irradiation. It is not a true variety but a treatment state. Natural maxixe is rare; most "maxixe" on the market is irradiated goshenite. The color fades to brown-yellow in daylight over months.

In the Vugg Simulator, maxixe-type could be implemented as:
- A rare natural variant requiring a `radiation_exposure_event`
- An unstable color that triggers `event_color_fade` in light
- A teaching moment about color centers vs. chromophores

### Heat Treatment in Trade
Nearly all commercial aquamarine is heat-treated at 400–600°C to convert Fe³⁺→Fe²⁺. This:
- Deepens blue
- Removes green component
- Is permanent and stable
- Is accepted by gem trade and labs
- Is undetectable without advanced spectroscopy

In game: heat treatment could be an optional post-formation event with flavor text but no gameplay consequence (unlike maxixe fading, which WOULD affect value).

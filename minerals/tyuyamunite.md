# Mineral Species — Tyuyamunite

## Species: Tyuyamunite

### Identity
- **Formula:** Ca(UO₂)₂(VO₄)₂·(5–8)H₂O
- **Crystal system:** Orthorhombic (mmm)
- **Mineral group:** Uranyl vanadate (carnotite group)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 1½ – 2
- **Specific gravity:** 3.57 – 4.35
- **Cleavage:** Perfect on {001} (micaceous); distinct on {100} and {010}
- **Fracture:** Waxy, flexible (due to layered structure and water content)
- **Luster:** Adamantine, waxy, pearly on {101}, dull when massive

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Bright canary yellow, lemon-yellow; greenish yellow upon sunlight exposure (dehydration to metatyuyamunite)
- **Color causes:** Charge transfer between U⁶⁺ and V⁵⁺ in the uranyl vanadate structure; the intense yellow is intrinsic to the UO₂²⁺–VO₄³⁻ chromophore
- **Transparency:** Translucent to opaque; thin plates can be translucent
- **Streak:** Yellow
- **Notable visual features:** Platy, micaceous crystals; often forms radiating sprays, crusts, or powdery coatings on sandstone. Waxy feel. Extremely soft — can be scratched by a fingernail. Flexible thin flakes. Greenish tinge develops with dehydration or sunlight exposure.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Platy crystals, often micaceous and flexible
- **Common forms/faces:** Tabular on {001}; radiating sprays; crusts; earthy/powdery coatings; massive
- **Twin laws:** Not commonly reported
- **Varieties:** None formally recognized (metatyuyamunite is the dehydration product, not a variety)
- **Special morphologies:** Radiating rosettes, botryoidal crusts, thin films on matrix

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 10–40°C (supergene/oxidation zone, ambient to shallow subsurface)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 15–25°C (near-surface oxidizing conditions)
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~100–150°C (loses water progressively; metatyuyamunite forms at room temperature in dry air)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Cool, humid conditions favor tyuyamunite; warm, arid conditions drive dehydration to metatyuyamunite

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Ca²⁺ (moderate), U⁶⁺ (high — from uraninite oxidation), V⁵⁺ (high — from primary vanadium mineral oxidation), H₂O
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** K⁺ (if present, carnotite forms instead — K analogue); trace Cu²⁺, Pb²⁺, Ba²⁺ may substitute
- **Inhibiting elements:** High K⁺ → carnotite (competitive exclusion of tyuyamunite); high Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺ → competes for V; high carbonate → competes for Ca
- **Required pH range:** 6.0–8.5 (slightly acidic to mildly alkaline; carnotite group stability is pH-sensitive)
- **Required Eh range:** Strongly oxidizing (+0.4 to +0.8 V) — requires U⁶⁺ and V⁵⁺
- **Required O₂ range:** Very high — supergene oxidation zone

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Dehydration releases H₂O vapor; oxidation of uraninite releases radon (Rn) and other daughter products (minor)
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Consumes Ca²⁺ + UO₂²⁺ + VO₄³⁻ from fluid; minor H⁺ release
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Dissolves in acid, releasing U⁶⁺ + V⁵⁺ + Ca²⁺; dehydration → metatyuyamunite + H₂O

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate — fast initial nucleation on surfaces, slow continued growth due to Ca/availability limits
- **Maximum crystal size:** Plates to ~1 cm; crusts to several mm thick; rarely larger
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** Microcrystalline to 1–2 mm plates; coatings and crusts more common than discrete crystals
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Lower T favors tyuyamunite; higher T/dry air favors metatyuyamunite
- **Competes with:** Carnotite (K analogue — K:Ca ratio determines which forms), autunite (phosphate analogue), tyuyamunite-metatyuyamunite stability (humidity-dependent)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Loses water at ~100°C; complete dehydration by 150°C → metatyuyamunite
- **Breaks down in light?** Indirectly — sunlight/UV accelerates dehydration; prolonged UV may alter color slightly
- **Dissolves in water?** Very slightly; soluble in dilute acids
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — soluble in HCl, HNO₃, H₂SO₄; dissolves with yellow color in solution
- **Oxidizes?** Already fully oxidized (U⁶⁺, V⁵⁺); stable in oxidizing conditions. Reducing conditions → reduction of U⁶⁺ → uraninite or coffinite.
- **Dehydrates?** Yes — spontaneously in dry atmosphere to metatyuyamunite Ca(UO₂)₂(VO₄)₂·(3–5)H₂O; reversible by rehydration
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Self-radiation damage from α-decay of U; amorphization over geological time; metamict zones may develop

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Uraninite (UO₂) and primary vanadium minerals (e.g., montroseite, corvusite) must oxidize first to provide U⁶⁺ and V⁵⁺; also requires a calcium source (calcite dissolution, plagioclase weathering)
- **Forms BEFORE:** May dehydrate to metatyuyamunite with time; may be dissolved/reprecipitated in fluctuating groundwater; late-stage may convert to calcium uranyl carbonates if CO₃ becomes available
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Uraninite, carnotite, metatyuyamunite, autunite, torbernite, uranophane, gypsum, calcite, limonite/goethite, montroseite, corvusite, hewettite
- **Zone:** Supergene/oxidation zone; Colorado Plateau-type sandstone-hosted U-V deposits
- **Geological environment:** Oxidized zones of sandstone-hosted uranium-vanadium deposits (roll-front, tabular deposits); also in calcrete/alkaline playa environments; fissure fillings in U-V deposits

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1:** Tyuya-Muyun (Tuya-Moyun), Fergana Valley, Osh Region, Kyrgyzstan — type locality (1912), named by Nenadkevich after this deposit
- **Classic locality 2:** Grants district, McKinley and Valencia Counties, New Mexico, USA — Colorado Plateau-type deposits, abundant yellow crusts
- **Classic locality 3:** Marie Mine, Carbon County, Montana, USA — microcrystalline yellow tyuyamunite in oxidized U-V ore
- **Notable specimens:** Bright yellow radiating sprays from Tyuya-Muyun; thick yellow crusts on sandstone from Grants, NM; specimens showing color zonation from tyuyamunite (bright yellow) to metatyuyamunite (greenish-yellow) rims

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally weak to none; associated calcite may fluoresce
- **SW (255nm) color:** Not typically fluorescent (uranyl minerals often fluoresce green/yellow, but tyuyamunite is weak)
- **MW (310nm) color:** Weak yellowish if any
- **LW (365nm) color:** Weak; some specimens show pale yellow response
- **Phosphorescent?** Not documented
- **Activator:** Weak intrinsic uranyl luminescence may be present but often quenched by V⁵⁺ and structural water disorder
- **Quenched by:** V⁵⁺ content, structural disorder, dehydration

### Flavor Text
> Tyuyamunite is the calcium twin of carnotite — same bright canary yellow, same uranyl vanadate bones, but with calcium where carnotite carries potassium. It is named for a cave in Kyrgyzstan where it was first found in 1912: Tyuya-Muyun, the "smoking mountain," where uranium and vanadium leached from oxidizing ore bodies and met calcium in the alkaline groundwater of the Fergana Valley. The mineral is so soft you can mark it with a fingernail, so hydrous it dehydrates to metatyuyamunite just sitting in dry air, and so radioactive it glows in a Geiger counter like a warning light. On the Colorado Plateau, it paints sandstone cliffs in yellow streaks — the color of oxidation, of uranium fleeing its reduced prison and marrying vanadium in the open air. It is not a mineral for collectors who prize permanence. It is a mineral that changes while you watch, losing water, shifting color toward green, recording the humidity of the room in its crystal structure. A mineral that breathes.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** V (vanadium) as trace element; humidity/atmospheric moisture tracker (for tyuyamunite ↔ metatyuyamunite equilibrium)
- **New events needed:** Uraninite oxidation event (releases U⁶⁺); vanadium mineral oxidation event (releases V⁵⁺); humidity fluctuation event (drives dehydration/rehydration)
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF scenario == "sandstone_hosted_UV" OR scenario == "oxidation_zone"
  AND trace_U > very_high AND trace_V > very_high AND trace_Ca > moderate
  AND pH > 6.0 AND pH < 8.5
  AND Eh > +0.4 AND O2 > very_high
  AND uraninite_present == oxidized
  AND humidity > moderate (favors tyuyamunite over metatyuyamunite)
  AND trace_K < trace_Ca (K dominance → carnotite instead)
  → nucleate tyuyamunite (max 2 nuclei, crust/habit)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF σ_tyuyamunite > 1.0 AND vug_fill < 1.0
  → grow at moderate rate
  habit = "crust" (default) | "platy" (if open vug) | "radiating" (if high supersaturation)
  IF humidity < low → convert to metatyuyamunite (same crystal, different hydration)
```
- **Habit selection logic:** Default crust/coating (sandstone-hosted habit). Open vug → platy crystals. High supersaturation → radiating rosettes.
- **Decomposition products:** Dehydration → metatyuyamunite + H₂O; dissolution → U⁶⁺ + V⁵⁺ + Ca²⁺ back to fluid; reducing conditions → uraninite or coffinite

### Variants for Game

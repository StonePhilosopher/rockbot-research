# Species: Apophyllite

## Identity
- **Formula:** (K,Na)Ca₄Si₈O₂₀(F,OH) · 8H₂O (fluorapophyllite-(K), the most common species)
- **Crystal system:** Tetragonal
- **Mineral group:** Phyllosilicate (layered silicate) — classified as a zeolite-associated mineral
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 4.5–5
- **Specific gravity:** 2.3–2.4
- **Cleavage:** Perfect on {001} (the basal pinacoid)
- **Fracture:** Uneven
- **Luster:** Vitreous to pearly on cleavage faces

## Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Colorless to white (most common); also green, yellow, pink, violet, brown
- **Color causes:** 
  - Green: trace Cu²⁺ substituting in the Ca site (most common colorant in Indian material)
  - Yellow: Fe³⁺ or other transition metal impurities
  - Pink/violet: Mn²⁺ traces
  - Brown: iron oxide inclusions or earthy impurities
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Prismatic or tabular crystals with pyramidal terminations; often exceptionally lustrous and gemmy; internal growth planes create rainbow iridescence in some specimens; anomalous birefringence produces patchy extinction under crossed polars; prism faces often fluorescent

## Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Prismatic to tabular tetragonal crystals with pyramidal terminations
- **Common forms/faces:** {100} prisms, {001} pinacoid (basal), {111} pyramidal terminations
- **Twin laws:** Rare; contact twins on {001} reported but uncommon
- **Varieties:**
  - Fluorapophyllite-(K) — the standard colorless-to-green form
  - Hydroxyapophyllite-(K) — white, colorless; hydroxyl-dominant end-member
  - Fluorapophyllite-(Na) — brown, yellow; natroapophyllite (Na replaces K)
  - "Bloody Apophyllite" — hematite-included variety from Nashik, India; phantoms visible inside transparent crystals
  - "Cinnamon Apophyllite" — thread-like hematite inclusions in tabular crystals, Nashik
- **Special morphologies:** Massive, granular; also occurs as thin platy crystals coating earlier zeolites; rarely as stalactitic forms

## Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 20–150°C (low-temperature zeolite facies to late hydrothermal)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 50–100°C (typical of late-stage zeolite-cavity environments)
- **Decomposition temperature:** >200°C (water loss begins, not immediately catastrophic but exfoliation occurs)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Lower temperatures favor more tabular, platy crystals; higher temperatures (still within low-T range) favor more prismatic forms

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Si (high), Ca (moderate), K (moderate to low), F or OH (anions)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Na (allows fluorapophyllite-(Na)), Cu (green color), Fe (yellow tint), Mn (pink tint)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Al can suppress apophyllite in favor of zeolites; high Mg favors chlorite/smectite over phyllosilicates
- **Required pH range:** 7.5–10 (alkaline, typical of zeolite-forming fluids)
- **Required Eh range:** Mildly reducing to oxidizing (not strongly redox-dependent; forms in fluid-filled cavities after primary minerals have stabilized)
- **Required O₂ range:** Low to moderate (cavity environment, not highly oxidizing)

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No significant release; growth consumes silica + Ca + K/F from fluid
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** May locally deplete Ca, allowing later calcite to nucleate at higher pH
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Releases H₂O vapor on heating (exfoliation); releases Ca²⁺, K⁺, SiO₂(aq), F⁻ into fluid if dissolved

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate to fast (faster than most zeolites, slower than quartz from saturated fluid)
- **Maximum crystal size in nature:** Up to 20+ cm in exceptional Deccan Traps specimens; typically 1–5 cm in vugs
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 0.5–3 cm
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Growth accelerates with temperature up to ~100°C, then slows as fluid chemistry shifts; retrograde behavior not pronounced
- **Competes with:** Zeolites (stilbite, heulandite, scolecite), prehnite, calcite, quartz — apophyllite typically wins late-stage cavity space because it crystallizes LAST

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes. Begins losing water at ~200°C; exfoliates (the name literally means "it flakes off"). Complete dehydration by ~400°C. Structure collapses.
- **Breaks down in light?** No (unlike realgar)
- **Dissolves in water?** Low solubility; slowly soluble in acidic water
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — HCl and other acids attack the silicate framework; exfoliates when treated with acid or simply rubbed (weaker hydrogen bonds between layers break)
- **Oxidizes?** Not directly, but included hematite may oxidize further
- **Dehydrates?** Yes — the defining characteristic. Loses 8H₂O on heating, flakes apart.
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Weakly radioactive due to ⁴⁰K (4.37% of K isotope is radioactive); some specimens fluoresce under UV

## Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Scolecite, stilbite, heulandite, mordenite, mesolite, natrolite (all zeolites); chalcedony; chlorite/smectite; prehnite; calcite (early generations)
- **Forms BEFORE:** Calcite (late generation), quartz (amethystine, if present), powellite
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Stilbite, heulandite, scolecite, prehnite, calcite, chalcedony, quartz, natrolite, mesolite, laumontite, chlorite, celadonite, hematite (as inclusions)
- **Zone:** Supergene/late hydrothermal — the terminal phase of basalt vesicle filling
- **Geological environment:** Vesicles in basalt and other volcanic rocks; also in granite pegmatites and skarns (rare). Most famous from Deccan Traps flood basalts (India) where it fills late-stage cavities after zeolite stages.

### Deccan Traps Paragenetic Sequence (Three Stages)
Based on Ottens et al. 2019 and field observations:

**Stage I — Near-surface cooling:**
- Clay minerals (celadonite, smectite)
- Early silica (chalcedony)

**Stage II — Burial metamorphism (subsequent lava flows):**
- Chlorite/smectite mixed layers
- Zeolite generation I: mordenite, heulandite, stilbite, scolecite, natrolite
- Chalcedony
- Zeolite generation II

**Stage III — Late hydrothermal:**
- Calcite (late generation)
- **Apophyllite** — LAST major phase
- Powellite
- Age gap: apophyllite formed 21–58 million years AFTER basalt eruption (65 Ma), spanning Paleogene to early Miocene

## Famous Localities
- **Jalgaon District, Nashik Division, Maharashtra, India:** The world's premier source. Enormous quantities of gemmy, well-formed crystals in all colors. Colorless, green, and "Bloody" varieties. Crystals to 20+ cm. Basalt quarries produce spectacular specimens on matrix with stilbite, heulandite, scolecite.
- **Pune (Poona) District, Maharashtra, India:** Classic historic locality; colorless to pale green prismatic crystals.
- **Nasik (Nashik) District, Maharashtra, India:** Source of "Cinnamon" and "Bloody" hematite-included varieties.
- **Ahmadnagar District, Maharashtra, India:** Exceptional green apophyllite crystals.
- **Berufjörður, Iceland:** Prismatic crystals in basalt with scolecite and other zeolites.
- **Kongsberg, Norway:** Colorless to white crystals in diabase.
- **Mont Saint-Hilaire, Quebec, Canada:** Associated with alkaline intrusive rocks; rare Na and unusual chemistry.
- **Harz Mountains, Germany:** Historical European locality.
- **Poona, India — notable specimens:** Single crystals to 10 cm, transparent and gemmy, on white stilbite matrix; these define the "classic" apophyllite aesthetic for collectors.

## Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Yes — many specimens fluoresce
- **SW (255nm) color:** Greenish-white to yellow-green (moderate to bright)
- **MW (310nm) color:** Yellow-green to white
- **LW (365nm) color:** Yellowish-white to pale green (often weaker than SW)
- **Phosphorescent?** Weakly in some specimens; short-lived after SW excitation
- **Activator:** Uranyl ion (UO₂²⁺) trace substitution is the most common cause of yellow-green fluorescence in apophyllite, as in many zeolite-associated minerals. Some green apophyllite may show TiO₆-activated fluorescence under UVC. Prism faces often fluoresce more brightly than basal faces due to different surface chemistry.
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺ inclusions can locally suppress fluorescence; hematite inclusions create dark non-fluorescent zones in "Bloody" specimens.

> Apophyllite is the last guest at the party. After the zeolites have had their run, after calcite and chalcedony have filled most of the cavity, the remaining alkaline fluid — still rich in silica, calcium, and potassium — finds a corner of the vug that no one else wanted. There it crystallizes into prisms of extraordinary clarity, stacked like tiny glass cathedrals. The name means "it flakes off" in Greek: heat it, rub it, or breathe acid on it, and the layers separate like pages from a wet book. Some carry ghosts — hematite phantoms from an earlier epoch, frozen in transparent calcium silicate. In the Deccan Traps, apophyllite arrived 21 million years after the lava cooled, making it the youngest mineral in cavities older than the dinosaurs that once walked above them.

## Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Clear Cathedral** — Colorless, transparent, prismatic crystals with pyramidal terminations. Forms in open vug space with clean fluid. High clarity, gemmy appearance. Conditions: low Cu, moderate-high Si, open cavity.
- **Variant 2: Green Deccan** — Green to yellow-green tabular crystals, often on white stilbite matrix. Classic Indian material. Conditions: Cu trace present, moderate temperature (50–80°C), late-stage nucleation after zeolites.
- **Variant 3: Bloody Phantom** — Colorless apophyllite with internal hematite phantoms — dark red-brown ghosts of earlier iron oxide precipitation. Requires: prior `event_hematite_inclusion` in same vug + apophyllite nucleation afterward. Visually striking; rare.

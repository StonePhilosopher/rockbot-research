# Mineral Species — Howlite

## Species: Howlite

### Identity
- **Formula:** Ca₂B₅SiO₉(OH)₅
- **Crystal system:** Monoclinic
- **Mineral group:** Borate (ino-nesoborate hybrid)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 3.5
- **Specific gravity:** 2.53–2.59
- **Cleavage:** None
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal to uneven
- **Luster:** Sub-vitreous to glimmering, earthy when porous

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** White, colorless; greyish-white with grey to black web-like veining
- **Color causes:** Pure howlite is white or colorless; the grey/black veining is due to manganese oxide or carbonaceous inclusions infiltrating along fractures and pore networks. Some specimens show pale brown or yellowish staining from iron oxides.
- **Transparency:** Translucent to opaque (nodules opaque; rare crystals translucent to transparent)
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Porous, cauliflower-like nodules with erratic web-like veining in grey or black. The veining pattern is superficially similar to turquoise spiderweb matrix, which is why howlite is so commonly dyed to imitate it. Crystals are exceptionally rare — found at only two localities worldwide — and show prismatic habit flattened on {100}.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Massive to nodular — irregular white nodules, often cauliflower-like or brain-like in form. Crystals are rare tabular prisms.
- **Common forms/faces:** {100} dominant flattening, {010}, {001} (crystals only); massive forms show no crystal faces
- **Twin laws:** None reported
- **Varieties:**
  - *Veined howlite* — the standard form, white with grey/black dendritic veining
  - *Crystal howlite* — rare prismatic crystals, known only from Tick Canyon, California and Iona, Nova Scotia
  - *Dyed howlite* — not a natural variety, but ubiquitous in trade as "turquenite" or fake turquoise
- **Special morphologies:** Nodular/botryoidal masses, porous aggregates, fracture fillings in gypsum. Nodules can reach 10+ cm.

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 20–80°C (low-temperature evaporite/hydrothermal brine)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 40–60°C
- **Decomposition temperature:** Dehydroxylates in two stages starting ~250–350°C (loses structural OH as water)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Lower T (<40°C) → more porous, finely veined nodules; higher T (60–80°C) → denser, more compact masses

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Ca²⁺ (high, >500 ppm), B (as borate, >300 ppm), Si (moderate, >100 ppm)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Higher Si encourages howlite over simpler borates; trace Mn contributes to dark veining
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Na⁺ (→ borax or ulexite instead); very high Mg²⁺ (→ competing magnesium borates); high SO₄²⁻ (→ gypsum/anhydrite competes for Ca)
- **Required pH range:** 8.0–10.5 (alkaline — borate stability and Ca solubility require elevated pH)
- **Required Eh range:** Not strongly redox-sensitive
- **Required O₂ range:** Not critical — forms in both oxic and anoxic brines

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Consumes Ca²⁺, borate, and dissolved silica from brine; releases H⁺ (slightly lowers pH as borate polymerizes)
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** None significant
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Dissolves in HCl with gel formation; releases borate ions back to solution

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Slow to moderate — borosilicate framework requires precise stoichiometry; much slower than simple borates like borax
- **Maximum crystal size:** Nodules to 10+ cm; crystals rare, to ~1 cm
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** Nodules 1–5 cm; fills cavities and fractures as irregular masses
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Growth accelerates with temperature up to ~80°C, but structural OH becomes unstable above ~100°C
- **Competes with:** Ulexite (if Na present), colemanite (if Ca>>Na), gypsum (competes for Ca), calcite

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes — dehydroxylates starting ~250°C; complete structural breakdown by ~600°C
- **Breaks down in light?** No (photostable)
- **Dissolves in water?** Very slightly soluble; much less soluble than borax or ulexite
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — soluble in HCl with characteristic gel formation (the silica content produces a gelatinous residue)
- **Oxidizes?** No — boron and silicon already in oxidized states
- **Dehydrates?** Partial — loses some structural OH at elevated temperature, but more stable than borax; does NOT effloresce in dry air like borax does
- **Radiation sensitivity:** No documented radiation effects

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Gypsum, anhydrite (howlite commonly replaces or fills cavities in gypsum deposits); borax (earlier, more Na-rich stage)
- **Forms BEFORE:** Colemanite (later Ca-dominant stage), calcite (late-stage carbonate)
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Gypsum, anhydrite, colemanite, ulexite, borax, calcite, halite, thenardite. Found in borate evaporite deposits and as a late-stage hydrothermal mineral in arid environments.
- **Zone:** Supergene/evaporite — arid-region evaporite deposits, borate lakes, and hydrothermal alteration of volcanic ash in alkaline brines
- **Geological environment:** Evaporite deposits in arid basins; alkaline lake sediments; hydrothermal borate deposits; alteration of volcanic tuff by boron-rich fluids. The type locality at Windsor, Nova Scotia formed in anhydrite/gypsum deposits of the Windsor Group (evaporites of the Windsor Sea, Carboniferous).

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1:** Windsor, Nova Scotia, Canada — type locality, discovered 1868 by Henry How in a gypsum quarry. White nodules in anhydrite/gypsum, associated with the Carboniferous Windsor Sea evaporites.
- **Classic locality 2:** Tick Canyon, Sierra Pelona Mountains, Los Angeles County, California — first reported crystals (elongated on [010]), found in borate deposits. Sterling Borax Mine area.
- **Classic locality 3:** Iona, Victoria County, Nova Scotia — second crystal locality, crystals elongated on [001], exposed by dissolution of anhydrite by Bras d'Or Lakes water. Well-exposed crystal clusters on anhydrite matrix.
- **Other notable:** Lang, Los Angeles County, California; various borate deposits in Turkey, Mexico, Russia, Germany; Death Valley region (minor)

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** No — or very weak if any
- **SW (255nm) color:** None reported
- **MW (310nm) color:** None reported
- **LW (365nm) color:** None reported
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** None
- **Quenched by:** N/A

### Flavor Text

> Howlite is the mineral that learned to wear another mineral's face. It was discovered in 1868 when Nova Scotia gypsum miners complained to Henry How about a white, veined nuisance clogging their quarry — he named it silico-boro-calcite, and it was later renamed howlite in his honor. For a century it was a curiosity: soft, white, porous, with a web of grey veins running through it like cracks in dried mud. Then someone realized you could drop it in blue dye and sell it as turquoise. Now howlite is probably better-known as "the fake" than as itself — which is a shame, because the real mineral has its own quiet strangeness. It forms in evaporite basins where calcium, boron, and silica meet in alkaline brine, growing into cauliflower-like nodules that replace or infill gypsum beds. Its porosity is extreme — it drinks dye like a sponge, but it also preserves the chemistry of the brine that made it, locked in those tiny channels. The rare crystals, found only at Tick Canyon and Iona, are prismatic and colorless, a completely different creature from the veined nodules. Howlite doesn't fluoresce, it doesn't sparkle, it isn't rare enough to be expensive or common enough to be ignored. It just sits there, white and veined, waiting to be mistaken for something else. There's something honest in that. Not every mineral wants to be famous.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** `trace_B` (boron) already in trace list; `trace_Si` already tracked; howlite needs the Ca-B-Si ternary system
- **New events needed:** `event_borate_brine` — alkaline, Ca+Si-rich borate fluid entering the vug system; `event_gypsum_dissolution` — howlite commonly replaces gypsum/anhydrite, so a gypsum-dissolving event could release Ca and create porosity for howlite precipitation
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF scenario == "evaporite_basin" OR scenario == "alkaline_brine"
  AND trace_Ca > 500 ppm
  AND trace_B > 300 ppm
  AND trace_Si > 100 ppm
  AND pH > 8.0
  AND temperature > 20 AND temperature < 80
  AND (gypsum_present == true OR anhydrite_dissolving == true)
  → nucleate howlite (nodular habit, max 2 nuclei)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF σ_howlite > 1.0
  → grow at rate 3 (slow — borosilicate framework)
  habit = "nodular" (default) | "veined" (if trace_Mn > 50 ppm)
  IF crystal_rare_conditions == true
    → "prismatic" habit (rare variant)
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - Default: nodular/ cauliflower masses
  - If trace_Mn > 50 ppm: grey/black veining pattern (decorative)
  - Very rare: prismatic crystals (only if slow growth in open cavity with ideal chemistry)
- **Decomposition products:**
  - ~250–350°C → dehydroxylated howlite + H₂O
  - >600°C → amorphous calcium borosilicate residue
  - HCl dissolution → gel (silica gel formation)

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Veined Howlite** — White nodular masses with grey/black web veining. Conditions: standard evaporite, trace Mn present. The common collector form.
- **Variant 2: Dense Howlite** — Solid white nodules, little veining. Conditions: higher temperature (60–80°C), slower growth, lower Mn. Preferred for carving and dyeing.
- **Variant 3: Crystal Howlite** — Rare prismatic monoclinic crystals, colorless to white, translucent. Conditions: very slow growth in open cavity, ideal Ca:B:Si ratio, no Mn. Only appears in "exceptional specimen" tier.

### Game Design Notes
Howlite is unusual among Vugg Simulator minerals because its real-world significance is partly cultural — it's the most commonly dyed mineral on the market, used to imitate turquoise, lapis, coral, and others. This could be reflected in a "trade mechanic" where howlite has low base value but can be "treated" (dyed) to imitate higher-value minerals — a wink at real mineral commerce. The porosity mechanic could also be unique: howlite absorbs fluid inclusions more readily than other minerals, making it a "sponge" for late-stage brine chemistry.

The rarity of crystals vs. abundance of nodules makes howlite a good "patience" mineral — most specimens will be the nodular form, but exceptional conditions can yield the rare crystal habit, rewarding players who maintain stable chemistry over long simulation runs.

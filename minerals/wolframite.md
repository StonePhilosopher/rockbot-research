## Species: Wolframite

### Identity
- **Formula:** (Fe,Mn)WO₄ — iron manganese tungstate
- **Crystal system:** Monoclinic (prismatic class 2/m)
- **Space group:** P2₁/c
- **Mineral group:** Tungstate (oxide subclass)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 4–4.5 (some sources cite up to 5.5 for iron-rich specimens)
- **Specific gravity:** 7.0–7.5 (remarkably heavy — among the densest non-metallic-looking minerals)
- **Cleavage:** Perfect {010} — one direction parallel to a and c axes
- **Fracture:** Uneven to rough
- **Luster:** Submetallic to resinous
- **Streak:** Reddish brown to brownish black
- **Unit cell:** a = 4.77 Å, b = 5.73 Å, c = 4.98 Å; β = 90.2°; Z = 2

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Dark gray to brownish-black to iron-black; iron-rich specimens darker
- **Color causes:** Fe²⁺ content darkens the mineral; Mn²⁺-rich specimens trend toward brownish-red hues
- **Transparency:** Translucent to opaque
- **Streak:** Brown to reddish brown
- **Notable visual features:** Crystals often striated lengthwise; submetallic sheen that catches light like gunmetal; some specimens weakly magnetic when iron-rich

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Tabular to short prismatic crystals, elongated along the c axis, flattened in the a axis direction
- **Common forms/faces:** Heavily modified tabular crystals with prominent basal faces
- **Twin laws:** Repeated twins common, producing V-shaped notches in the center of crystal groups
- **Varieties:**
  - **Ferberite** — Fe-dominant end member (Fe:Mn ≥ 4:1), darker, harder, slightly more magnetic
  - **Hübnerite** — Mn-dominant end member (Mn:Fe ≥ 4:1), reddish-brown, slightly less dense
  - Intermediate compositions (20–80% range of series) = true "wolframite"
- **Special morphologies:**
  - Bladed clusters and rosettes
  - Columnar aggregates
  - Reticulated networks
  - Lamellar masses
  - Granular and massive forms
  - Acicular (needle-like) in rare cases
  - Dense encrusting on vein walls

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 250–400°C (high-temperature hydrothermal)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 300–380°C (most productive vein-type deposits)
- **Decomposition temperature:** >1473°C (melting point of WO₃ component); wolframite itself stable to very high temperatures
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - Higher temperatures (350–400°C) favor ferberite (Fe-rich) precipitation
  - Lower temperatures (250–300°C) favor hübnerite (Mn-rich) precipitation
  - Mn/Fe ratio in fluid controls zonation — deeper/earlier = more Fe-rich; shallower/later = more Mn-rich

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:**
  - W (tungsten): minimum ~100 ppm (solubility at 300°C, pH 4–6 reaches hundreds of ppm)
  - Fe OR Mn: minimum ~50 ppm (either can dominate; both present = intermediate wolframite)
  - NaCl: 5–15 wt% equivalent salinity enhances solubility
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - CO₂: enhances W transport in solution; CO₂ degassing triggers precipitation
  - Sn: commonly associated but not required
  - F: enhances fluid mobility (greisen association)
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - Ca: competes for W to form scheelite (CaWO₄) — wolframite and scheelite are mutually exclusive in most deposits
  - High sulfur: promotes sulfide precipitation, competing for Fe
  - Very low pH (<3): keeps W in solution as tungstic acid, prevents precipitation
- **Required pH range:** 4–6 (weakly acidic to neutral)
- **Required Eh range:** Moderately reducing to near-neutral (W⁶⁺ stable)
- **Required O₂ range:** Low — sub-oxidizing; too oxidizing converts Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺ (hematite/goethite)

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No significant acid or gas release during wolframite precipitation
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Minor H⁺ consumption (alkalinization of local fluid)
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Tungstic acid (H₂WO₄) or tungstate ions (WO₄²⁻) in oxidizing, low-pH conditions; may form secondary tungstates (Fe/Mn hydroxides with adsorbed W)

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Slow to moderate (much slower than quartz, calcite, or fluorite)
- **Maximum crystal size:** Up to 10+ cm in exceptional specimens (Panasqueira, Portugal)
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 2–20 mm; rarely up to 5 cm in best pockets
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — growth rate decreases with falling temperature; retrograde solubility means wolframite precipitates as fluid cools and/or pH shifts
- **Competes with:**
  - Scheelite (for W — pH and Ca availability determine which wins)
  - Quartz (for space in vein; wolframite often nucleates on quartz or forms earlier)
  - Cassiterite (for Sn if present)
  - Arsenopyrite, pyrite (for Fe)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Stable to extreme temperatures (>1400°C); no thermal decomposition in geological conditions
- **Breaks down in light?** No — not light-sensitive
- **Dissolves in water?** Essentially insoluble in neutral water; solubility increases in alkaline solutions
- **Dissolves in acid?** Slowly in strong acids; attacked by aqua regia and hot concentrated alkalis
- **Oxidizes?** Surface alteration to tungstite (WO₃·H₂O) and Fe/Mn hydroxides in supergene conditions
- **Dehydrates?** No — anhydrous mineral
- **Radiation sensitivity:** No significant effect

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Early quartz vein opening; some wolframite nucleates directly on vein walls before massive quartz
- **Forms BEFORE:** Late-stage sulfides (pyrite, chalcopyrite, galena, sphalerite), carbonate, scheelite (if Ca becomes available)
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - Quartz (ubiquitous gangue)
  - Cassiterite (Sn-W granite association)
  - Arsenopyrite, pyrite, chalcopyrite, galena, sphalerite
  - Fluorite, apatite, siderite, tourmaline (greisen assemblage)
  - Muscovite, topaz (alteration minerals)
  - Scheelite (in Ca-bearing wallrocks)
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene — high-temperature hydrothermal veins, greisens, skarns
- **Geological environment:**
  - Granitic pegmatites
  - Greisen-bordered quartz veins
  - High-temperature hydrothermal quartz veins (mesothermal to hypothermal)
  - Skarns (contact metamorphic)
  - Rarely in alluvial placers (as resistant heavy mineral)

### Famous Localities
- **Panasqueira, Portugal:** The world's premier wolframite locality. Active since 1896, producing world-class euhedral ferberite crystals to 10 cm, often with quartz, fluorapatite, siderite, and cassiterite. Subhorizontal Sn-W quartz veins in Hercynian phyllites.
- **Nanling Range, China:** China's largest W province; Yaogangxian and Taoxikeng mines produce exceptional wolframite-quartz vein specimens. China produces ~60% of world tungsten.
- **Cornwall, England, UK:** Historic tin-tungsten district; Carrock Fell, Cligga Head, and other mines produced fine wolframite associated with cassiterite and tourmaline.
- **Other notable localities:** Colorado and South Dakota (USA); Rwanda; Bolivia; Korea; Russia (Transbaikalia); New South Wales (Australia); Myanmar (Mawchi mine)
- **Notable specimens:**
  - Panasqueira ferberite: perfect bladed crystals with glassy quartz, often showing V-twin notches
  - Yaogangxian: wolframite perched on terminated quartz crystals — classic display specimens
  - Record size: crystals to 15 cm reported from some Chinese localities

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** No — wolframite itself is not fluorescent
- **SW (255nm) color:** None
- **MW (310nm) color:** None
- **LW (365nm) color:** None
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** N/A
- **Quenched by:** N/A
- **Note:** Associated scheelite (if present) fluoresces bright blue-white under SW UV — this is often used in field exploration ("black light" prospecting for tungsten). Wolframite deposits may have scheelite rims or alteration halos that fluoresce.

### Flavor Text

Wolframite is the wolf that devours tin — or so the old miners believed, naming it from the German *wolf rahm*, "wolf's froth," for the way it seemed to consume tin during smelting. In truth it is a solid solution, a treaty between iron and manganese over who gets to hold the tungsten. It forms in the dark, hot veins where granite bleeds its last riches into the Earth: 300 degrees, a slow crystallization, tabular crystals growing along the c axis like compressed shadows. The Panasqueira mines have pulled wolframite from Portuguese earth for over a century — bladed, striated, impossibly dense at 7.5 g/cm³, heavy as a secret in your palm. It carries no fluorescence, no flash, no color but the muted gunmetal of deep time. What it carries instead is weight — physical, historical, strategic. This was the mineral that hardened armor-piercing shells in two world wars, that lit the filaments of the first electric light bulbs, that makes the carbide tools with which we cut everything else. Wolframite does not announce itself. It waits in the vein, darker than quartz, heavier than it has any right to be, and when you lift it you understand that some stones earn their gravity.

```
   → nucleate wolframite
   → habit = tabular_prismatic (default) OR bladed_cluster (if slow growth)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF temperature > 200°C AND temperature < 400°C
   AND W > 80 ppm
   AND (Fe > 40 ppm OR Mn > 40 ppm)
   AND pH >= 4 AND pH <= 7
   → grow at rate SLOW (0.3× quartz baseline)
   → Fe/Mn ratio in crystal = Fe/Mn ratio in broth
   → if Fe:Mn >= 4:1, label = ferberite
   → if Mn:Fe >= 4:1, label = hübnerite
   → else label = wolframite
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - Tabular prismatic: default, moderate supersaturation
  - Bladed cluster: low supersaturation + long growth time
  - V-twinned: random chance (15%) when multiple nuclei near each other
  - Granular/massive: very high nucleation density, limited space
- **Decomposition products:** None in-game (stable to extreme T); if oxidation mechanics added: tungstite + Fe/Mn hydroxides

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Ferberite** — Fe-dominant (>80% Fe), darker gunmetal-black, slightly harder (H~5.5), weakly magnetic. Forms at higher temperatures (350–400°C). Label shows "Ferberite" in crystal detail.
- **Variant 2: Hübnerite** — Mn-dominant (>80% Mn), reddish-brown to chestnut, slightly less dense. Forms at lower temperatures (250–320°C). More common in late-stage veins.
- **Variant 3: Panasqueira-style** — Exceptional habit: large bladed crystals with perfect terminations, often perched on quartz. Requires very slow growth (low supersaturation sustained for many steps) + open vug space. Rare spawn condition.

## Species: Hiddenite

### Identity
- **Formula:** LiAlSi₂O₆ (lithium aluminium inosilicate — same as spodumene base)
- **Crystal system:** Monoclinic (α-spodumene, low-T form)
- **Mineral group:** Inosilicate, Pyroxene group (clinopyroxene subgroup)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 6.5–7
- **Specific gravity:** 3.18–3.23
- **Cleavage:** Perfect prismatic, two directions {110} ∧ {110} at 87° (near 90°)
- **Fracture:** Uneven to subconchoidal
- **Luster:** Vitreous, pearly on cleavage surfaces
- **Streak:** White
- **Tenacity:** Brittle

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Pale yellowish-green to deep emerald green; classic hiddenite is emerald-green
- **Color causes:** Cr³⁺ (trivalent chromium) substituting for Al³⁺ in the octahedral site of the spodumene structure. Cr³⁺ produces the characteristic emerald-green color via spin-allowed d-d transitions in the octahedral crystal field. V³⁺ may contribute in some specimens.
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent (gem varieties)
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:**
  - **Strong pleochroism:** Deep emerald green along the c-axis, yellowish-green to colorless perpendicular to it. The color difference is dramatic and diagnostic.
  - **Color stability:** Unlike kunzite, hiddenite does NOT tenebresce. The Cr³⁺ color center is photostable. It does not fade in sunlight.
  - **Luster:** Strong vitreous luster; well-cut stones have a glass-like brilliance.
  - **Striations:** Dense vertical striations parallel to {100} on prism faces (same as all spodumene).
  - **Bicolor specimens:** Some crystals show yellow one end and green the other (Pala, California) — a Mn→Cr gradient in the same crystal.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Prismatic, elongated along [001] (c-axis), often flattened on {100}
- **Common forms/faces:** {100}, {110}, {010} — striated prisms with steep pyramidal or pinacoidal terminations
- **Twin laws:** Common on {100} — simple contact twins, sometimes repeated producing fishtail or butterfly forms (same as kunzite and colorless spodumene)
- **Varieties:**
  - **True hiddenite:** Emerald-green, Cr-colored, from North Carolina or other verified Cr-localities
  - **Green spodumene (non-hiddenite):** Pale green, Fe-colored (not Cr), from Brazil, Afghanistan, Madagascar. Most "green spodumene" on the market is NOT true hiddenite.
  - **Bicolor:** Pink (kunzite) + green (hiddenite) in same crystal — extremely rare, Pala district only
- **Special morphologies:**
  - **Alpine cleft crystals:** Small, well-formed prisms in fractures (North Carolina type)
  - **Pegmatite pocket crystals:** Larger, more blocky forms in LCT environments

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 300–650°C (broader than kunzite; includes alpine cleft and pegmatite environments)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 450–600°C for gem-quality crystals
- **Decomposition temperature:** α→β spodumene inversion at ~900°C (structural change)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - Alpine cleft (<450°C): smaller, more euhedral, higher clarity
  - Pegmatite pocket (450–650°C): larger, sometimes more blocky

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:**
  - Li: >100 ppm (essential — defines spodumene)
  - Al: present (structural)
  - Si: present (structural)
  - Cr: >1 ppm (essential for true hiddenite color)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - V: trace — may contribute to green color in some specimens
  - Fe: low (Fe-colored spodumene is pale yellow-green, not emerald)
  - Mn: trace — if present, may produce bicolor or compete with Cr color
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - High Mn (favors kunzite color instead)
  - High Fe (creates pale, muddy green, not true hiddenite)
- **Required pH range:** Mildly acidic to neutral (pH 5.0–7.5)
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing to mildly oxidizing (Cr must remain Cr³⁺; oxidizing conditions could produce Cr⁶⁺ which is not a chromophore)
- **Required O₂ range:** Low to moderate

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Same as all spodumene — enriches residual fluid in Cs, Rb, Ta
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Late-stage fluid enrichment
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Alteration to cookeite + quartz + Li-rich fluid

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate (same as spodumene baseline)
- **Maximum crystal size:** 68 mm tall recorded before 1892 (Kunz); modern crystals to ~10 cm from North Carolina
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 5–30 mm
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — same retrograde behavior as all spodumene
- **Competes with:** Kunzite for the spodumene structure (Mn vs Cr chromophore competition); emerald for Cr in some environments

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Crystal structure inverts to β-spodumene at ~900°C; color is stable to much higher temperatures than kunzite (Cr³⁺ is thermally stable)
- **Breaks down in light?** NO — hiddenite is photostable. Unlike kunzite, it does not fade in sunlight. This is a key distinguishing property.
- **Dissolves in water?** Insoluble
- **Dissolves in acid?** Slowly attacked by hot concentrated acids
- **Oxidizes?** No — Li⁺, Al³⁺, Si⁴⁺, Cr³⁺ are all stable
- **Dehydrates?** N/A — anhydrous
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Generally stable; Cr³⁺ does not show color-center instability like Mn³⁺

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Early pegmatite quartz, K-feldspar, albite, muscovite; in alpine clefts, after wall-rock metamorphism
- **Forms BEFORE:** Late hydrothermal lepidolite, cookeite, eucryptite
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - **North Carolina alpine type:** Emerald, quartz, chromian dravite (Cr-bearing tourmaline), pyrite, calcite
  - **LCT pegmatite type:** Quartz, albite, K-feldspar, lepidolite, beryl, tourmaline, columbite-tantalite
  - **Key point:** True hiddenite (Cr-green) is associated with other Cr-bearing minerals — emerald, chromian dravite, chromite — confirming the Cr source
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene (two distinct environments: alpine clefts and LCT pegmatites)
- **Geological environment:**
  1. **Alpine clefts (North Carolina type):** Fractures in migmatitic schists and gneisses, associated with emerald-bearing quartz veins. Cr sourced from ultramafic wall rock or chromite alteration.
  2. **LCT pegmatites:** Fractionated granitic pegmatites with Cr introduced from wall rock or early chromite. Much rarer than kunzite in this setting.

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1: Hiddenite, Alexander County, North Carolina, USA** — The type locality. Discovered 1879 by A.E. Hidden, who was commissioned by Thomas Edison to find platinum (unsuccessful). The green crystals were initially thought to be diopside. J. Lawrence Smith correctly identified them as spodumene and named the variety "hiddenite" in honor of Hidden. The community was later renamed Hiddenite. During the 1880s–1890s heyday, also known as "lithia emerald." The North American Emerald Mine and Rist/Ellis Tracts still produce hiddenite today. The geology: Precambrian migmatitic schists cross-cut by steeply-dipping quartz veins; emerald and hiddenite in altered wall rock.
- **Classic locality 2: Aracuaí, Minas Gerais, Brazil** — Green spodumene, though much of it is Fe-colored (not true Cr-hiddenite). Produces large crystals.
- **Classic locality 3: Madagascar** — Green spodumene reported; status as true hiddenite debated.
- **Notable specimens:**
  - Finest crystal before 1892: 68 mm tall, could have yielded a 5.5-ct gem (Kunz, 1892)
  - 2-ct hiddenite in Augustus C. Hamlin collection — considered among the finest large stones of the era
  - A.E. Hidden's original specimens — in Smithsonian and private collections
  - 5.14-ct hiddenite from North Carolina — classic emerald-green color, verified Cr content

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally NO under standard UV lamps
- **SW (255nm) color:** None typically
- **MW (310nm) color:** None typically
- **LW (365nm) color:** None typically
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** N/A for visible UV fluorescence
- **Note:** Cr³⁺ produces R-line luminescence in spectroscopy (sharp emission lines at ~693 nm) but this is NOT visible fluorescence under standard UV lamps. The lack of visible fluorescence distinguishes hiddenite from Mn-bearing kunzite.
- **Quenched by:** N/A

### Flavor Text

> **The Green That Shouldn't Exist**
>
> Hiddenite is the emerald-green variety of spodumene, and it should not exist. Spodumene is a lithium aluminum pyroxene; its structure has an octahedral site that prefers Al. Chromium — the element that colors emerald, that demands a beryl structure with its larger site — somehow wedges itself into spodumene's tighter lattice and turns it green. Not pale green. Emerald green. The color of forests in stone.
>
> It was found in 1879 in Alexander County, North Carolina, by a man named Hidden — A.E. Hidden, a mining agent who had been sent by Thomas Edison to find platinum. He didn't find platinum. He found something stranger: green crystals in quartz veins, initially mistaken for diopside. J. Lawrence Smith, a chemist in Louisville, identified them correctly as a new variety of spodumene and named them for the man who brought them to him. The town became Hiddenite. The mineral became legend.
>
> True hiddenite requires chromium. Most green spodumene does not have Cr; it is Fe-colored, yellowish, weak — the difference between a whisper and a shout. True hiddenite, deep green, is rarer than emerald and far less known. The North American Emerald Mine still produces it, deep in the same fractures that hold emerald, quartz, and chromian tourmaline. And unlike kunzite, its pink sister, hiddenite does not fade. It is stable in light, stable in time, stable in memory.
>
> In the vugg, hiddenite would be the rarest of the spodumene variants: a Cr-colored miracle, appearing only when chromium and lithium meet in the same fractionated fluid, at the same moment, in the same pocket. The odds are low. The color, when it happens, is unforgettable. It does not ask to be hidden away. It simply is green, defiantly, in a mineral that has no right to be.

## Paragenetic Context

Hiddenite is part of the **LCT pegmatite spodumene color-variety system**, sharing the LiAlSi₂O₆ framework with kunzite (Mn-pink) and colorless spodumene. The three are mutually exclusive in color: a given pocket produces one chromophore dominance.

| Variety | Chromophore | Required Condition | Relative Rarity |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Colorless spodumene | None | Low chromophores | Common |
| Kunzite | Mn³⁺ | Mn > 1 ppm, Fe/Mn < 1 | Uncommon |
| Hiddenite | Cr³⁺ | Cr > 1 ppm, Mn < Cr | Rare |

**Two geological models for hiddenite:**
1. **Alpine cleft (North Carolina):** Cr from ultramafic wall rock or chromite alteration; lower T (300–450°C); associated with emerald, quartz, chromian dravite
2. **LCT pegmatite:** Cr introduced by wall-rock interaction or early chromite; higher T (450–650°C); associated with beryl, tourmaline, lepidolite

**Key distinguishing property — Photostability:**
Unlike kunzite, hiddenite does NOT tenebresce. This is a gameplay-relevant difference: kunzite fades in light events; hiddenite persists. In a simulator with light/dark cycles or UV events, this creates meaningful differentiation between the two varieties.

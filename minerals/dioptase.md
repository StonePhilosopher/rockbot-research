# Research: Dioptase — Cu₆Si₆O₁₈·6H₂O

## Species: Dioptase

### Identity
- **Formula:** Cu₆Si₆O₁₈·6H₂O (also written CuSiO₂(OH)₂, the empirical formula)
- **Crystal system:** Trigonal (rhombohedral)
- **Mineral group:** Cyclosilicate
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 5
- **Specific gravity:** 3.28–3.35
- **Cleavage:** Perfect in three directions (rhombohedral)
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal
- **Luster:** Vitreous to sub-adamantine

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Intense emerald-green to bluish-green
- **Color causes:** Jahn-Teller distorted Cu²⁺ in octahedral coordination — the d⁹ electron configuration creates the characteristic emerald-green absorption. Broad visible absorption band at ~752 nm.
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent
- **Streak:** Green
- **Notable visual features:** Internal cleavage planes visible through unbroken crystals (gave the mineral its name: Greek *dia* "through" + *optos* "visible"). Crystals are very fragile and must be handled with care.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Six-sided prisms terminated by rhombohedrons
- **Common forms/faces:** Rhombohedral crystals, often elongated; modified rhombohedrons common
- **Twin laws:** Not commonly reported
- **Varieties:** None formally recognized, but color intensity varies by locality (Kazakhstan specimens are often darker teal, Tsumeb specimens are brighter emerald-green)
- **Special morphologies:** Massive forms are uncommon; typically found as well-formed individual crystals or drusy coatings on quartz. Microcrystalline crusts in some localities.

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 20–80°C (low-temperature supergene)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 40–60°C
- **Decomposition temperature:** Not applicable under Earth-surface conditions; stable to ~200°C, begins dehydrating above 300°C
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Higher temperatures (closer to 80°C) favor larger, more euhedral crystals; lower temperatures favor microcrystalline crusts

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:**
  - Cu: >50 ppm (highly soluble in oxidizing acid conditions)
  - SiO₂: >30 ppm (requires alkaline pH to mobilize silica)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - Ca: buffers acid, maintains alkaline pH, promotes silica solubility
  - Carbonate (CO₃²⁻): buffers acid generated by sulfide oxidation, creating the alkaline conditions silica needs
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - High Fe: competes for Si in solution, favors goethite/hematite
  - High Al: may form clay minerals instead
  - Strongly acidic pH (<4): suppresses silica solubility entirely
  - Very high SO₄²⁻: maintains acidity, prevents silica mobilization
- **Required pH range:** 8.5–10.5 (highly alkaline — this is the key constraint)
- **Required Eh range:** Strongly oxidizing (+0.5 to +0.8 V) — Cu must be in Cu²⁺ state
- **Required O₂ range:** High — needs open-air or well-oxygenated groundwater

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No significant byproducts
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Consumes Cu²⁺ and SiO₂ from solution; releases H⁺ slightly (minor acidification)
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Dissolves in acid to release Cu²⁺ (toxic), silica, and water

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Slow to moderate — slower than malachite/azurite, comparable to chrysocolla
- **Maximum crystal size:** Up to 9 cm reported (Kostov & Kostov 1999 claim), but typical crystals are <2 cm; most fine specimens are 0.5–1.5 cm
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–10 mm crystals in drusy coatings; exceptional pockets produce 1–3 cm crystals
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — growth accelerates with temperature up to ~60°C, then slows as silica solubility drops
- **Competes with:** Chrysocolla (same elements, amorphous/microcrystalline), plancheite (similar Cu-silicate), malachite (for Cu), quartz (for Si)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Begins dehydrating above ~300°C; complete structural breakdown by 500°C
- **Breaks down in light?** No — color stable in light (unlike realgar)
- **Dissolves in water?** Very slightly; solubility increases with acidity
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — dissolves readily in HCl and H₂SO₄, releasing toxic Cu²⁺
- **Oxidizes?** No — Cu is already in +2 oxidation state, fully oxidized
- **Dehydrates?** Yes, above 300°C; transforms to amorphous material
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Not particularly radiation-sensitive; color stable under UV

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Primary copper sulfides (chalcopyrite, chalcocite, bornite) must oxidize first to release Cu²⁺; silica must be mobilized (requires alkaline pH, typically from carbonate buffering)
- **Forms BEFORE:** In arid climates, may persist indefinitely; in wet climates, may eventually alter to chrysocolla or be replaced by malachite
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Chrysocolla, malachite, azurite, calcite, quartz, cerussite, shattuckite, plancheite, willemite (some localities)
- **Zone:** Supergene/oxidation zone — specifically the alkaline, silica-rich microenvironments within the broader oxidation zone
- **Geological environment:** Desert/arid-region copper deposits where evaporation concentrates solutions and carbonate host rocks buffer acidity. Famous localities are all in arid/semi-arid climates (Kazakhstan, Namibia, Arizona, Congo).

### Famous Localities
- **Altyn-Tyube mine, Kazakhstan:** Type locality (1797). Discovered by copper miners who thought they'd found emerald. Brownish quartzite host distinguishes these specimens. Still producing handsome material.
- **Tsumeb Mine, Namibia:** The finest transparent crystals — the most sought-after by collectors. Crystals up to 2+ cm, often on white dolomite matrix. The epitome of dioptase quality.
- **Mammoth-Saint Anthony Mine, Arizona:** Small crystals, fine micromount specimens. Classic US locality.
- **Rénéville, Congo-Brazzaville:** Interesting locality where dioptase appears to be primary (crystallized with quartz, native copper, and malachite), suggesting unusual formation conditions.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally no — non-fluorescent in UV
- **SW (255nm) color:** None
- **MW (310nm) color:** None
- **LW (365nm) color:** None
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** N/A
- **Quenched by:** N/A

- **New events needed:** `carbonate_buffer_event` — when carbonate host rock is present, sulfide oxidation acid is buffered, creating alkaline conditions


### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Altyn-Tyube Emerald** — Deep teal-green, from Kazakhstan; darker color, often on brown quartzite; nucleates at slightly higher pH (9.0–10.0)
- **Variant 2: Tsumeb Crystal** — Bright emerald-green, transparent, large crystals; requires very slow growth rate and stable conditions; the "prize" variant
- **Variant 3: Desert Crust** — Microcrystalline drusy coating, paler green; forms in more variable conditions, lower Cu concentration; common but less valuable

## Paragenetic Note: The Copper Silicate Subgroup

Dioptase does not form in isolation. It belongs to a group of secondary copper silicates that share the same fundamental requirements: oxidizing conditions, dissolved copper, and mobilized silica. The key difference between them is structural:

- **Chrysocolla** (amorphous / microcrystalline): Cu₂₋ₓAlₓ(H₂₋ₓSi₂O₅)(OH)₄·nH₂O — forms at broader pH range, less crystalline
- **Shattuckite** (chain silicate): Cu₅(SiO₃)₄(OH)₂ — orthorhombic, forms in similar conditions but with different Cu:Si ratio
- **Plancheite** (chain silicate): Cu₈(Si₄O₁₁)₂(OH)₄·H₂O — closely allied to shattuckite in structure and appearance
- **Dioptase** (cyclosilicate): Cu₆Si₆O₁₈·6H₂O — trigonal, the most crystalline and gemmy of the group

The presence of any one suggests the others may also form. In the simulator, if dioptase nucleates, shattuckite and chrysocolla should have elevated nucleation probability (same microenvironment).

# Mineral Species Research — Vugg Simulator

## Species: Native Chromium

### Identity
- **Formula:** Cr (elemental chromium)
- **Crystal system:** Cubic (isometric), body-centered cubic (BCC) lattice (space group Im3̄m)
- **Mineral group:** Native element (transition metal)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 9 (exceptionally hard — among the hardest native elements; comparable to corundum)
- **Specific gravity:** 7.14–7.19 (heavy, though less dense than native gold or platinum-group metals)
- **Cleavage:** None
- **Fracture:** Hackly to uneven
- **Luster:** Metallic, brilliant when fresh

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Brilliant silvery-white to steel-gray; quickly tarnishes to dull gray or iridescent blue-black
- **Color causes:** Metallic bonding; thin oxide films produce iridescent interference colors similar to steel
- **Transparency:** Opaque
- **Streak:** Steel-gray
- **Notable visual features:** Extremely small grain size in all known natural occurrences (typically <1 mm); no macroscopic crystals known. Occurs as microscopic inclusions in chromite, diamond, and mantle xenoliths. Metallic luster on polished surfaces.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Granular, microscopic inclusions; no known macroscopic crystal forms in nature
- **Common forms/faces:** None visible at hand-sample scale; electron microscopy reveals irregular equant grains
- **Twin laws:** None reported
- **Varieties:** None established
- **Special morphologies:** Small rounded or angular inclusions (typically 0.1–1 mm) within chromite, diamond, and ultramafic matrix; occasionally as interstitial grains between chromite crystals

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 400–1200°C (mantle to deep crustal; inferred from mantle xenolith and diamond inclusion associations)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 600–900°C (upper mantle/lower crustal conditions associated with reduced mantle fluids)
- **Decomposition temperature:** Melts at 1907°C; oxidizes at much lower temperatures in surface conditions
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** No visible habit variation due to microscopic grain size; theoretical high-T forms larger grains in highly reduced mantle environments

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Cr (extremely high concentration, >1000 ppm — chromium is overwhelmingly lithophile and forms chromite or silicates; native Cr requires extraordinary reducing conditions or chromite breakdown)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Fe (forms ferrochrome alloys in industrial settings; not relevant to natural specimens)
- **Inhibiting elements:** O₂ (oxidizes Cr to Cr³⁺ instantly at surface conditions); S (forms sulfides); Si (forms silicates); Al (forms chromite with Al³⁺ substitution); virtually everything in the crust suppresses native Cr
- **Required pH range:** Any — irrelevant at mantle conditions; at surface, pH is meaningless because oxidation is instantaneous
- **Required Eh range:** Extremely reducing (Eh < -400 mV) — chromium is one of the most easily oxidized native metals; it requires mantle-reducing or impact-melting conditions to persist as Cr⁰
- **Required O₂ range:** Effectively zero — any measurable O₂ converts Cr⁰ to Cr³⁺

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No direct release; Cr⁰ precipitation is effectively a redox sink consuming strongly reducing conditions
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** May consume H₂ or CH₄ if reduction proceeds via fluid-mediated pathways in mantle
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Instant oxidation → chromite (FeCr₂O₄), eskolaite (Cr₂O₃), or Cr³⁺-bearing silicates; rapid surface passivation by Cr₂O₃ film prevents further oxidation (industrial chrome's corrosion resistance comes from this passive layer)

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Unknown in natural settings; inferred to be very slow or episodic
- **Maximum crystal size:** ~1 mm (all known natural specimens are microscopic); industrial ferrochrome crystals can be much larger but are anthropogenic
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** Not applicable — native chromium does not form in vugs. It forms in solid rock as inclusions or interstitial grains.
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Presumed faster at higher T but always constrained by extreme reducing requirements
- **Competes with:** Chromite (FeCr₂O₄) — the overwhelmingly dominant Cr mineral; chrome-diopside, uvarovite, chromian clinochlore, and virtually every other Cr-bearing mineral

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Stable to 1907°C (melting point) in reducing conditions; oxidizes above ~300°C if O₂ present
- **Breaks down in light?** No
- **Dissolves in water?** Insoluble; rapidly oxidizes and passivates
- **Dissolves in acid?** Resistant to HCl and H₂SO₄ due to passive Cr₂O₃ film; dissolves in hot HNO₃ and aqua regia; attacked by alkali fusion (NaOH + NaNO₃)
- **Oxidizes?** Instantly in air — surface tarnish to Cr₂O₃ within seconds; the oxide film is self-limiting (passive layer ~2–3 nm), which is why stainless steel works
- **Dehydrates?** N/A
- **Radiation sensitivity:** None notable

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Chromite, chrome-diopside, and all other Cr-bearing minerals — native chromium is essentially impossible to form in equilibrium with normal crustal chemistry; it requires either:
  1. **Extreme reduction** in mantle xenoliths (methane-bearing fluids, diamond-stable fO₂)
  2. **Impact melting** of chromite-bearing rocks under highly reducing conditions
  3. **Meteoritic reduction** in shocked meteorites (Cr⁰ reported from some chondrites)
- **Forms BEFORE:** Immediate oxidation to chromite or eskolaite upon uplift to crustal conditions
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Chromite (host), diamond (inclusion in some kimberlites), olivine, pyroxene (mantle xenoliths), kamacite/taenite (meteorites — Cr⁰ reported in some reduced meteorites)
- **Zone:** Mantle (diamond-stable depths, ~150 km); possibly impact melt environments; meteorites
- **Geological environment:** Upper mantle xenoliths in kimberlite and alkali basalt; shocked meteorites; theoretical in some highly reduced ore deposits but not documented

### Famous Localities
- **Type locality 1:** Yangliuping Cu-Ni-PGE deposit, Sichuan Province, China — the first and most documented terrestrial occurrence. Native Cr occurs as microscopic inclusions (up to ~0.7 mm) in chromite from a Cu-Ni-PGE deposit in the Garzê Autonomous Prefecture. Associated with pentlandite, chalcopyrite, and millerite. The reducing conditions in this deposit are attributed to the ultramafic host and localized methane-bearing fluid inclusions.
- **Locality 2:** Udachnaya kimberlite pipe, Yakutia, Russia — not confirmed as native Cr, but eskolaite (Cr₂O₃) inclusions in diamond suggest Cr-reduced mantle conditions. Native Cr is theoretically possible in the same paragenesis but not documented.
- **Locality 3:** Meteorites — native chromium (as kamacite/taenite alloy inclusions or as separate Cr⁰ grains) reported from some highly reduced enstatite chondrites and ureilites. These are extraterrestrial and not Earth-sourced.
- **Notable specimens:** All terrestrial specimens are microscopic and preserved in polished thin sections or grain mounts. No museum-quality hand specimens exist. The Sichuan grains are the reference standard.

### Flavor Text

> Native chromium is the mineral that shouldn't exist. Chromium is so eager to bond with oxygen that it forms chromite, chrome-diopside, uvarovite, and a hundred other minerals before it ever considers staying metallic. The only place it succeeds is in the deep mantle, under diamond-stable conditions, where methane-bearing fluids are so reducing that even chromium surrenders to neutrality. The grains are tiny — fractions of a millimeter — embedded in chromite from a single Chinese Cu-Ni deposit and a handful of shocked meteorites. There are no crystals. There are no hand specimens. There is only the principle: that under sufficient pressure and sufficient reduction, even the most lithophile element can briefly become a native metal, only to oxidize the instant it reaches the surface. The oxide film forms in seconds, a self-healing armor of Cr₂O₃ that is the reason stainless steel exists. Native chromium is the ghost in the machine — the reduced state that industrial metallurgy rediscovered, that geology produced once or twice by accident, and that the crust immediately erased.

## Linked Paragenetic Context: The Chromium System

Native chromium occupies an extreme position in the chromium paragenesis — it is the most reduced and most rare end-member.

### Normal Cr Paragenesis (Oxidized to Moderately Reduced)
1. **Chromite (FeCr₂O₄)** — spinel structure, the dominant Cr mineral everywhere. Forms from mafic/ultramafic magmas at ~1200–1400°C.
2. **Chrome-diopside** — Cr³⁺ substitutes for Al in diopside, common in kimberlites and eclogites.
3. **Uvarovite (Ca₃Cr₂Si₃O₁₂)** — rare green garnet, forms in skarns and rodingites.
4. **Chromian clinochlore** — Cr³⁺ in chlorite, serpentinite alteration product.
5. **Eskolaite (Cr₂O₃)** — high-T oxidation product, reported from Udachnaya kimberlite diamond inclusions.

### Extreme Reduction (Native Chromium)
- **Native Cr (Cr⁰)** — Only in the Sichuan deposit and meteorites. Requires fO₂ below the iron-wüstite buffer, approaching diamond-graphite equilibrium.

### Simulator Notes
Native chromium should NOT be a player-target mineral in Vugg. It is a rare easter egg / trace inclusion that appears in extreme mantle scenarios. Its value is educational and geological — it demonstrates the reducing limit of the simulator's redox model. If a player sees "native chromium detected (microscopic inclusion)" in a kimberlite scenario, it means they've reached the most reducing conditions the game allows.

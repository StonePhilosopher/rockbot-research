## Species: Raspite

### Identity
- **Formula:** PbWO₄ — lead tungstate (identical to stolzite)
- **Crystal system:** Monoclinic (prismatic class 2/m)
- **Space group:** P2₁/b (or P2₁/c in modern setting)
- **Mineral group:** Tungstate (oxide subclass)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 2.5–3 (very soft, similar to stolzite)
- **Specific gravity:** 8.34 (extraordinarily dense — lead + tungsten combined)
- **Cleavage:** Perfect {100} — one direction, well-developed
- **Fracture:** Uneven to conchoidal
- **Luster:** Adamantine to resinous
- **Streak:** White to pale yellow
- **Unit cell:** a = 5.50 Å, b = 12.03 Å, c = 5.46 Å; β = 107.7°; Z = 4

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Light yellow, yellowish brown, straw-yellow, smoky gray, occasionally greenish or orange
- **Color causes:** Pb-WO₄ charge transfer; variations due to trace Mo substitution (greenish) and minor Fe inclusions
- **Transparency:** Translucent to transparent in thin edges
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Tabular crystals with perfect cleavage and adamantine luster; may show striations on prism faces; high refractive indices (nα ≈ 2.27, nβ ≈ 2.27, nγ ≈ 2.30) give brilliant surface reflections comparable to diamond

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Tabular crystals, flattened on {100} and elongated along [001]
- **Common forms/faces:** {100} dominant, {001}, {101} — tabular to elongate prismatic
- **Twin laws:** Not commonly reported; penetration twins possible but rare
- **Varieties:** Forms a complete solid-solution series with wulfenite (PbMoO₄) where Mo replaces W; intermediate compositions show gradational colors from yellow (W-rich) to orange-green (Mo-rich)
- **Special morphologies:** Small isolated crystals; often partially altered or pseudomorphed by stolzite at elevated temperatures

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** <100°C (supergene oxidation zone, ambient to warm)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 15–80°C (low-temperature weathering environment)
- **Decomposition temperature:** Inverts to stolzite at ~395°C; above this temperature, stolzite (tetragonal) is the stable form
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - Below 395°C: raspite (monoclinic) is the thermodynamically stable low-T form
  - Above 395°C: stolzite (tetragonal) becomes stable
  - In nature, both form at low T; raspite is the rarer dimorph because stolzite nucleates more readily even below the inversion temperature (metastable persistence of the high-T form)

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:**
  - Pb²⁺: minimum ~50 ppm (from oxidized galena or other Pb minerals)
  - W (as tungstate WO₄²⁻): minimum ~20 ppm (from oxidized wolframite/scheelite)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - Mo: forms wulfenite-stolzite-raspite solid solution; shifts color toward orange/green
  - Ca: trace amounts may stabilize lattice but not required
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - CO₃²⁻: high carbonate activity promotes cerussite (PbCO₃) instead
  - SO₄²⁻: promotes anglesite (PbSO₄)
  - S²⁻: reducing conditions precipitate galena, stripping Pb from fluid
- **Required pH range:** 4–7 (mildly acidic to neutral; oxidation zone conditions)
- **Required Eh range:** Oxidizing (Eh > 0.2 V) — must oxidize primary sulfides to release Pb²⁺ and W
- **Required O₂ range:** High — supergene/oxidizing zone

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No significant release; Pb²⁺ and WO₄²⁻ combine directly
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Consumes Pb²⁺ and WO₄²⁻ from oxidizing fluid
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Dissolves in HCl releasing Pb²⁺ and WO₄²⁻; thermal decomposition (>395°C) releases no chemistry but reorganizes into stolzite structure

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Slow — rare mineral requiring specific Pb+W coexistence in oxidation zone
- **Maximum crystal size:** Crystals to ~2 cm reported from Broken Hill (exceptional)
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–5 mm
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Growth rate increases with temperature but raspite is unstable above 395°C; in practice, low-T supergene conditions are slow
- **Competes with:**
  - Stolzite (same formula, different structure — direct polymorphic competition)
  - Wulfenite (if Mo present)
  - Cerussite (for Pb²⁺ in CO₂-rich fluids)
  - Anglesite (for Pb²⁺ in sulfate-rich fluids)
  - Pyromorphite/mimetite (for Pb²⁺ in P/As-rich fluids)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** YES — converts to stolzite at ~395°C; this is a reconstructive phase transition (not simple thermal decomposition)
- **Breaks down in light?** No known photodegradation
- **Dissolves in water?** Very low solubility
- **Dissolves in acid?** Decomposes in HCl; slowly soluble in strong acids
- **Oxidizes?** Already fully oxidized — both Pb²⁺ and W⁶⁺ are in highest stable oxidation states
- **Dehydrates?** N/A (anhydrous)
- **Radiation sensitivity:** PbWO₄ is famously radiation-hard (used in CERN CMS electromagnetic calorimeter as a scintillator), but natural raspite's monoclinic structure may be slightly less radiation-stable than synthetic tetragonal PbWO₄

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Primary sulfide mineralization (galena for Pb; wolframite/scheelite/hübnerite for W) must be present and undergo oxidation
- **Forms BEFORE:** May be replaced by cerussite (carbonate conditions), anglesite (sulfate conditions), or converted to stolzite if reheated above 395°C
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - Stolzite (dimorph — often co-occurring at Broken Hill)
  - Wulfenite (Mo analog)
  - Cerussite, anglesite, pyromorphite, mimetite (other Pb secondary minerals)
  - Galena (relict primary)
  - Scheelite, wolframite (relict primary W sources)
  - Limonite/goethite (oxidized iron minerals)
- **Zone:** Supergene/oxidation zone — upper levels of tungsten-bearing lead deposits
- **Geological environment:** Oxidized zones of hydrothermal veins containing both Pb and W minerals; most common where wolframite/scheelite-bearing veins intersect galena-bearing horizons

### Famous Localities
- **Broken Hill, New South Wales, Australia** — Type locality (1897). Discovered by Charles Rasp, the German-Australian prospector after whom the mineral is named. Both raspite and stolzite occur together here; raspite forms yellowish tabular crystals in oxidized ore.
- **Cordillera Mine, New South Wales, Australia** — Renowned for co-occurrence of both dimorphs in the same specimens
- **Clara Mine, Black Forest, Germany** — Well-crystallized specimens, often with wulfenite
- **Tuena Mine, New South Wales, Australia** — Associated with stolzite
- **Darwin District, Inyo County, California, USA** — Small crystals to 1 cm
- **Ore Mountains (Erzgebirge), Bohemia, Czech Republic** — Historical locality for tungsten minerals

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Not typically reported as fluorescent under standard UV lamps
- **SW (255nm) color:** None reported
- **MW (310nm) color:** None reported
- **LW (365nm) color:** None reported
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** None known
- **Note:** Synthetic PbWO₄ (tetragonal, scheelite-structure) is a famous scintillator, emitting blue light under ionizing radiation — but this is radioluminescence, not UV fluorescence. Natural raspite's monoclinic structure does not exhibit this property.

### Flavor Text

> Raspite is stolzite's shy twin — same chemistry, same weight, same lead-tungsten heart, but where stolzite chose tetragonal symmetry, raspite crystallized monoclinic. Same formula, different handshake. It forms in the cool oxidizing zone where galena's lead meets wolframite's tungsten, both stripped from their primary minerals by weathering water. The result is a tabular crystal of extraordinary density and perfect cleavage, light yellow to smoky brown, with an adamantine luster that seems almost too bright for so soft a mineral. It was named for Charles Rasp, the German prospector who found the Broken Hill lode — a fitting namesake for a mineral that only reveals itself after the primary ore has been broken open and left to the air. Raspite is rare because stolzite, its high-temperature dimorph, persists metastably even in the cool zone where raspite should rule. So raspite hides in the shadows of its own twin, waiting for the right combination of chemistry and kinetics to let the low-temperature form win. Some days it does. Most days, stolzite takes the crystal. That is the quiet tragedy of polymorphism: thermodynamics says one thing, kinetics says another, and the rarer form survives only where nucleation was too slow for the commoner one to get there first.

```
IF temp < 100 AND temp > 15
   AND Eh > 0.2 (oxidizing)
   AND Pb > 50 ppm
   AND WO4 > 20 ppm
   AND pH >= 4 AND pH <= 7
   AND (stolzite_nucleation_suppressed OR slow_nucleation_conditions)
   → nucleate raspite
   → habit = tabular (default) OR elongate_prismatic (if growth space limited)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF temp < 395 AND temp > 15
   AND Pb > 40 ppm AND WO4 > 15 ppm
   AND Eh > 0.15
   → grow at rate SLOW (0.4× quartz baseline)
   → if temp > 395: convert to stolzite (polymorphic transition)
   → Mo trace > 20 ppm: shift color toward orange/green
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - Tabular: default, moderate supersaturation
  - Elongate prismatic: confined growth in narrow fractures
  - Massive/drusy: very high nucleation density
- **Decomposition products:**
  - Thermal: converts to stolzite at >395°C (reconstructive phase transition)
  - Weathering: may alter to cerussite or anglesite depending on fluid chemistry

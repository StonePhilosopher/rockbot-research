## Species: Enargite

### Identity
- **Formula:** Cu₃AsS₄
- **Crystal system:** Orthorhombic (mm2)
- **Mineral group:** Sulfosalt (thioarsenate)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 3
- **Specific gravity:** 4.4–4.5 (measured), 4.40 (calculated)
- **Cleavage:** Perfect on {110}, good on {100} and {010}
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal to uneven
- **Luster:** Metallic

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Steel gray, blackish gray, violet-black
- **Color causes:** Intrinsic metallic bonding; no chromophore needed
- **Transparency:** Opaque
- **Streak:** Dark gray to black
- **Notable visual features:** Deeply striated prism faces parallel to [001]. Thin coatings of pyrite or chalcopyrite are common. Crystals can appear coated with a dull film of oxidation products.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Prismatic, elongate parallel to [001]; also tabular on {001}
- **Common forms/faces:** {001} pinacoid, {110} prism zone (deeply striated), {101}, {011}
- **Twin laws:** Twin plane {320} common; rarely interpenetrating pseudohexagonal trillings
- **Varieties:** None named. Dimorphous with luzonite (see below).
- **Special morphologies:** Massive aggregates common in ore bodies; slender prismatic crystals in vugs and veins

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 300–500°C (porphyry Cu), up to 600°C in high-sulfidation systems
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 400–450°C
- **Decomposition temperature:** Inverts to luzonite below ~300°C. Above 600°C, decomposes to chalcocite + arsenopyrite + sulfur (or melts incongruently)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Below 300°C → luzonite (tetragonal) is the stable polymorph. Enargite is strictly the high-T form. In nature, cooling enargite may partially invert or develop luzonite overgrowths/rims.

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Cu (≥5000 ppm), As (≥1000 ppm), S (as sulfide, S²⁻)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Fe (stabilizes structure, up to ~1% Fe common), Sb (forms solid solution toward famatinite Cu₃SbS₄), Zn (minor substitution)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Pb suppresses enargite in favor of galena + tennantite; high Mo favors molybdenite; very high Fe favors chalcopyrite/pyrite
- **Required pH range:** Acidic to mildly acidic, pH 2–5 (porphyry fluids)
- **Required Eh range:** Strongly reducing, Eh −0.2 to +0.1 V (sulfide-stable)
- **Required O₂ range:** <0.1 ppm dissolved O₂ (anoxic)

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Consumes As³⁺ and Cu⁺ from fluid, precipitating as thioarsenate (AsS₄)³⁻ complex
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Local fluid acidification (HS⁻ consumption shifts H₂S ⇌ H⁺ + HS⁻)
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Oxidation releases Cu²⁺, AsO₄³⁻, and SO₄²⁻ into fluid; if Fe is present, drives scorodite (FeAsO₄·2H₂O) precipitation. In acid mine drainage contexts, enargite is a major arsenic source.

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate — faster than pyrite, slower than chalcopyrite at same temperature
- **Maximum crystal size in nature:** Up to 15 cm (Butte, Montana — exceptional)
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–20 mm; exceptional vugs to 2 cm
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — rapid above 400°C, slowing below 350°C; below 300°C growth effectively stops as luzonite becomes stable
- **Competes with:** Chalcopyrite (for Cu), tennantite (for Cu+As), bornite (for Cu), pyrite (for S)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes. Above 600°C: incongruent melting or decomposition to chalcocite + arsenopyrite. Below 300°C: polymorphic inversion to luzonite.
- **Breaks down in light?** No — not light-sensitive (unlike realgar)
- **Dissolves in water?** Very low solubility in pure water. Oxidative dissolution is the concern.
- **Dissolves in acid?** Dissolves in oxidizing acids (HNO₃, aqua regia). Stable in non-oxidizing HCl.
- **Oxidizes?** Yes — primary oxidation product is scorodite (FeAsO₄·2H₂O) if Fe is available; otherwise Cu-arsenates (olivenite, clinoclase) and Cu-sulfates (brochantite, antlerite). Bulk oxidation in air is slow at ambient T.
- **Dehydrates?** No — anhydrous mineral
- **Radiation sensitivity:** None documented

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Early quartz, pyrite, some chalcopyrite (enargite is a middle-stage porphyry mineral, not the earliest)
- **Forms BEFORE:** Bornite, covellite, chalcocite (late-stage Cu enrichment), tennantite (if Sb/As ratio shifts), supergene oxidation minerals (scorodite, olivenite, brochantite)
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Quartz, pyrite, chalcopyrite, bornite, sphalerite, galena, tennantite, molybdenite, barite, alunite (high-sulfidation systems)
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene — porphyry copper systems and high-sulfidation epithermal deposits
- **Geological environment:** Porphyry Cu-Mo-Au deposits (2–5 km depth), high-sulfidation epithermal Au-Cu deposits (near-surface, <1 km), and less commonly moderate-T hydrothermal veins

### Famous Localities
- **Butte, Montana, USA:** The world's finest enargite locality. Leonard Mine and other Butte mines produced superb prismatic crystals to 15 cm, steel-gray with metallic luster, often on quartz or pyrite matrix. Most specimens mined a generation ago; modern specimens are rare and highly valued. Crystals typically 1–2 cm, exceptional to 5+ cm.
- **Chuquicamata, Chile:** Massive enargite in one of the world's largest porphyry copper deposits. Less crystalline than Butte but economically important.
- **El Indio–Tambo district, Chile:** High-sulfidation epithermal system. Enargite associated with alunite, pyrite, and gold mineralization. Famous for arsenic environmental legacy as well as mineralogy.
- **Quiruvilca, La Libertad, Peru:** Fine lustrous columnar crystals with quartz.
- **Morococha, Peru:** Another important Peruvian enargite locality.
- **Tsumeb, Namibia:** Enargite in upper oxidation zone (rare for supergene occurrence), crystals to 80 mm. Also type locality for luzonite as trace constituent.
- **Bingham Canyon (Kennecott), Utah, USA:** Porphyry Cu-Mo; enargite in deeper sulfide zones.

- **Habit selection logic:**

  - Low fluid turbulence, stagnant → massive aggregate habit

### Variants for Game

- **Variant 1: Butte Blade**
  - What makes it different: Exceptionally large prismatic crystals, deeply striated, metallic steel-gray. The classic collector's enargite.
  - Conditions: Moderate T (380–420°C), slow cooling, quartz-rich matrix, low fluid turbulence. Vug with ample space.

- **Variant 2: High-Sulfidation Enargite**
  - What makes it different: Fine-grained, massive to poorly crystalline, often coated with alunite or pyrite. Less visually striking but geologically significant.
  - Conditions: Higher T (450–550°C), very acidic pH (2–3.5), high SO₄²⁻ from magmatic vapor, rapid deposition. Associated with alunite, pyrite, enargite-gold mineralization.

- **Variant 3: Luzonite-Rimmed Enargite**
  - What makes it different: Enargite core with tetragonal luzonite overgrowth rim — a visible record of cooling through the polymorphic inversion temperature. Rare in nature, spectacular when found.
  - Conditions: Precipitate enargite at 350–400°C, then cool below 300°C over 5+ steps while maintaining Cu+As availability. Luzonite nucleates on enargite surfaces (epitaxial overgrowth).

- **Variant 4: Oxidized Enargite Pseudomorph**
  - What makes it different: Enargite crystal shape preserved but replaced by scorodite + olivenite + brochantite. A ghost crystal — the form remains, the chemistry has walked away.
  - Conditions: Form enargite, then expose to oxidizing, acidic, Fe-bearing fluid (Eh > +0.3 V, pH < 4, trace_Fe > 1000 ppm). Slow oxidation over 10+ steps preserves external form while internal structure collapses.

## Dimorph Note: Luzonite

Enargite and luzonite share the formula Cu₃AsS₄ but are dimorphs — different crystal structures at different temperatures:
- **Enargite:** Orthorhombic, mm2, high-T stable form (>300°C)
- **Luzonite:** Tetragonal, I4̄2m, low-T stable form (<300°C)

The transition is sluggish; enargite can persist metastably below 300°C, especially if cooling is rapid. In the simulator, cooling an enargite-bearing vug below 300°C should eventually trigger luzonite nucleation on enargite surfaces or full polymorphic conversion. Luzonite is visually similar (steel-gray, metallic) but crystals are stubbier, less prismatic, with different cleavage and twinning.

Luzonite is NOT in the original species list but should be added as a builder-requested mineral once the polymorph system supports it. For now, document it as a variant/condition of enargite.

## Simulator Fit

Enargite slots naturally into the **Porphyry Copper** scenario (already implemented) as a middle-stage sulfide. It fills the gap between early pyrite/chalcopyrite and late bornite/covellite. The arsenic chemistry connects to the broader oxidation zone narrative: enargite in the primary zone → scorodite/olivenite in the supergene zone → the arsenic eventually walks into surface water or secondary phosphates/arsenates.

The builder can implement enargite without adding new trace elements. Cu, As, S, Fe are all tracked. The only new mechanic is the **polymorphic inversion** (enargite → luzonite below 300°C), which is a precedent for other dimorphs in the game (sphalerite/wurtzite, argentite/acanthite, stolzite/raspite).

**Priority for builder:** Medium. Enargite is a builder-added species, not in the original ~100. Its gameplay value is connecting porphyry chemistry to arsenic oxidation zone minerals (scorodite, olivenite) and demonstrating polymorphic transitions. Visually distinctive (metallic prisms) but not as colorful as azurite or fluorite.

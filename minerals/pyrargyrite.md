## Species: Pyrargyrite

### Identity
- **Formula:** Ag₃SbS₃
- **Crystal system:** Trigonal (rhombohedral)
- **Mineral group:** Sulfosalt (sulfantimonite subgroup)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 2.5
- **Specific gravity:** 5.77–5.86 (slightly denser than proustite due to heavier Sb)
- **Cleavage:** Poor to indistinct; parting on {1011} reported
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal to uneven; sectile — soft enough to cut with a knife
- **Luster:** Metallic-adamantine; distinctly more metallic than proustite
- **Space group:** R3c (no. 161) — isostructural with proustite
- **IMA symbol:** Pyr

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Deep ruby-red to cherry-red in thin splinters; grayish-black to iron-black in thick opaque masses
- **Color causes:** Charge transfer between silver and the (SbS₃)³⁻ pyramidal groups; antimony's larger atomic size shifts absorption edge toward longer wavelengths vs. arsenic analogue
- **Transparency:** Small crystals and thin splinters are translucent to transparent and glow deep ruby-red in transmitted light; larger crystals are opaque and metallic-gray
- **Streak:** Purplish-red to brownish-red — **diagnostic:** this distinguishes pyrargyrite from proustite (which has a scarlet-vermilion streak)
- **Notable visual features:** The contrast between opaque metallic-gray bulk and ruby-red translucency in thin edges is striking. Hemimorphic character visible in well-developed crystals — striations on prism faces directed toward one termination only.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Hexagonal prisms terminated by low hexagonal pyramids or drusy basal planes; prismatic crystals to 5 cm+
- **Common forms/faces:** {1010} prisms, {0001} basal plane, {1011} rhombohedra
- **Twin laws:** Common twinning on {1011} and {0112}; cyclic twins produce trillings; contact twins also reported
- **Varieties:** None significant; near end-member composition is standard; intermediate members of the proustite-pyrargyrite series are rare in nature
- **Special morphologies:** Massive, granular, compact; rarely as well-formed euhedral crystals; often as coatings or replacements of earlier silver minerals

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 100–320°C
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 150–230°C (slightly cooler than proustite's sweet spot)
- **Decomposition temperature:** Melting point ~480°C (similar to proustite); thermal degradation begins at lower temperatures than proustite
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Higher temperatures (180–280°C) favor large prismatic crystals with hemimorphic terminations; lower temperatures favor massive or granular forms; very low temperatures produce incrustations

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Ag (minimum ~2000 ppm), Sb (minimum ~500 ppm), S (minimum ~500 ppm)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** As (traces stabilize structure; too much As routes to proustite); Cu (common in same epithermal veins but competes); Mn (may enhance crystal development)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Fe favors pyrite/stibnite; high Pb pushes toward galena/boulangerite; high Zn toward sphalerite; high Cu toward tetrahedrite/tennantite; excess As (>50% of As+Sb) suppresses pyrargyrite in favor of proustite
- **Required pH range:** Mildly acidic to neutral, pH 4–7
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing to mildly reducing — sulfosalts require reduced sulfur species
- **Required O₂ range:** Low — oxidizing conditions decompose sulfosalts

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Consumes Ag⁺, Sb³⁺, and S²⁻ from fluid; no significant byproducts
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Depletes Ag from fluid — can suppress later native silver precipitation
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Light/heat decomposition releases native silver (metallic Ag) and acanthite (Ag₂S) internally; antimony may oxidize to cervantite (Sb₂O₄) or stibiconite (Sb₃O₆(OH)) if O₂ present

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate (2.8× baseline in game — slightly faster than proustite) — pyrargyrite typically forms larger crystals than proustite in nature
- **Maximum crystal size:** Up to 5 cm+ (Andreasberg, Germany; San Cristobal, Bolivia); exceptional masses to several kg in rich ore
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 2–8 mm; thumbnail to small-cabinet specimens common
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Growth accelerates at higher temperatures within stability window; pyrargyrite is slightly more tolerant of temperature variation than proustite
- **Competes with:** Proustite (As-analog), acanthite, native silver, tetrahedrite/tennantite, stibnite, galena, miargyrite

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes — internal silver migration begins at modest temperatures; thermal degradation produces acanthite inclusions; slightly more heat-tolerant than proustite but not by much
- **Breaks down in light?** **YES — light-sensitive, though slightly less severely than proustite.** Direct sunlight causes darkening over days to weeks. Same mechanism as proustite: photon energy mobilizes Ag⁺ ions, which nucleate microscopic acanthite internally. The darkening is permanent.
- **Dissolves in water?** Very low solubility
- **Dissolves in acid?** Soluble in nitric acid; attacked by cyanide solutions
- **Oxidizes?** Yes — oxidizes to cervantite/stibiconite + acanthite in weathering zone
- **Dehydrates?** No hydrates
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Not particularly radiation-sensitive

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Acanthite, native silver (in some sequences); tetrahedrite/tennantite (in others); stibnite; early sulfides like pyrite, galena, sphalerite
- **Forms BEFORE:** Native silver (in other sequences); anglesite, cerussite in oxidation zone; quartz, calcite, barite as late fillings
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Acanthite, native silver, proustite, galena, sphalerite, chalcopyrite, tetrahedrite, stibnite, miargyrite, polybasite, stephanite, quartz, calcite, barite, fluorite
- **Zone:** Epithermal / low-temperature hydrothermal; supergene enrichment zone; oxidation zone of silver-antimony deposits
- **Geological environment:** Low-to-moderate temperature epithermal silver veins (especially Sb-rich systems); supergene enrichment blankets; volcanic-hosted precious metal systems; antimony-silver districts

### Famous Localities
- **Andreasberg, Harz Mountains, Germany:** Historic source of exceptional large prismatic crystals in quartz vugs. The Harz was a major silver mining district from the 10th century. Specimens reached 5 cm+ with perfect hexagonal prisms.
- **San Cristobal, Potosí, Bolivia:** Modern classic for large, deep-red crystals in epithermal veins. Bolivian pyrargyrite is typically darker and more metallic than German material.
- **Zacatecas / Fresnillo, Mexico:** The Veta Grande District produced historic pyrargyrite specimens associated with acanthite, native silver, and quartz. Mexican pyrargyrite is often finely crystalline and associated with rich silver ore.
- **Notable specimens:** The Andreasberg specimens are benchmarks for crystal size and form. The Bolivian material is prized for color intensity. Mexican pyrargyrite from the 19th-century bonanzas is legendary among silver mineral collectors.

### Flavor Text
> Pyrargyrite carries two identities in one body. Hold a thick crystal in your hand and it is gunmetal gray, dull, almost disappointing — a metallic rock like any other. But crack it, or find a thin edge where light can pass, and it ignites: deep ruby-red, cherry, the color of heart's blood seen by candle flame. The Greeks named it for fire and silver — *pyr* and *argyros* — and Glocker gave it that name in 1831 because he understood what it was: not a gray mineral that happens to be red at the edges, but a red mineral imprisoned in its own opacity. It is the dark ruby silver, heavier and slightly more common than its scarlet sister proustite, born in the same epithermal wombs where silver, antimony, and sulfur negotiate their slow crystalline treaty. Like proustite, it carries a death sentence in light. The silver in its lattice is restless. Given time and photons, the Ag⁺ ions will migrate, find each other, and nucleate acanthite in the heart of the crystal — black inclusions that spread like a mineral cancer. The process is thermal as well as optical. Even in darkness, the internal silver is never fully at rest. A century-old specimen in a museum drawer is not the same mineral it was when it came out of the ground. It is a crystal in the process of becoming something else.

## Paragenetic Sequence Notes (Pyrargyrite ↔ Proustite)

Pyrargyrite and proustite are isostructural (isomorphous) end members of a solid solution series with the general formula Ag₃(As,Sb)S₃. Both crystallize in trigonal space group R3c with the same atomic framework. The only structural difference is the pyramidal anion group: (SbS₃)³⁻ in pyrargyrite vs. (AsS₃)³⁻ in proustite.

Natural specimens rarely show intermediate compositions. The As:Sb ratio in the hydrothermal fluid is the controlling factor:

| X_As = As/(As+Sb) | Mineral |
|-------------------|---------|
| > 0.7 | Proustite (pure) |
| 0.5 – 0.7 | Proustite-dominant (may have Sb traces) |
| 0.3 – 0.5 | Pyrargyrite-dominant (may have As traces) |
| < 0.3 | Pyrargyrite (pure) |

Geologically, pyrargyrite is significantly more common than proustite because antimony is more abundant than arsenic in most epithermal silver systems. Arsenic-rich fluids that would produce proustite are restricted to specific geological settings — Carlin-type gold systems, some volcanic fumaroles, and certain arsenic-silver districts.

Both minerals are **late-stage precipitates** in the epithermal paragenetic sequence. They form after the major base metal sulfides (galena, sphalerite, chalcopyrite) and often replace or intergrow with earlier acanthite. In the supergene zone, both may dissolve and reprecipitate as secondary enrichment phases.

Both share the **light-sensitive decomposition** mechanic — photodegradation via internal silver migration and acanthite nucleation. This makes them the only known mineral pair in the game where:
1. Isostructural substitution (As ↔ Sb) controls which species appears
2. Both species share an irreversible light-degradation pathway
3. The degradation product (acanthite) is itself a collectible mineral in the game

This creates a unique gameplay loop: players may grow beautiful ruby silver crystals, but if they leave them exposed in the "light" (simulated by game events or display choices), the crystals slowly blacken from within, eventually yielding acanthite — a different, darker treasure.

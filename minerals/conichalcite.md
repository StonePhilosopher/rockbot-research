## Species: Conichalcite

### Identity
- **Formula:** CaCu(AsO₄)(OH)
- **Crystal system:** Orthorhombic (pseudo-monoclinic due to twinning)
- **Mineral group:** Arsenate (hydrous calcium copper arsenate)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 4.5
- **Specific gravity:** 4.2–4.4
- **Cleavage:** {010} good, {100} poor, {001} poor
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal to uneven
- **Luster:** Vitreous to sub-adamantine; often resinous or waxy on fractures
- **Streak:** Greenish
- **Tenacity:** Brittle

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Emerald green, apple green, yellowish green, grass green, dark green, bluish green; rare colorless to white
- **Color causes:** Cu²⁺ (primary chromophore — all green shades); trace Fe³⁺ may shift toward yellowish green; pure end-member is emerald green
- **Transparency:** Translucent to opaque; rare transparent crystals
- **Streak:** Pale green to greenish white
- **Notable visual features:** Often forms botryoidal to reniform (kidney-shaped) crusts and masses with a radiating fibrous internal structure. The botryoidal surface is smooth and waxy, resembling malachite or chrysocolla. Also forms prismatic to acicular crystals, often in radiating aggregates. Concentric color banding common. Strongly resembles olivenite (Cu₂(AsO₄)(OH)) but is distinguished by the Ca content, hardness, and color (conichalcite is typically brighter green, olivenite more olive/dark green).

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Prismatic to acicular; commonly botryoidal, reniform, globular
- **Common forms/faces:** {010}, {100}, {001}, {110} — orthorhombic prisms; often twinned on {110}
- **Twin laws:** Common on {110} — produces pseudo-monoclinic appearance; penetration and contact twins
- **Varieties:** None formally recognized, but color variations noted:
  - **Emerald-green:** Typical, Cu-rich
  - **Apple-green:** Lower Cu or higher Ca
  - **Yellowish-green:** Fe-bearing
- **Special morphologies:**
  - **Botryoidal masses:** Smooth, rounded, grape-like clusters — most common habit
  - **Reniform crusts:** Kidney-shaped layered masses
  - **Radiating aggregates:** Spiky clusters of prismatic crystals
  - **Stalactitic:** Concentric-layered dripstone forms
  - **Drusy coatings:** Fine crystals coating vug walls
  - **Pseudomorphs:** After azurite, malachite, or other copper minerals (rare)

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 10–100°C (supergene/oxidation zone)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 15–40°C (ambient to warm surface temperatures)
- **Decomposition temperature:** Loses OH at ~300–400°C; decomposes above 500°C
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Lower temperatures favor botryoidal forms; higher temperatures (>50°C) may promote more prismatic/acicular habits

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Ca (minimum ~100 ppm), Cu (minimum ~50 ppm), As (minimum ~50 ppm)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - Fe (trace to 10%) — shifts color toward yellowish-green; may form solid solution toward arseniosiderite
  - Zn (trace) — may substitute for Cu, shifting toward adamite-like compositions
  - P (trace) — may partially substitute for As
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Pb (favors mimetite); high Co (favors erythrite); high Ni (favors annabergite); high Fe (favors scorodite or arseniosiderite)
- **Required pH range:** Mildly acidic to neutral (pH 5.0–7.5)
- **Required Eh range:** Oxidizing (As must be +5; Cu typically +2)
- **Required O₂ range:** Moderate to high (oxidizing environment)

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Consumes OH⁻ from fluid (slightly lowers pH)
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Local pH drop
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Releases Cu (toxic) and As (toxic) upon acid dissolution or heating

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate (for a supergene mineral)
- **Maximum crystal size:** Up to 5 cm (rare); typical 1–10 mm
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–5 mm (small botryoidal masses or prismatic crystals)
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — growth accelerates at higher temperatures but dehydration also accelerates
- **Competes with:** Olivenite (Cu₂(AsO₄)(OH) — same elements minus Ca); malachite (Cu₂(CO₃)(OH)₂ — if CO₃ available); chrysocolla (Cu₂₋ₓAlₓ(H₂ₓ₋₂Si₂O₅)(OH)₄·nH₂O — silicate alternative); azurite (if CO₃ and higher Cu)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes — dehydroxylates at ~300–400°C, decomposes at >500°C
- **Breaks down in light?** No
- **Dissolves in water?** Very slightly soluble
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — dissolves readily in HCl and HNO₃ with effervescence if carbonates present
- **Oxidizes?** Cu is already +2; As is already +5; no further oxidation
- **Dehydrates?** Partially — loses weakly bound water at low temperatures; structurally stable to ~300°C
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Generally stable; may darken under prolonged UV from defect formation

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Primary copper sulfides (chalcopyrite, bornite, chalcocite) and arsenides (arsenopyrite, tennantite, enargite) must oxidize first to release Cu and As
- **Forms BEFORE:** More stable Cu carbonates (malachite, azurite — if CO₃ available); late silicates (chrysocolla); or dehydration products
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - **Copper source minerals:** chalcopyrite, bornite, chalcocite, chrysocolla, malachite, azurite
  - **Arsenic source minerals:** arsenopyrite, tennantite, enargite, lollingite
  - **Co-precipitates:** olivenite, clinoclase, cornubite, tyrolite, chrysocolla
  - **Calcium sources:** calcite, dolomite, gypsum, apophyllite (in vugs)
  - **Other arsenates:** scorodite, adamite, erythrite, annabergite, pharmacolite, haidingerite
- **Zone:** Supergene/oxidation zone — intermediate stage between early Fe-arsenates and late carbonates/silicates
- **Geological environment:**
  1. **Oxidized copper-arsenic vein deposits:** Weathering of Cu-As sulfide veins in arid to semi-arid climates
  2. **Porphyry copper deposits:** Oxidation zones above primary sulfide ore
  3. **Copper mine dumps and tailings:** Secondary mineralization on waste rock
  4. **Vug fillings in volcanic rocks:** Where Cu and As are leached from volcanic glass or sulfides
  5. **Geothermal areas:** Low-temperature precipitation from hot springs

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1: Wheal Gorland, St Day, Cornwall, England** — The type locality (described by Breithaupt in 1850s). Beautiful emerald-green botryoidal conichalcite coating vug walls, associated with olivenite, clinoclase, and chrysocolla. Classic specimens from this 19th-century copper mine.
- **Classic locality 2: Cap Garonne, Var, France** — World-famous oxidation zone with spectacular conichalcite. Brilliant green botryoidal masses to 5 cm, often with radiating fibrous structure. Associated with pyromorphite, mimetite, and duftite. One of the finest display localities.
- **Classic locality 3: Mapimi, Durango, Mexico** — Exceptional conichalcite in botryoidal and stalactitic forms. Deep emerald green, often associated with hemimorphite, wulfenite, and hydrozincite. Specimens from the Ojuela mine are world-class.
- **Classic locality 4: Broken Hill, New South Wales, Australia** — Famous polymetallic district. Conichalcite in the oxidized zone with cerussite, smithsonite, and pyromorphite.
- **Classic locality 5: Bisbee, Arizona, USA** — Historic copper district. Conichalcite with malachite, azurite, and chrysocolla in the oxidized upper levels of copper sulfide deposits.
- **Notable specimens:**
  - Botryoidal masses to 5 cm from Cap Garonne — among the finest known
  - Wheal Gorland specimens — historical significance, classic Cornwall mineral
  - Mapimi stalactitic forms — rare and visually striking

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally no
- **SW (255nm) color:** None
- **MW (310nm) color:** None
- **LW (365nm) color:** None (or very weak greenish in some specimens from trace impurities)
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** None confirmed; any weak response is from trace impurities, not intrinsic
- **Quenched by:** N/A

### Flavor Text

> **The Green of Arsenic and Copper**
>
> Conichalcite is what you get when copper and arsenic oxidize together in the presence of calcium and water. The result is unmistakable: a vivid emerald green that rivals malachite, a color so saturated it seems almost painted onto the rock. But unlike malachite — which needs carbonate — conichalcite needs arsenate. It is the green of poisoned groundwater, of old mine dumps where sulfides have surrendered their metals to the air.
>
> The mineral forms botryoidal masses: smooth, rounded, grape-like clusters that look like they grew in a living tissue rather than a stone. Cut one open and the interior is fibrous, radiating from a center like the grain of wood or the structure of a kidney. This reniform habit is diagnostic. The surface is waxy, almost greasy, and the color can shift from apple green to deep forest green depending on the exact ratio of copper to calcium and the trace of iron.
>
> Conichalcite is often confused with its close cousin olivenite. Both are copper arsenates. Both are green. But conichalcite contains calcium, which makes it harder (Mohs 4.5 vs 3 for olivenite) and typically brighter in color. Olivenite is the olive-drab older brother; conichalcite is the vivid emerald younger sibling. In a vug, they may even grow together — two shades of green on the same wall, one dull, one brilliant, both born from the same oxidizing fluid.
>
> The mineral needs three things that do not always coincide: copper (from oxidizing sulfides), arsenic (from oxidizing arsenides), and calcium (from dissolving calcite or dolomite). When all three are present in the right proportions, conichalcite crystallizes in the vug's protected hollow, safe from the mechanical erosion that would grind it away. It is a mineral of specific chemistry and fortunate circumstance, a green jewel that marks the exact intersection of three elemental paths.
>
> In Cornwall's Wheal Gorland, where the type specimens were collected in the 1800s, conichalcite lined the vugs alongside clinoclase and chrysocolla — a trinity of green copper minerals each with its own chemistry, its own shade, its own story. The mine is long closed, but the specimens survive in museums and collections: small, heavy, impossibly green, proof that even poison can be beautiful.

- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF conichalcite exists
  AND Ca > 50 ppm
  AND Cu > 25 ppm
  AND As > 25 ppm
  AND As_oxidation_state = +5
  AND temperature < 80°C
  AND pH > 4.5
THEN grow at rate = base_rate * (Ca_ppm / 200) * (Cu_ppm / 100) * (As_ppm / 100)
  where growth is faster in neutral pH, slower in acid
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - Slow growth + high humidity: botryoidal/reniform (classic conichalcite)
  - Faster growth + vug space: prismatic/acicular (rare but possible)
  - Very slow growth + vug walls: drusy coatings
  - Stalactitic: forms in drip conditions (protected overhangs)
- **Decomposition products:**
  - Heat >300°C → dehydroxylated conichalcite + H₂O
  - Heat >500°C → CuO + CaO + As₂O₅ (or As₂O₃ if reducing)
  - Acid dissolution → Cu²⁺ + Ca²⁺ + HAsO₄²⁻ + H₂O

**Formation sequence in copper-arsenic oxidation zone:**
1. Primary stage: chalcopyrite, bornite, chalcocite, arsenopyrite, tennantite (hypogene)
2. Early oxidation: scorodite (Fe-rich, acidic, early)
3. Intermediate oxidation: **olivenite** (Cu-rich, no Ca) → **conichalcite** (Cu+Ca-rich)
4. Late oxidation: malachite/azurite (if CO₃ available), chrysocolla (if Si available)
5. Dehydration/alteration: arseniosiderite (Fe+Ca-rich, very late)

**Relationship to olivenite:**
Conichalcite (CaCu(AsO₄)(OH)) and olivenite (Cu₂(AsO₄)(OH)) are structural analogs. Both are orthorhombic, both are copper arsenates, both are green. The key difference is calcium:
- Olivenite: Cu₂(AsO₄)(OH) — Cu/Cu end-member
- Conichalcite: CaCu(AsO₄)(OH) — Ca/Cu end-member

They can form a solid solution series in theory, but natural specimens usually cluster near one end or the other. In the oxidation zone, olivenite forms where Cu is abundant and Ca is scarce; conichalcite forms where both Cu and Ca are available. The two may coexist in the same vug, representing different microchemical environments.

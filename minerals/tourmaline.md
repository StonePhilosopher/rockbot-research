## Species: Tourmaline

### Identity
- **Formula:** General: (Ca,K,Na,□)(Al,Fe,Li,Mg,Mn)₃(Al,Cr,Fe,V)₆(BO₃)₃(Si,Al,B)₆O₁₈(OH,F)₄
  - **Schorl endmember:** NaFe²⁺₃Al₆Si₆O₁₈(BO₃)₃(OH)₃OH
  - **Dravite endmember:** NaMg₃Al₆Si₆O₁₈(BO₃)₃(OH)₃OH
  - **Elbaite endmember:** Na(Li₁.₅Al₁.₅)Al₆Si₆O₁₈(BO₃)₃(OH)₃OH
  - **Uvite endmember:** CaMg₃(Al₅Mg)Si₆O₁₈(BO₃)₃(OH)₃OH
- **Crystal system:** Trigonal (ditrigonal pyramidal class, 3m)
- **Mineral group:** Cyclosilicate (borosilicate with six-membered SiO₄ rings)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 7.0–7.5
- **Specific gravity:** 3.02–3.32
- **Cleavage:** Indistinct (poor on {1010} and {0001})
- **Fracture:** Uneven, small conchoidal
- **Luster:** Vitreous, sometimes resinous
- **Streak:** White
- **Tenacity:** Brittle
- **Optical:** Uniaxial (−); nω = 1.635–1.675, nε = 1.610–1.650; birefringence δ = −0.018 to −0.040; strong pleochroism

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Schorl: black; Dravite: dark yellow to brownish-black; Elbaite: virtually any color
- **Color causes:**
  - **Black (schorl):** High Fe²⁺ + Fe³⁺ + Ti concentrations, absorbing all visible light
  - **Brown/yellow (dravite):** Fe³⁺ + Ti⁴⁺; Mg-rich base with Fe substitution
  - **Red/pink (rubellite):** Mn²⁺ → Mn³⁺ by natural irradiation (from ⁴⁰K decay in host granite)
  - **Blue/green (indicolite/verdelite):** Fe²⁺ in Y-site; Ti⁴⁺ + Fe²⁺ charge transfer for some greens
  - **Neon blue/green (Paraíba):** Cu²⁺ + Mn²⁺ in elbaite (cuprian elbaite)
  - **Watermelon:** Green exterior (Fe-rich) + pink interior (Mn-rich), reflecting changing fluid chemistry
  - **Colorless (achroite):** Very low transition metal content
- **Transparency:** Translucent to opaque (schorl/dravite); transparent to translucent (gem elbaite)
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Triangular cross-section — no other common mineral has this. Heavy vertical striations on prism faces producing rounded triangular effect. Hemimorphic (asymmetrical) terminations. Strong pleochroism: red stones show dark/light red; green shows dark green/yellow-green; blue shows dark blue/light blue. Piezoelectric and pyroelectric — attracts then repels hot ashes.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Long, slender to thick prismatic and columnar crystals, triangular in cross-section. Striated parallel to c-axis.
- **Common forms/faces:** {1010} prism, {0001} basal pinacoid (often absent or poorly developed), {0111} rhombohedron. Prism faces often curved and striated.
- **Twin laws:** Very rare. Contact twins on {1010} or {0001} reported but extremely uncommon.
- **Varieties (by species + color):**
  - **Schorl:** Black, iron-rich. Most common tourmaline (~95% of all natural tourmaline).
  - **Dravite:** Brown to yellow, magnesium-rich. From metasediments, marbles, calc-silicate rocks.
  - **Elbaite:** Lithium-rich, the gem species. Rubellite (red), indicolite (blue), verdelite (green), achroite (colorless).
  - **Uvite:** Calcium-magnesium tourmaline. Dark green to black, often from skarns and marbles.
  - **Liddicoatite:** Calcium-lithium tourmaline. Famous for spectacular color zoning in triangular "bullseye" patterns (Madagascar).
  - **Foitite:** Vacancy-dominant X-site, iron-aluminum tourmaline. Forms late in pegmatite crystallization.
  - **Olenite:** Sodium-aluminum tourmaline with high Al content. Rare, from high-grade metamorphic rocks.
  - **Tsilaisite:** Manganese-dominant Y-site. Yellow to brown, rare.
  - **Paraíba-type:** Cuprian elbaite. Neon blue/green from Cu²⁺ + Mn²⁺. First from Brazil, now also Mozambique and Nigeria.
- **Special morphologies:** Acicular (needle-like) prisms, sometimes radiating (daisy-like) in aplite. Massive in tourmalinite. Cross-sections show rounded triangular outline. Liddicoatite shows spectacular concentric triangular color zoning. Watermelon tourmaline: green rind, pink core.

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** <150°C (diagenetic/metamorphic) to >900°C (high-grade metamorphic/anatectic). Most gem-quality: 400–700°C.
- **Optimal growth temperature:**
  - **Pegmatite magmatic:** 550–700°C (schorl in border zones, dravite in intermediate zones)
  - **Pegmatite pocket (hydrothermal):** 350–500°C (elbaite, liddicoatite in miarolitic cavities)
  - **Metamorphic/hydrothermal replacement:** 300–600°C (tourmalinization of host rock)
  - **Diagenetic/low-grade metamorphic:** <200°C (some dravite in evaporites and sandstones)
- **Decomposition temperature:** >~900°C in the presence of quartz, tourmaline breaks down to borosilicate glass + cordierite or other phases. Upper stability in B-rich systems extends to >800°C with appropriate fluid composition.
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - High-T magmatic: black schorl, often opaque, small to moderate crystals
  - Mid-T pegmatite: dravite → elbaite progression as melt fractionates
  - Lower-T hydrothermal: gem-quality elbaite in miarolitic pockets — larger, clearer, more colorful
  - Very low-T: rare diagenetic tourmaline in sedimentary rocks

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:**
  - **B:** minimum ~100–500 ppm (boron is the defining element; average crust 10 ppm, so B enrichment required)
  - **Si:** abundant (27.7% of crust)
  - **Al:** significant (8% of crust)
  - **O:** ubiquitous
  - **OH/F:** essential — tourmaline is hydrous
  - **Na or Ca for X-site:** Na (schorl, dravite, elbaite) or Ca (uvite, liddicoatite)
  - **Fe²⁺/Mg/Li for Y-site:** determines species
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - **Li:** drives elbaite (gem) composition; enriches in late-stage fractionated melts
  - **Mn²⁺:** for pink/red color (rubellite); oxidizes to Mn³⁺ by natural irradiation deepening color
  - **Cu²⁺:** for Paraíba-type neon blue/green
  - **Cr³⁺:** for chrome-dravite (deep green)
  - **V³⁺:** for vanadium-dravite (green)
  - **Ti⁴⁺:** common accessory; contributes to dark colors when combined with Fe²⁺
  - **Cs, Rb:** trace elements in highly fractionated pegmatite tourmalines
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - **Very low B:** tourmaline cannot form without boron enrichment
  - **High Ca in schorl/dravite systems:** drives uvite/liddicoatite instead
  - **High Fe³⁺/Ti⁴⁺:** produces dark, opaque schorl rather than gem elbaite

#### Required pH / Eh / O₂ Range
- **pH:** 4–8 (acidic to mildly alkaline). Pegmatite melts are mildly acidic; hydrothermal fluids can range widely.
- **Eh:** Variable. Magmatic systems: moderately reducing (Fe²⁺ stable). Late hydrothermal/oxidation zone: more oxidizing (Mn²⁺ → Mn³⁺, Cu⁺ → Cu²⁺ for Paraíba color).
- **O₂:** Low O₂ for Fe²⁺-rich schorl/dravite. Higher O₂ for Mn³⁺-colored rubellite and Cu²⁺-colored Paraíba.

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release chemicals when forming?** Tourmaline is generally a sink rather than a source. However:
  - Boron in tourmaline is sequestered, depleting surrounding fluid in B.
  - Incongruent breakdown at very high T releases B, Si, Al into melt/fluid.
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** At >900°C, tourmaline + quartz → borosilicate melt + cordierite/mullite depending on composition. In weathering, tourmaline is highly resistant — can survive multiple sedimentary cycles as a heavy mineral.

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate to slow. Faster than quartz under supersaturated B-rich conditions, slower than many sulfides. Gem-quality elbaite grows slowly in pocket environments over weeks to months (geologically).
- **Maximum crystal size:** Up to 1+ meters for schorl (reported from Namibia and Brazil). Gem elbaite: up to ~20 cm (exceptional). Paraíba: usually <5 cm.
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 0.5–10 cm for gem elbaite in miarolitic cavities. Needle-schorl: mm-scale in aplite. Dravite: 1–5 cm in marbles and calc-silicates.
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes. Higher T = faster nucleation but smaller crystals (undercooling). Lower T pocket conditions = slower growth, larger, clearer crystals.
- **Competes with:**
  - **Quartz:** both use Si; quartz dominates if B is insufficient
  - **Feldspar:** for Al and Na
  - **Muscovite/Lepidolite:** for Al, K, Li in late-stage pegmatites
  - **Spodumene:** for Li in Li-rich pegmatites
  - **Boron minerals (danburite, datolite):** compete for B in non-silicate systems

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes. Upper thermal stability ~800–900°C with quartz; higher in B-rich systems. Breakdown products: cordierite, mullite, borosilicate glass.
- **Breaks down in light?** No — tourmaline is photostable. However, some irradiation-induced colors (Mn³⁺ pink in rubellite) can fade with prolonged UV exposure or heating above ~400°C.
- **Dissolves in water?** Very low solubility. Tourmaline is among the most resistant minerals to chemical weathering. Survives as detrital grains.
- **Dissolves in acid?** Resistant to most acids. HF attacks silicates slowly. Not a significant concern.
- **Oxidizes?** Fe²⁺ in schorl can oxidize to Fe³⁺ altering color. Mn²⁺ oxidizes to Mn³⁺ deepening pink tones.
- **Dehydrates?** Tourmaline loses OH only at very high T (>~700°C), structurally breaking down. Unlike gypsum, it does not have a simple dehydration product.
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Generally resistant. Natural gamma irradiation from host granite (⁴⁰K, U, Th) is what deepens pink tourmaline color over geological time by oxidizing Mn²⁺ → Mn³⁺.

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** In pegmatites, tourmaline crystallizes throughout the sequence:
  - Early: schorl (border zone, with quartz + feldspar + muscovite)
  - Intermediate: dravite (as melt fractionates, Mg component)
  - Late: elbaite + liddicoatite (core zone, pocket minerals, with lepidolite + cleavelandite + spodumene + beryl)
- **Forms BEFORE:** In late-stage pegmatites, tourmaline is one of the last major silicates before pocket fluids precipitate gem minerals.
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - **Pegmatite:** quartz, K-feldspar, albite, muscovite, lepidolite, spodumene, beryl, cassiterite, columbite
  - **Marble/skarn:** calcite, diopside, phlogopite, scapolite, uvite (Ca-Mg tourmaline)
  - **Metamorphic:** garnet, staurolite, kyanite, sillimanite, biotite (tourmalinization replaces biotite)
  - **Hydrothermal:** quartz, sulfides (pyrite, chalcopyrite), fluorite
  - **Sedimentary:** quartz, zircon, rutile (as heavy mineral suite)
- **Zone:**
  - Primary magmatic/hypogene: schorl in granite and pegmatite border zones
  - Late magmatic/hydrothermal: elbaite in miarolitic cavities
  - Metamorphic: dravite in metapelites, marbles, calc-silicates
  - Supergene/weathering: tourmaline survives as resistant residual mineral
- **Geological environment:**
  - **Granitic pegmatites:** primary source of gem tourmaline (elbaite, liddicoatite)
  - **Granite aureoles:** tourmalinization of country rock by B-rich fluids
  - **Metamorphic rocks:** schists, gneisses, marbles (dravite, uvite)
  - **Tourmalinite:** B-rich metasomatic rock composed almost entirely of tourmaline
  - **Placer deposits:** detrital tourmaline in sands and gravels

### Famous Localities
- **Himalaya Mine, San Diego Co., California, USA:** One of the most productive gem tourmaline mines in history. ~250,000 pounds of specimens extracted. Famous for pink rubellite, green verdelite, bicolor crystals. The "89 Pocket" (1989) produced exceptional specimens. Associated with lepidolite, cleavelandite, spodumene.
- **Pala / Stewart Lithia Mine, San Diego Co., California, USA:** Historic source of California pink tourmaline. Chinese Dowager Empress Tz'u-Hsi loved pink tourmaline from here; much of Pala's early production was shipped to China. Operated by Pala International since 1970s. Associated with spodumene, kunzite, morganite.
- **Jonas Mine, Conselheiro Pena, Minas Gerais, Brazil:** World-class source of rubellite (red elbaite). Spectacular gem crystals, often associated with lepidolite and albite. One of Brazil's most important pegmatite districts.
- **São José da Batalha, Paraíba, Brazil:** Type locality for cuprian elbaite ("Paraíba tourmaline"). Discovered late 1980s. Neon blue/green color from Cu²⁺ + Mn²⁺. Now among the most valuable gemstones per carat. Similar material later found in Mozambique (Mavuco) and Nigeria.
- **Antsirabé, Madagascar:** Famous for liddicoatite with spectacular concentric triangular "bullseye" color zoning. Also dravite and uvite from metamorphic marbles.
- **Maine, USA (Mt. Mica, Newry):** Historic source of fine tourmaline. Mt. Mica first discovered in 1820 by Elijah Hamlin and Ezekiel Holmes. Maine tourmaline is the state gemstone. Colors: pink, green, bicolor, watermelon.
- **Urals, Russia:** Historic source of red rubellite ("Siberian ruby"). Also dravite from the Turinsk area.
- **Sri Lanka (Ceylon):** Alluvial gem tourmaline, including rare colorless achroite and fine green/blue elbaite. The name "tourmaline" derives from Sinhalese "tōramalli" (carnelian).
- **Namibia (Erongo, Karibib):** Schorl crystals up to 1 meter long from pegmatites. Also fine aquamarine, schorl, and elbaite. Famous for giant black tourmaline sprays with aquamarine and fluorite.
- **Afghanistan (Nuristan):** Fine rubellite and blue/green elbaite from pegmatites in the Hindu Kush.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally inert to very weak. Most tourmaline does NOT fluoresce under standard UV lamps.
- **SW (255nm):** Inert to very weak red-violet in some pink stones (LW same)
- **MW (310nm):** Inert to very weak
- **LW (365nm):** Very weak red to violet in some pink/red rubellite; inert in most other colors
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** Mn²⁺ in some pink stones may produce very weak luminescence. However, strong fluorescence in tourmaline is extremely rare and usually indicates artificial treatment or a different mineral entirely.
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺ suppresses any weak luminescence in dark-colored varieties.
- **Special note:** Paraíba tourmaline and some Mn-rich pink stones may show very weak red/orange luminescence under SW UV due to Mn²⁺ activator. Most gemological references classify tourmaline as "inert" under UV.

```
IF trace_B > 200 AND temperature < 700 AND temperature > 150:

  ELSE IF trace_Mg > trace_Fe AND trace_Na > 10:

  ELSE IF trace_Fe > trace_Mg AND trace_Na > 10:

  ELSE IF trace_Ca > trace_Na AND trace_Mg > 20:
    nucleate uvite (max 2 per zone, skarn/marble context)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
σ_tourmaline = f(trace_B, temperature, pH, trace_Na/trace_Ca)
IF σ_tourmaline > 1.0 AND trace_B > 100:
  IF species == elbaite:
    grow at rate 2.5 (moderate; Li enrichment required)
  ELSE IF species == schorl:
    grow at rate 4.0 (faster; Fe abundant)
  ELSE IF species == dravite:
    grow at rate 3.5 (moderate-fast; Mg common)
  ELSE IF species == uvite:
    grow at rate 2.0 (slower; Ca-Mg specific)
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - High B + high Fe + low Li → schorl (black, prismatic, common)
  - High B + high Mg + moderate Na → dravite (brown, prismatic)
  - High B + high Li + moderate Na → elbaite (gem, various colors)
  - High B + high Ca + high Mg → uvite (skarn/marble context)
  - Very high Li + very low Fe → achroite (colorless elbaite)
  - Cu > 20 + Mn > 10 in elbaite → Paraíba-type (neon blue/green)
  - Mn²⁺ in elbaite + oxidizing late fluid → rubellite (red/pink)
  - Fe²⁺ + Ti⁴⁺ in elbaite → indicolite/verdelite (blue/green)
- **Decomposition products:**
  - At >900°C with quartz: borosilicate melt + cordierite
  - At >900°C without quartz: borosilicate melt + mullite
  - Tourmaline does NOT decompose at simulator temperatures (<600°C); it is stable throughout all game scenarios

### Variants for Game

- **Variant 1: Schorl (Black Tourmaline)**
  - *What makes it different:* Iron-dominant, opaque black, most common. High Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺ + Ti. Nucleates easily, grows fast, competes for Fe with pyrite and hematite.
  - *Conditions:* trace_Fe > 200, trace_B > 200, trace_Na > 10, trace_Li < 30. Temperature 300–700°C. Any pH.

- **Variant 2: Dravite (Brown/Yellow Tourmaline)**
  - *What makes it different:* Magnesium-dominant, brown to yellow-brown. From metasediments, marbles, calc-silicates. Lower value, more "earthy" aesthetic.
  - *Conditions:* trace_Mg > trace_Fe, trace_B > 200, trace_Na > 10, trace_Li < 30. Temperature 300–600°C. Often in metamorphic/scenario contexts.

- **Variant 3: Elbaite (Gem Tourmaline — Rubellite/Indicolite/Verdelite)**
  - *What makes it different:* Lithium-rich, transparent to translucent, gem-quality. Color depends on chromophores. Most valuable tourmaline variant.
  - *Conditions:* trace_Li > 50, trace_B > 300, trace_Na > 10, trace_Al > 100. Temperature 350–550°C (pocket conditions). pH 5–7.
  - *Sub-variants by chromophore:*
    - Rubellite: trace_Mn > 20, oxidizing late fluid (Mn³⁺)
    - Indicolite: trace_Fe > 30 (Fe²⁺), low Mn
    - Verdelite: trace_Fe > 30 + trace_Ti > 5, or trace_Cr > 10
    - Achroite: very low all chromophores (<10 each)

- **Variant 4: Paraíba-type (Cuprian Elbaite)**
  - *What makes it different:* Neon blue to green, Cu²⁺-colored. Most valuable per carat. Rare.
  - *Conditions:* trace_Cu > 20, trace_Mn > 10, trace_Li > 50, trace_B > 300. Temperature 350–500°C. Requires oxidizing conditions for Cu²⁺.

- **Variant 5: Liddicoatite (Bullseye Tourmaline)**
  - *What makes it different:* Calcium-lithium species with spectacular concentric triangular color zoning. The "bullseye" pattern is diagnostic.
  - *Conditions:* trace_Ca > trace_Na, trace_Li > 50, trace_B > 300. Temperature 400–550°C. Madagascar-style pegmatite context.

- **Variant 6: Watermelon Tourmaline**
  - *What makes it different:* Bicolor — green exterior (Fe-rich early growth) + pink interior (Mn-rich late growth). A single crystal recording two fluid chemistries.
  - *Conditions:* Starts with indicolite/verdelite conditions (trace_Fe high), then fluid shifts to rubellite conditions (trace_Mn rises, trace_Fe drops) mid-growth. Very rare, requires changing fluid chemistry during single crystal growth.

## Implementation Blockers

| Blocker | Element | Status |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Boron (B) not in trace system | B | **Critical** — needed for ALL tourmaline species |
| Lithium (Li) not in trace system | Li | **Critical** — needed for elbaite, liddicoatite, spodumene |
| Aluminum (Al) not in trace system | Al | Medium — needed for structural formula, but Al is abundant enough that a generic Al parameter may suffice |
| Calcium (Ca) not in trace system | Ca | Medium — needed for uvite and liddicoatite |

**Priority:** Add **B** and **Li** to the trace element system before tourmaline can be implemented. These are already on the "Elements Needing Addition" list in `proposals/vugg-mineral-species-list.md`.

Boron is the single most limiting element for tourmaline — average crustal abundance is only ~10 ppm, so any tourmaline-bearing system requires significant B enrichment (typically 100–1000× crustal average). This makes tourmaline a powerful indicator of B-rich fluid sources (evaporites, marine sediments, or highly fractionated granitic melts).

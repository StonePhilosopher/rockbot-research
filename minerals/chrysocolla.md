# Research: Chrysocolla — Cu₂₋ₓAlₓ(H₂₋ₓSi₂O₅)(OH)₄·nH₂O

## Species: Chrysocolla

### Identity
- **Formula:** Cu₂₋ₓAlₓ(H₂₋ₓSi₂O₅)(OH)₄·nH₂O (x < 1) — or (Cu,Al)₂H₂Si₂O₅(OH)₄·nH₂O. Variable Al-for-Cu substitution and variable hydration make this a mineraloid as much as a mineral.
- **Crystal system:** Orthorhombic (rarely demonstrated by XRD); typically **amorphous** or microcrystalline. A 2006 spectrographic study (Farges et al.) suggests much material identified as chrysocolla may be a submicroscopic mixture of copper hydroxide spertiniite + amorphous silica + water.
- **Mineral group:** Phyllosilicate (kaolinite-serpentine group) — when crystalline; mineraloid when amorphous
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 2.5–3.5 for typical chrysocolla; up to ~6–7 for heavily **silicified** material ("chrysocolla chalcedony" or "gem silica"). The hardness range is extraordinary and directly proportional to silica content.
- **Specific gravity:** 1.9–2.4 (unusually low for a copper mineral; feels light in the hand)
- **Cleavage:** None
- **Fracture:** Irregular to sub-conchoidal
- **Luster:** Vitreous to dull; waxy when massive; silky when fibrous

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Cyan, blue-green, green, dark blue to blackish blue; rarely brown or yellow. The color range is broader than any other copper silicate.
- **Color causes:** Cu²⁺ in octahedral and distorted coordination within the silicate framework. Higher Cu content pushes toward deep navy; higher Si content lightens to cyan or green. Iron and manganese traces can darken or brown the color.
- **Transparency:** Translucent to opaque; thin edges may be translucent
- **Streak:** White to blue-green
- **Notable visual features:** Often shows concentric color banding (blue-green-white), botryoidal surfaces that look like frozen drips, or a fine spiderweb matrix pattern when silicified. The "Eilat stone" is a brecciated mixture of chrysocolla + turquoise + malachite + azurite in swirling patterns.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Botryoidal (grape-like) masses, crusts, and vein fillings. True crystals are virtually unknown.
- **Common forms/faces:** None — amorphous or cryptocrystalline
- **Twin laws:** None
- **Varieties:**
  - **Chrysocolla chalcedony / Gem silica:** Heavily silicified, translucent, intensely colored blue-green to apple-green; hard enough for jewelry (approaches 7). The premium lapidary material.
  - **Eilat stone:** Brecciated mixture with turquoise, malachite, azurite from Israel's Timna Valley; national stone of Israel.
  - **Silica-blue chrysocolla:** High-silica, pale blue variety; often powdery or earthy.
- **Special morphologies:**
  - Stalactitic (pipe-like drip formations in vugs)
  - Fibrous (velvet-like coatings)
  - Massive (solid replacement bodies)
  - **Pseudomorphs after azurite/malachite** — double and triple pseudomorphs are classic from Congo (Lupoto Mine) and Australia (Whim Creek)

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 15–100°C (very broad — the broadest of all copper silicates)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 30–60°C
- **Decomposition temperature:** Above ~600°C chrysocolla becomes less amenable to acid leaching, supposedly forming insoluble copper silicates. Structural breakdown begins earlier (~300°C) with dehydration.
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Higher temperatures favor more massive, compact forms; lower temperatures favor botryoidal and stalactitic growths.

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:**
  - Cu: >20 ppm (lower threshold than dioptase/shattuckite due to amorphous structure and less strict stoichiometry)
  - SiO₂: >10 ppm (lower than dioptase — amorphous phases tolerate lower silica)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - Al: promotes crystallinity (higher Al = more ordered structure, higher hardness)
  - Ca: buffers acidity, stabilizes high pH for silica mobilization
  - Carbonate (CO₃²⁻): buffers acid from sulfide oxidation; chrysocolla often forms alongside malachite/azurite
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - Very high Fe: competes for Si, favors goethite/hematite
  - Strongly acidic pH (<5): suppresses silica mobilization and Cu²⁺ solubility
  - Very high SO₄²⁻: maintains acidity, but less inhibiting to chrysocolla than to dioptase
- **Required pH range:** 6.5–9.5 (broader than dioptase or shattuckite; can form at mildly acidic to moderately alkaline conditions)
- **Required Eh range:** Oxidizing (+0.3 to +0.7 V) — Cu must be Cu²⁺
- **Required O₂ range:** Moderate to high — needs oxygenated groundwater

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No major byproducts; consumes Cu²⁺, SiO₂, and OH⁻ from solution
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Minor H⁺ release (slight acidification of local fluid)
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Dissolves in acid to release Cu²⁺ (toxic), silica gel, and water

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate — faster than dioptase (crystalline), comparable to shattuckite, slower than malachite/azurite
- **Maximum crystal size:** As a mineraloid, does not form euhedral crystals; botryoidal masses up to 30 cm reported; stalactites up to 10 cm
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–20 mm botryoidal masses; crusts up to 5 mm thick
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Growth accelerates with temperature but the amorphous structure means less temperature sensitivity than crystalline copper silicates
- **Competes with:** Dioptase (for Cu and Si at high pH), shattuckite (for Cu and Si at moderate pH), malachite/azurite (for Cu), plancheite (for Cu and Si), quartz (for Si)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Begins dehydrating above 300°C; structural degradation by 500–600°C
- **Breaks down in light?** No — color stable in light
- **Dissolves in water?** Very slightly; more soluble in acidic water
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — dissolves in HCl and H₂SO₄, though silicified varieties resist longer due to silica armor. Acid dissolution leaves a silica gel residue (the "rind" effect).
- **Oxidizes?** No — Cu is already fully oxidized to Cu²⁺
- **Dehydrates?** Yes, progressively above 300°C
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Not particularly radiation-sensitive

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Primary copper sulfides (chalcopyrite, chalcocite, bornite) must oxidize to release Cu²⁺; silica must be available (from host rock dissolution or hydrothermal fluid)
- **Forms BEFORE:** In arid climates, chrysocolla is stable and may persist as the final copper mineral. In wet climates, may alter to malachite or be replaced by later silica deposition (silicification). Can pseudomorph after azurite and malachite, indicating it forms late in the oxidation sequence.
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - **Within copper oxidation zone:** Malachite, azurite, cuprite, native copper, tenorite, brochantite, antlerite, chalcanthite
  - **Within copper silicate group:** Dioptase (at higher pH), shattuckite (at similar pH), plancheite
  - **Other common associates:** Quartz, limonite, goethite, hematite, calcite, iron oxides
- **Zone:** Supergene/oxidation zone — the broadest pH tolerance means it appears across more of the oxidation zone than dioptase or shattuckite
- **Geological environment:** Desert/arid-region copper deposits; also forms in wetter climates but is more leached and less preserved. Particularly common in:
  - Porphyry copper deposits (Arizona, Chile)
  - Volcanogenic massive sulfide (VMS) deposits
  - Sandstone-hosted copper deposits
  - Copper skarns

### Famous Localities
- **Morenci / Ray Mine, Arizona, USA:** Classic US locality; deep blue-green botryoidal masses and crusts on quartz matrix. The Ray mine produced some of the finest Arizona chrysocolla specimens.
- **Bisbee, Arizona, USA:** Banded blue-green and white chrysocolla, often associated with malachite and azurite. Historic copper district with exceptional secondary mineralogy.
- **Lupoto Mine, DR Congo:** Source of the famous double-pseudomorphs: chrysocolla after malachite after azurite. Bladed azurite crystals first pseudomorphed to malachite, then to brilliant turquoise-blue chrysocolla. Highly sought by collectors.
- **Whim Creek, Western Australia:** Another classic pseudomorph locality; chrysocolla after azurite/malachite.
- **Iquique Province, Chile:** Powder-blue stalactitic chrysocolla in vugs inside tyrolite boulders; unusual formation in a nearly solid copper arsenate matrix.
- **Katanga Copperbelt, DR Congo:** Massive chrysocolla as a copper ore mineral; important economic locality.
- **Timna Valley, Israel:** Source of Eilat stone (mixed chrysocolla + turquoise + malachite + azurite); legendary association with King Solomon's mines.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally **no** — non-fluorescent in UV
- **SW (255nm) color:** None
- **MW (310nm) color:** None
- **LW (365nm) color:** None
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** N/A
- **Quenched by:** N/A
- **Note:** The presence of uranium traces in some specimens (particularly from Arizona) may cause weak fluorescence, but this is not characteristic of the mineral.

### Flavor Text

But chrysocolla is its own treasure: a blue-green gel frozen in time, the amorphous edge of the copper oxidation zone where chemistry ran out of patience before it ran out of copper. It forms botryoidal masses that look like frozen drips, or replaces azurite blade-for-blade in pseudomorphs so perfect they keep the ghost-shape of a mineral that no longer lives there. Heavily silicified, it becomes gem silica — hard enough to cut, translucent enough to glow. From King Solomon's mines to the Ray Mine in Arizona, chrysocolla is the color of oxidized copper refusing to choose between blue and green.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** `silica_saturation` — chrysocolla nucleates at lower silica than dioptase; `aluminum_content` — higher Al promotes harder, more crystalline varieties
- **New events needed:** `silicification_event` — late-stage silica deposition can harden and clarify chrysocolla into gem silica
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF Cu > 20 ppm AND SiO₂ > 10 ppm AND pH > 6.5 AND pH < 9.5 AND Eh > +0.3V AND T < 100°C:
    nucleate chrysocolla
    // Broader pH and lower concentration thresholds than dioptase/shattuckite
    // Amorphous structure = less chemically picky
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF chrysocolla_nucleated AND Cu > 15 ppm AND SiO₂ > 5 ppm AND pH > 6.0 AND Eh > +0.2V:
    grow at rate = 0.5 * base_rate  // Moderate grower
    // Growth is less temperature-sensitive than crystalline copper silicates
    IF silica_content > 200 ppm AND late_stage:
        transform toward chrysocolla_chalcedony  // Silicification path
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
```
IF vug_surface AND dripping_conditions:
    habit = "stalactitic"  // Pipe-like drip formations
ELSEIF vug_wall AND moderate_supersaturation:
    habit = "botryoidal"  // Grape-like masses
ELSEIF replacing_previous_mineral AND Cu_rich:
    habit = "pseudomorph"  // After azurite/malachite
ELSEIF high_Si AND low_T:
    habit = "crust_fibrous"  // Velvet-like coatings
ELSEIF massive_replacement:
    habit = "massive"  // Solid replacement bodies
```
- **Decomposition products:** Amorphous silica + CuO (tenorite) + water at high temperature
- **Pseudomorph mechanics:** Chrysocolla can pseudomorph after azurite and malachite. Implementation: if azurite or malachite exists in the vug AND chrysocolla nucleates nearby, there is a probability (increasing with time and Cu concentration) that chrysocolla replaces the earlier mineral while preserving its crystal habit.

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Arizona Blue** — Deep blue-green botryoidal masses from Morenci/Ray; classic copper-country chrysocolla; nucleates at moderate pH (7.0–8.0), moderate Cu (30–80 ppm)
- **Variant 2: Gem Silica** — Heavily silicified, translucent apple-green to blue-green; hard (6–7), glows when backlit. Requires late-stage silica flooding event (`silicification_event`) after initial chrysocolla formation. The "prize" lapidary variant.
- **Variant 3: Congo Pseudomorph** — Chrysocolla after malachite after azurite; preserves bladed azurite crystal shape in brilliant turquoise-blue. Requires azurite → malachite sequence first, then chrysocolla replacement. Rare and valuable.
- **Variant 4: Eilat Stone** — Brecciated mixed-copper-mineral variety; swirls of chrysocolla + turquoise + malachite + azurite. Requires multiple copper minerals present simultaneously + brecciation event.
- **Variant 5: Desert Crust** — Powdery, pale blue, low-silica variety; forms in arid conditions with limited silica. Low hardness (2.5–3), low value, but common.

## Paragenetic Note: Chrysocolla in the Copper Silicate Group

Chrysocolla occupies the broad, forgiving center of the copper silicate paragenesis. Where dioptase demands strict alkaline pH (8.5–10.5) and shattuckite prefers moderate alkalinity (7.5–8.5), chrysocolla will form across pH 6.5–9.5 — even overlapping into mildly acidic conditions where silica is barely mobile. This is the secret of its ubiquity: it is the mineralogical equivalent of a generalist species.

**Paragenetic sequence within the copper silicate subgroup (broad strokes):**

```
Primary sulfide oxidation
        │
        ▼
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  pH 6.5–7.5          pH 7.5–8.5          pH 8.5–10.5  │
  │     │                    │                    │       │
  │     ▼                    ▼                    ▼       │
  │  Chrysocolla         Shattuckite           Dioptase   │
  │  (amorphous)         (chain silicate)      (cyclosilicate)
  │  ├─ low Si            ├─ moderate Si       ├─ high Si  │
  │  ├─ broad pH          ├─ narrower pH       ├─ strict pH│
  │  └─ low T tolerance   └─ moderate T        └─ low T    │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Chrysocolla also forms **after** the crystalline copper silicates in some sequences — a late-stage amorphous or replacement phase that fills remaining porosity or replaces earlier minerals pseudomorphically. The double-pseudomorphs (chrysocolla after malachite after azurite) are the textbook example: each step represents decreasing carbonate and increasing silica in the fluid.

**Fluid evolution path:**
```
Early oxidation (acidic, high CO₂):
  Azurite → Malachite
        │
        ▼
Carbonate depleted, silica mobilized (alkaline):
  Malachite → Chrysocolla (replacement)
        │
        ▼
Late silica flooding:
  Chrysocolla → Gem silica (silicification)
```

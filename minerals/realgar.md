## Species: Realgar

### Identity
- **Formula:** As₄S₄ (often written historically as AsS; α-As₄S₄ is the stable form)
- **Crystal system:** Monoclinic
- **Mineral group:** Sulfide
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 1.5–2
- **Specific gravity:** 3.48–3.64
- **Cleavage:** Good on {010}
- **Fracture:** Sectile (soft enough to cut with a knife), uneven
- **Luster:** Resinous to greasy
- **Space group:** P2₁/n (no. 14)
- **Unit cell:** a = 9.325 Å, b = 13.571 Å, c = 6.587 Å, β = 106.40°; Z = 16
- **IMA symbol:** Rlg

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Bright orange-red to scarlet, ruby-red; occasionally reddish-brown
- **Color causes:** Charge transfer between As and S in the As₄S₄ cage structure; the As-As bonds and As-S bonds create the intense red-orange color
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent in thin fragments; commonly opaque in thicker masses
- **Streak:** Orange-red to scarlet
- **Notable visual features:** Crystals often have a diamond-like cross section when viewed down the prism axis. Massive material can be intensely colored and waxy. The color is one of the most vivid in the mineral kingdom — true "ruby sulfur."

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Prismatic crystals elongated along [001], striated parallel to length; also massive, granular, or powdery
- **Common forms/faces:** Prismatic with prominent {010} and {110} faces
- **Twin laws:** Contact twins on {100}
- **Varieties:** None significant; though pararealgar (a polymorph, not a variety) is produced by light alteration
- **Special morphorphologies:** Incrustations, botryoidal masses, compact granular; rarely as well-formed crystals to 12 cm

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 50–300°C (low-temperature hydrothermal; volcanic sublimates can form at even lower temperatures)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 100–200°C
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~267°C (α-β realgar transition); melts at ~320°C. Thermally stable to relatively high temperatures for such a soft mineral.
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Lower temperatures favor incrustations and powdery forms; higher temperatures allow prismatic crystals to develop

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** As (minimum ~1000 ppm as As-sulfide species), S (minimum ~500 ppm)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Sb (forms solid solution toward getchellite), Hg (associates in Carlin-type deposits), Tl (Allchar-type deposits)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Fe favors pyrite/marcasite over arsenic sulfides; high Cu pushes toward enargite/tennantite; high Pb toward galena
- **Required pH range:** Acidic to mildly acidic, pH 2–6. Strongly acidic conditions favor arsenic sulfide precipitation.
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing to mildly reducing. Realgar requires reduced sulfur (S²⁻ or H₂S-dominated fluid).
- **Required O₂ range:** Very low — realgar is unstable under oxidizing conditions; oxidizes to arsenolite (As₂O₃) or scorodite (FeAsO₄·2H₂O) in the presence of O₂ + Fe

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No significant byproducts during nucleation.
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Consumes As and S from fluid.
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Light decomposition produces pararealgar (same formula, different structure) + arsenolite (As₂O₃) if oxygen present. Thermal decomposition above 267°C produces β-realgar; above melting point releases arsenic and sulfur vapor.

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate to fast — softer minerals often grow quickly due to low lattice energy.
- **Maximum crystal size:** Up to 12 cm in exceptional specimens (e.g., Macedonia)
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–10 mm; incrustations up to several cm
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Growth accelerates at higher temperatures within stability window; above 267°C the α→β transition disrupts crystal integrity.
- **Competes with:** Orpiment (As₂S₃) — the two are paragenetically linked; stibnite; native arsenic; arsenopyrite in higher-T veins.

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes — α→β transition at ~267°C; melts at ~320°C.
- **Breaks down in light?** **YES — this is the defining stability issue.** Exposure to light (wavelengths 500–670 nm, especially sunlight) transforms realgar to pararealgar, a yellow polymorph. In air, further oxidation produces arsenolite (white As₂O₃). Complete transformation can occur in hours of direct sunlight.
- **Dissolves in water?** Very low solubility; slowly weathers in moist air.
- **Dissolves in acid?** Soluble in nitric acid and alkaline sulfide solutions.
- **Oxidizes?** Yes — oxidizes to arsenolite (As₂O₃) in air, especially when combined with light exposure.
- **Dehydrates?** No hydrates.
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Not particularly radiation-sensitive; radioactivity in associated minerals (e.g., uranium deposits) may accelerate alteration.

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Pyrite, arsenopyrite (in higher-temperature stages); native arsenic can precede or intergrow
- **Forms BEFORE:** Orpiment can replace realgar during late-stage alteration; calcite, barite, fluorite commonly fill fractures later
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Orpiment (ubiquitous partner), stibnite, cinnabar, native arsenic, native sulfur, getchellite, lorandite (Allchar), pyrite, marcasite, quartz, barite, calcite, fluorite, gold (Carlin-type)
- **Zone:** Epithermal / low-temperature hydrothermal; volcanic fumaroles; hot springs; Carlin-type gold deposit alteration halos
- **Geological environment:** Low-temperature hydrothermal veins; volcanic fumarole sublimates; hot spring deposits; Carlin-type disseminated gold systems (Nevada); epithermal precious metal veins

### Famous Localities
- **Getchell Mine, Nevada, USA:** The modern classic — massive red realgar intergrown with yellow orpiment, stibnite, and gold. Type locality for getchellite. Carlin-type gold deposit.
- **Allchar (Alšar) Deposit, North Macedonia:** Historic source of exceptional realgar crystals and the type locality for lorandite (TlAsS₂). Low-temperature As-Tl-Sb hydrothermal system.
- **Shikoku, Japan:** Classic Japanese locality for realgar in epithermal gold-silver veins.
- **Notable specimens:** The Getchell specimens are world-famous for color intensity and size. Allchar produced the best euhedral crystals. The mineral was known to ancient Greeks and Chinese — used as a pigment ("ruby sulfur") and in fireworks.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally no — realgar is not known for strong fluorescence. Some specimens may show weak response due to trace impurities or associated minerals.
- **SW (255nm) color:** None typically
- **MW (310nm) color:** None typically
- **LW (365nm) color:** None typically
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** None known
- **Quenched by:** Not applicable

## Paragenetic Sequence Notes (Realgar ↔ Orpiment)

Realgar and orpiment share the same formation environment — low-temperature, As-S-rich, acidic, reducing fluids. They differ in their As:S ratio and temperature sensitivity:

- **Realgar** = As₄S₄ (As:S = 1:1) — stable at slightly higher temperatures, more As-rich
- **Orpiment** = As₂S₃ (As:S = 2:3) — stable at lower temperatures, more S-rich

In nature, orpiment commonly replaces realgar during late-stage alteration (fluid becomes more S-rich or cools). The transition is:
```
Realgar (As₄S₄) + S → Orpiment (As₂S₃) + As
```

Or conversely, realgar can form from orpiment under As-rich conditions. In the game, both should check for the other's presence as a paragenetic indicator — finding one should raise the probability of the other nucleating nearby.

Both minerals are **light-sensitive** — a unique shared mechanic. Realgar transforms to pararealgar; orpiment is more stable but can still alter to arsenolite over long exposure. This makes them "curator's minerals" — beautiful but demanding.

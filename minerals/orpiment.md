## Species: Orpiment

### Identity
- **Formula:** As₂S₃
- **Crystal system:** Monoclinic
- **Mineral group:** Sulfide
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 1.5–2
- **Specific gravity:** 3.46–3.49
- **Cleavage:** Perfect on {010}
- **Fracture:** Sectile, flexible in thin sheets; foliated
- **Luster:** Pearly on cleavage faces; resinous to waxy on other surfaces
- **Space group:** P2₁/n
- **Unit cell:** a = 11.49 Å, b = 9.59 Å, c = 4.25 Å, β = 90.45°; Z = 4
- **IMA symbol:** Orp

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Lemon-yellow to golden-yellow, canary yellow, occasionally orange-yellow
- **Color causes:** The As₂S₃ layer structure absorbs blue light strongly; the sulfur-arsenic bonding in the sheet-like lattice produces the intense yellow color. Higher grades of orpiment are among the most vividly colored minerals known.
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent in thin plates; foliated aggregates are translucent
- **Streak:** Lemon-yellow to pale yellow
- **Notable visual features:** Perfect foliation — cleavage sheets can be peeled like mica, but are softer and more flexible. The color is so intense that powdered orpiment was used as a pigment ("king's yellow") in ancient times, though its toxicity made it dangerous. Crystals are rare; massive or foliated forms dominate.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Foliated columnar or fibrous aggregates; massive; rarely as prismatic crystals
- **Common forms/faces:** When crystalline, prismatic with prominent {010} cleavage; crystals to 10 cm recorded
- **Twin laws:** On {100}
- **Varieties:** None significant, though color intensity varies with purity
- **Special morphologies:** Reniform or botryoidal masses; granular; powdery; earthy coatings. The foliated structure gives it a distinctive "silky" appearance when fresh.

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 50–250°C (low-temperature hydrothermal; lower than realgar)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 100–180°C
- **Decomposition temperature:** Melts at 300–325°C; boils at 707°C without decomposition
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Lower temperatures favor botryoidal and reniform masses; moderate temperatures allow foliated columnar growth; higher temperatures within stability window produce larger crystal faces

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** As (minimum ~800 ppm), S (minimum ~1000 ppm) — orpiment is more sulfur-rich than realgar
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Sb (minor substitution), Tl (Allchar-type paragenesis), Hg (associates in Carlin systems)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Fe favors pyrite/marcasite; high Cu → enargite/tennantite; high Pb → galena; high CO₃ may suppress sulfide formation
- **Required pH range:** Acidic, pH 2–5.5 — orpiment requires even more acidic conditions than realgar
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing; S must be present as S²⁻ or H₂S
- **Required O₂ range:** Very low — oxidizes readily to arsenolite (As₂O₃) + sulfuric acid in air

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No significant byproducts.
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Consumes As and S from fluid.
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Oxidation produces arsenolite (As₂O₃, white) + sulfuric acid. The acid can drive further alteration of the vug wall rock. In air: 2 As₂S₃ + 9 O₂ → 2 As₂O₃ + 6 SO₂ (then SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₄).

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate to fast — soft minerals with layered structures grow readily from supersaturated fluids.
- **Maximum crystal size:** Exceptional crystals to 10 cm (rare); massive foliations can be 60 cm across
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–5 mm crystals; foliated masses up to several cm
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Growth rate peaks at 150–200°C; below 100°C nucleation is slow but massive forms accumulate.
- **Competes with:** Realgar (paragenetic pair), stibnite, native sulfur, cinnabar in same environment.

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Melts at 300–325°C; boils at 707°C. More thermally stable than realgar in terms of melting behavior.
- **Breaks down in light?** Less light-sensitive than realgar, but still alters over extended exposure. Oxidizes in light + air to arsenolite.
- **Dissolves in water?** Very low solubility; decomposes slowly in moist air.
- **Dissolves in acid?** Soluble in nitric acid and alkaline sulfide solutions.
- **Oxidizes?** Yes — in moist air oxidizes to arsenolite (As₂O₃) with release of SO₂/H₂SO₄. The oxidation is slower than realgar's light-induced alteration but still significant over months to years.
- **Dehydrates?** No hydrates.
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Not particularly sensitive; associated radioactive minerals may accelerate surface alteration.

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Realgar commonly (orpiment replaces realgar during late-stage cooling or sulfur enrichment); pyrite, arsenopyrite in higher-T assemblages
- **Forms BEFORE:** Calcite, barite, fluorite, quartz in fracture fillings; native sulfur in extreme sulfur-rich conditions
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Realgar (ubiquitous), stibnite, cinnabar, native arsenic, native sulfur, getchellite, lorandite (Allchar), calcite, barite, gypsum, fluorite, quartz, gold (Carlin-type), pyrite
- **Zone:** Epithermal / low-temperature hydrothermal; volcanic fumaroles; hot springs; Carlin-type alteration halos; oxidation zones of arsenic-bearing sulfide deposits
- **Geological environment:** Low-temperature hydrothermal veins; volcanic fumaroles and hot springs; Carlin-type gold deposits (Nevada); epithermal precious metal systems; the oxidation zone above primary arsenopyrite-bearing ores

### Famous Localities
- **Getchell Mine, Nevada, USA:** World-famous for massive yellow orpiment intergrown with red realgar. The contrast specimens are among the most visually striking in mineralogy. Carlin-type gold deposit.
- **Twin Creeks Mine, Nevada, USA:** Also produced exceptional orpiment specimens in the Carlin trend.
- **Allchar (Alšar) Deposit, North Macedonia:** Historic source of foliated orpiment associated with lorandite and realgar. Tl-As-Sb low-temperature hydrothermal system.
- **Khaidarkan (Khaydarkan) Deposit, Kyrgyzstan:** Major source of orpiment in Central Asia; mercury-arsenic epithermal system.
- **Shikoku, Japan:** Classic Japanese epithermal gold-silver locality.
- **Notable specimens:** The Getchell-Twin Creeks specimens set the modern standard. Massive yellow plates intergrown with ruby realgar are museum pieces. Orpiment's use as a pigment in illuminated manuscripts and Asian art gives it cultural significance — "king's yellow" was once more prized than gold leaf.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally no — orpiment does not fluoresce strongly.
- **SW (255nm) color:** None typically
- **MW (310nm) color:** None typically

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** None beyond what realgar needs (light exposure optional for orpiment — less sensitive)
- **New events needed:** `event_oxidation_zone` — orpiment can form from the oxidation of earlier arsenopyrite or realgar
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF temperature < 250°C
AND As_ppm > 800
AND S_ppm > 1000
AND pH < 5.5
AND Eh < reducing threshold
AND O2_ppm < 15
AND S_ppm > As_ppm * 1.2 (sulfur-rich relative to realgar)
THEN nucleate orpiment
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF temperature < 300°C
AND S/As ratio > 1.2
AND pH < 5.5
THEN grow at rate 0.9× baseline
IF light_exposure_steps > long_threshold
THEN gradual surface alteration to arsenolite (white coating)
```
- **Habit selection logic:** Lower T → botryoidal/reniform; moderate T → foliated columnar; higher T within window → prismatic crystals
- **Decomposition products:** Arsenolite (oxidation in air); melts at 300–325°C; no solid-state polymorphic transition like realgar

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1:** Foliated — classic columnar or sheet-like masses, pearly luster, intense lemon-yellow. Best "king's yellow" appearance.
- **Variant 2:** Botryoidal — reniform or globular masses, waxy luster, deeper golden-yellow. Lower T, faster growth.
- **Variant 3:** Crystalline — rare prismatic crystals, transparent to translucent, gemmy yellow. Requires slow growth and space.
- **Variant 4:** Altered (arsenolite-coated) — white powdery coating on yellow core. Simulates oxidation in an open vug.

## Paragenetic Sequence Notes (Orpiment ↔ Realgar)

Orpiment and realgar are the arsenic sulfide twins — same environment, different stoichiometry, different colors. They share more than they differ:

**Formation sequence:**
```
Early stage (higher T, more As): Realgar (As₄S₄) nucleates
Late stage (lower T, more S): Orpiment (As₂S₃) replaces or overgrows
```

Orpiment can also form directly from the oxidation of arsenopyrite:
```
Arsenopyrite (FeAsS) + O₂ + H₂O → Orpiment + Scorodite + H₂SO₄
```

In the vugg simulator, both minerals should be **light-sensitive** but to different degrees:
- **Realgar:** High sensitivity — transforms to pararealgar in ~10 steps of light exposure
- **Orpiment:** Low sensitivity — surface oxidizes to arsenolite over ~50 steps

Both are **soft and sectile** — hardness 1.5–2 means they should have low structural integrity in the simulator (easily damaged, poor mechanical stability).

Both require **acidic, reducing, As-S-rich broth** — the gate conditions are nearly identical, with the As:S ratio being the primary discriminator between them.

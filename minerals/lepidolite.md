# Species: Lepidolite

## Identity
- **Formula:** K(Li,Al)₃(Al,Si)₄O₁₀(F,OH)₂ — generalized; actual composition varies along the polylithionite–trilithionite series
- **Crystal system:** Monoclinic
- **Mineral group:** Phyllosilicate, Mica group (trioctahedral micas)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 2.5–3
- **Specific gravity:** 2.8–2.9
- **Cleavage:** {001} perfect — splits into thin, flexible sheets (basal cleavage, one plane)
- **Fracture:** Uneven
- **Luster:** Vitreous to pearly on cleavage faces

### IMA Series Note
Lepidolite is no longer recognized as a single mineral species by the IMA. It is the common name for the **polylithionite–trilithionite series**, where Li:Al ratios vary:
- **Polylithionite:** KLi₂AlSi₄O₁₀(F,OH)₂ (Li:Al = 2:1)
- **Trilithionite:** KLi₁.₅Al₁.₅(AlSi₃)O₁₀(F,OH)₂ (Li:Al ≈ 1:1)
- **Lepidolite (intermediate):** K(Li,Al)₃(Al,Si)₄O₁₀(F,OH)₂

For gameplay purposes, "lepidolite" is treated as the intermediate member — the name collectors know.

## Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Lilac, lavender, purple, rose-red, violet-gray; also pale yellow (Itinga, Brazil), gray, or colorless
- **Color causes:** Trace **Mn²⁺** — not lithium. The pink-to-purple hues that define lepidolite are caused by divalent manganese substituting in the octahedral sites. Evans & Raftery (1982) confirmed this.
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:**
  - Scaly, micaceous aggregates that shimmer like fish scales (the name derives from Greek *lepidos*, "scale")
  - "Books" — stacked hexagonal tabular crystals with pearly sheen
  - Perfect cleavage produces paper-thin sheets that bend but are inelastic (unlike true muscovite, which is elastic)
  - Radiating sprays in some pegmatite cavities

---

## Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Tabular to prismatic pseudohexagonal crystals; commonly scaly aggregates and massive
- **Common forms/faces:** Hexagonal tablets with rounded terminal faces; foliated masses
- **Twin laws:** Rare — composition plane {001}, twin axis [310]
- **Varieties:**
  - **Yellow lepidolite** — rare variety from Itinga, Minas Gerais, Brazil (higher Fe, different Mn oxidation state)
  - **Lithian muscovite** — sometimes incorrectly called "lepidolite"; lower Li content, more elastic
  - **Scaly aggregates** — most common form in nature
- **Special morphologies:**
  - Foliated / platy masses
  - Scaly "books" up to 20 cm across in exceptional pegmatites
  - Fine-grained aggregates coating other minerals

## Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** ~350–550°C (late magmatic to early hydrothermal)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** ~400–500°C in volatile-rich pegmatite fluids
- **Decomposition temperature:** >600°C begins dehydroxylation; complete breakdown >800°C
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - Higher temperatures → larger, more perfect hexagonal books
  - Lower temperatures / hydrothermal alteration → finer scaly aggregates, replacement of earlier spodumene

### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:**
  - **Li:** Minimum ~1000 ppm (lepidolite is the most abundant Li-bearing mineral, containing ~7.7 wt% Li)
  - **K:** Minimum ~500 ppm (framework cation)
  - **Al:** Minimum ~1000 ppm (octahedral and tetrahedral sites)
  - **Si:** Minimum ~2000 ppm (tetrahedral sheets)
  - **F:** Minimum ~200 ppm (lepidolite is F-rich; F substitutes for OH)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - **Mn:** 50–500 ppm (color — the purple chromophore)
  - **Rb:** Substitutes for K; lepidolite is the major source of rubidium in nature
  - **Cs:** Trace substitution for K
  - **Na:** Minor substitution for K
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - **Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺:** >500 ppm suppresses the purple color, shifts toward gray/greenish tones
  - **Mg:** Shifts composition toward biotite/zinnwaldite, destabilizing lepidolite structure
- **Required pH range:** 6.5–8.5 (mildly alkaline, pegmatite fluid range)
- **Required Eh range:** Moderately oxidizing (Mn²⁺ must remain divalent for purple color)
- **Required O₂ range:** Low to moderate (pegmatite setting, not highly oxidizing)

### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?**
  - Yes — late-stage crystallization of lepidolite strongly depletes the residual fluid in **Li, F, and K**
  - This chemical "vacuum" can trigger replacement of earlier spodumene → lepidolite + eucryptite
  - Fluorine incorporation reduces pH slightly in the immediate fluid boundary layer
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** None significant
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:**
  - Dehydroxylation releases H₂O and HF
  - Above 800°C: breaks down to K-feldspar + corundum + Li-silicate melt

### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Slow (compared to quartz baseline)
  - Micas grow layer-by-layer (TOT sheet addition)
  - Late-stage pegmatite crystallization = slow, volatile-rich, low undercooling
- **Maximum crystal size:** 20 cm across (exceptional books from Himalaya Mine, California)
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–10 mm scaly aggregates; 1–3 cm books in open cavities
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?**
  - Retrograde? No — lepidolite solubility is not strongly retrograde
  - However, rapid cooling in pegmatite dikes can produce skeletal or patchy zoning
- **Competes with:**
  - **Spodumene** — earlier Li-silicate; lepidolite may replace it
  - **Muscovite** — same structure, lower Li; lepidolite wins when Li is abundant
  - **Zinnwaldite** — Fe-Li mica; lepidolite wins when Fe is low

### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes. >600°C dehydroxylation begins. >800°C structural collapse.
- **Breaks down in light?** No — color stable under normal light
- **Dissolves in water?** Very low solubility; resistant to water
- **Dissolves in acid?** Slowly in strong acids (HF attacks the silicate structure); otherwise resistant
- **Oxidizes?** Mn²⁺ may oxidize to Mn³⁺/Mn⁴⁺ in strongly oxidizing conditions, dulling the purple color
- **Dehydrates?** Partially — dehydroxylation above 600°C converts OH to O, destabilizing the mica structure
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Generally stable; radiation may affect color subtly but no major structural damage documented

## Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:**
  - Major rock-forming minerals (quartz, feldspar, muscovite) in pegmatite
  - Often replaces **spodumene** during late hydrothermal alteration
  - Postdates **columbite, cassiterite, beryl** (accessory minerals of the wall zone)
- **Forms BEFORE:**
  - Final cavity-filling minerals (clay minerals, zeolites in rare cases)
  - Sometimes intergrown with late **tourmaline** (elbaite)
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - **Quartz** — always present in pegmatite
  - **Feldspar** (microcline, albite, orthoclase) — major pegmatite constituents
  - **Spodumene** — earlier Li-mineral, often replaced by lepidolite
  - **Tourmaline** (elbaite) — same volatile-rich environment
  - **Beryl** — same pegmatite, typically earlier
  - **Amblygonite** — phosphate, same Li-rich fluids
  - **Columbite, cassiterite** — accessory Nb/Ta/Sn oxides
  - **Topaz** — F-rich, indicates volatile saturation
- **Zone:** Late-stage magmatic / early hydrothermal in granite pegmatites
- **Geological environment:**
  - Granite pegmatites (primary environment)
  - Greisens (F-rich altered granites)
  - High-temperature quartz veins
  - Rarely in lithium-rich hydrothermal veins

### Paragenetic Sequence in LCT Pegmatites
(typical lithium-cesium-tantalum pegmatite, inner zones):
1. Wall zone: quartz + feldspar + muscovite + **columbite** + **cassiterite**
2. Intermediate zone: quartz + feldspar + **beryl** + **spodumene**
3. Core / core margin: **lepidolite** + **tourmaline** (elbaite) + **amblygonite** + quartz
4. Late hydrothermal: lepidolite replaces spodumene; fine mica aggregates fill fractures

## Famous Localities
- **Virgem da Lapa, Minas Gerais, Brazil** — the classic locality. Split-color varieties, high-quality books. Virgem da Lapa pegmatite is one of the most famous in the world.
- **Himalaya Mine, Mesa Grande District, San Diego County, California, USA** — lavender "books" up to 5 cm, associated with tourmaline and beryl. The mine is legendary among pegmatite collectors.
- **Tanco Mine, Bernic Lake, Manitoba, Canada** — one of the world's richest lithium deposits. Lepidolite occurs with pollucite, spodumene, and tantalite.
- **Pala District, San Diego County, California, USA** — Stewart Mine, Little Three Mine. Lepidolite associated with morganite (pink beryl) and tourmaline.
- **Madagascar** — Mt. Bity, Maharitra. Fine purple crystalline masses.
- **Rožná pegmatite, Moravia, Czech Republic** — type locality (original source of the name). Historical significance: Bunsen & Kirchhoff extracted 150 kg of lepidolite here in 1861 to isolate rubidium.
- **Itinga, Minas Gerais, Brazil** — source of rare yellow lepidolite.

### Notable Specimens
- Exceptional books to 20 cm from the Himalaya Mine
- Radiating sprays from White Elephant pegmatite, Black Hills, South Dakota
- The Bunsen & Kirchhoff rubidium isolation lepidolite (Rožná, 1861) — historically important specimen

## Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Generally **no** — lepidolite is typically non-fluorescent under standard UV lamps. The Fluomin database lists it as "NR" (non-reported/weak).
- **However:** some specimens show **thermoluminescence** — they emit visible light when heated to 50–200°C. This is a distinct phenomenon from fluorescence.
- **SW (255nm):** None to very weak
- **MW (310nm):** None to very weak
- **LW (365nm):** None to very weak
- **Phosphorescent?** No known phosphorescence
- **Activator:** None for fluorescence. For thermoluminescence, the mechanism involves trapped charge carriers released by thermal energy.
- **Quenched by:** Not applicable (not fluorescent)
- **Game note:** If thermoluminescence is implemented as a mechanic, lepidolite would be one of the few minerals showing it. The player could "heat" the crystal (simulating thermal stimulation) to see a brief glow.

## Flavor Text

> Lepidolite arrives last to the party — so late that the granite has already thrown out most of its guests. In the final, volatile-charged dregs of a crystallizing pegmatite, where lithium and fluorine have nowhere left to go, this mica condenses like frost on the inside of a window. Its purple color comes not from the lithium it hoards but from manganese, a trace guest that stains the sheets the color of bruised twilight. The Greeks called it *lepidos* — scale — because it splits into shimmering plates thinner than paper. Handle it gently: at hardness 2.5, your fingernail can scar it, and its perfect cleavage will surrender a flake to the slightest pressure. In 1861, Bunsen and Kirchhoff roasted 150 kilograms of lepidolite from Moravia to extract a few grams of an unknown element, and named it rubidium — after rubidus, the red of its flame. The stone gave up a secret it didn't even know it was keeping. Some crystals are teachers disguised as rocks.

## Simulator Implementation Notes

### New Parameters Needed
- **trace_Li** — lithium concentration (currently not tracked; must be added to broth chemistry)
- **trace_F** — fluorine concentration (may already exist as trace element; verify)

### New Events Needed
- **pegmatite_magma_pulse** — Late-stage volatile-rich magmatic injection
  - Temp: 400–550°C
  - Chemistry: High Li, F, K, Al, Si; moderate Mn
  - pH: 7.0–8.0
  - Duration: 20–40 steps
- **spodumene_replacement** — If spodumene exists and late fluids are Li+F-rich, chance to convert spodumene → lepidolite + quartz

### Nucleation Rule Pseudocode
```
IF temperature >= 350 AND temperature <= 550
   AND trace_Li >= 1000 ppm
   AND trace_K >= 500 ppm
   AND trace_Al >= 1000 ppm
   AND trace_Si >= 2000 ppm
   AND trace_F >= 200 ppm
   AND pH >= 6.5 AND pH <= 8.5
   AND vug_has_late_stage_magmatic_signature = true
   AND existing_lepidolite_count < max_lepidolite (3-4 per vug)
   → nucleate lepidolite
```

### Growth Rule Pseudocode
```
IF trace_Li >= 800 ppm
   AND trace_F >= 150 ppm
   AND temperature >= 300 AND temperature <= 600
   AND pH >= 6.0 AND pH <= 9.0
   → grow at rate SLOW (0.3× quartz baseline)
   
IF trace_Mn >= 50 ppm
   → color = purple (intensity scales with Mn concentration)
   
IF trace_Mn < 50 ppm AND trace_Fe >= 500 ppm
   → color = gray / greenish-gray
   
IF trace_Mn < 50 ppm AND trace_Fe < 500 ppm
   → color = colorless / pale yellow
```

### Habit Selection Logic
- IF growth_rate > 0.5× slow AND temperature > 450°C → hexagonal books (tabular crystals)
- IF growth_rate < 0.5× slow OR temperature < 350°C → scaly aggregates
- IF replacing existing spodumene → fine scaly replacement rims

### Decomposition Products
- At >600°C: dehydroxylated lepidolite (metalepidolite) → unstable
- At >800°C: K-feldspar + corundum + Li-silicate glass/melt
- Releases: H₂O, HF, Li into fluid

## Variants for Game

### Variant 1: Purple Books
- **What makes it different:** Classic lilac-purple hexagonal tabular crystals with pearly luster
- **Conditions:** Mn 100–500 ppm, moderate Fe (<300 ppm), temperature 400–500°C, slow growth
- **Visual:** Stacked hexagonal "books" with violet sheen

### Variant 2: Scaly Aggregate
- **What makes it different:** Massive, foliated, shimmering scaly form — most common in nature
- **Conditions:** Lower temperature (350–400°C), higher fluid turbulence, faster nucleation
- **Visual:** Glistening purple-gray crusts, no distinct crystal form

### Variant 3: Yellow Lepidolite (Itinga Type)
- **What makes it different:** Rare yellow coloration
- **Conditions:** Higher Fe, different Mn speciation, specific to Itinga-type chemistry
- **Visual:** Pale to deep yellow, still micaceous
- **Rarity:** Rare (5% spawn rate if conditions met)

### Variant 4: Rubidium-Rich
- **What makes it different:** Higher Rb content (pinker hue, distinct flame-test red)
- **Conditions:** Very late-stage crystallization where K is depleted and Rb concentrates
- **Visual:** Deeper rose-red than standard purple
- **Rarity:** Uncommon (10% spawn rate)
- **Easter egg:** Hover text references Bunsen & Kirchhoff discovery

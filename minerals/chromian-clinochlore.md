## Species: Chromian Clinochlore (variety: Kämmererite)

### Identity
- **Formula:** (Mg,Fe²⁺)₆(Si,Al)₄O₁₀(OH)₈ with Cr³⁺ substituting for Al and/or Fe in the M sites. The chromium end-member would approach vakhrushevaite, the hypothetical Cr-analogue of clinochlore.
- **Crystal system:** Monoclinic (2/m)
- **Mineral group:** Chlorite group (phyllosilicate, layered mica-like structure)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 2–2.5 (very soft — among the softest collectible minerals)
- **Specific gravity:** 2.65–2.78 (slightly heavier than normal clinochlore due to Cr)
- **Cleavage:** Perfect basal {001} — one perfect cleavage, micaceous (flakes split easily)
- **Fracture:** Lamellar, flexible but inelastic (unlike true micas, chlorites are not elastic)
- **Luster:** Vitreous to pearly on cleavage faces; may appear slightly greasy
- **Streak:** White to pale green (chromian varieties may show pale purple streak)

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Emerald green, grass green, deep green (Cr³⁺ in octahedral coordination, same chromophore as emerald and uvarovite). The chromium variety kämmererite ranges from pink through violet to deep crimson-red.
- **Color causes:** Cr³⁺ substituting for Al³⁺ in the octahedral M sites. In octahedral coordination, Cr³⁺ produces green at low crystal-field strength and violet-to-red at higher field strength (as in kämmererite). The exact hue depends on the local geometry and co-substitutions.
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent in thin flakes; massive aggregates are translucent to opaque
- **Streak:** White to pale greenish
- **Notable visual features:** Micaceous — splits into thin flexible sheets. Chromian varieties have a distinctive "wet" or greasy luster. Often forms scaly, foliated, or rosette aggregates. May show a slightly pearly iridescence on cleavage surfaces. Unlike true micas (muscovite, biotite), chlorites feel softer and lack elasticity.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Tabular pseudohexagonal crystals; also thick flakes, micaceous masses, scaly aggregates
- **Common forms/faces:** {001} basal plates, tabular to prismatic with hexagonal outline
- **Twin laws:** Not common; occasionally contact twins on {001}
- **Varieties:**
  - **Kämmererite:** The chromium-rich variety, lilac to deep crimson-red, named after August Alexander Kämmerer (Russian mining director, 1819–1889). Most famous and collectible variety. Sometimes spelled "kaemmererite."
  - **Seraphinite:** A trade name for chatoyant clinochlore showing feathery silver inclusions, used as a minor gemstone. Not chromium-related but worth noting as a game variant.
  - **Pennine:** Pseudohexagonal clinochlore (not chromian-specific but a common form)
  - **Sheridanite:** Al-rich variety of clinochlore (more Al, less Cr)
- **Special morphologies:** Rosettes, dense scaly coatings, massive foliated forms, pseudomorphs after garnet or other minerals

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 150–450°C (retrograde hydrothermal / serpentinization)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 200–350°C (the cooler, wetter tail of the hydrothermal system)
- **Decomposition temperature:** 500–600°C (dehydroxylation begins; by ~750°C the chlorite structure collapses to olivine-like or spinel-like assemblages)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Higher temperatures (300–450°C) favor larger, more euhedral pseudohexagonal plates. Lower temperatures (150–250°C) favor fine-grained scaly or rosette aggregates. Kämmererite specifically tends to form at the lower end (150–300°C) as a late alteration product.

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Mg (major, >1000 ppm), Al (moderate, >200 ppm), Si (major, >500 ppm), OH (major, buffered by pH), Cr (>100 ppm for chromian variety)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Fe²⁺ (promotes transition toward chamosite, darkens color), Fe³⁺ (promotes oxidation and color shift), Ni (common in ultramafic environments)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Ca suppresses chlorite in favor of uvarovite, diopside, or garnet; high K suppresses chlorite in favor of phlogopite or biotite; high Na suppresses in favor of sodic amphiboles
- **Required pH range:** 8–11 (alkaline to strongly alkaline, typical of serpentinizing fluids which have pH 9–11 due to brucite and serpentine formation)
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing (serpentinization is a strongly reducing environment; H₂ is generated)
- **Required O₂ range:** Very low O₂ (<0.1 ppm)

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Yes — chlorite formation in serpentinites is part of the broader serpentinization reaction:
  - 2 Mg₂SiO₄ (olivine, forsterite) + 3 H₂O → Mg₃Si₂O₅(OH)₄ (serpentine) + Mg(OH)₂ (brucite)
  - During this process, H₂ gas is generated as a byproduct of Fe²⁺ oxidation to Fe³⁺ in magnetite
  - Chromian clinochlore forms as a later alteration of chromite or earlier-formed clinochlore, consuming Al and Cr from dissolving chromite spinel
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** H₂ gas (from concurrent serpentinization); minor heat release (exothermic hydration reactions)
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Above 500–600°C, dehydroxylation releases H₂O; at higher temperatures the structure breaks down to olivine + spinel or pyroxene + spinel depending on composition

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Fast compared to most silicates (phyllosilicates precipitate readily from aqueous fluids). Much faster than uvarovite.
- **Maximum crystal size:** Exceptional kämmererite crystals reach 5–10 mm (from Kop Krom mine, Turkey). Most natural crystals are 1–3 mm.
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 0.5–3 mm flakes and rosettes; scaly coatings up to several cm² in area but only mm-thick
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — higher temperatures produce larger crystals but slower nucleation density; lower temperatures produce fine-grained scaly aggregates with rapid nucleation
- **Competes with:** Serpentine (lizardite, antigorite) for Mg and Si; uvarovite for Cr and Ca; chromite for Cr; talc for Mg and Si; brucite for Mg and OH

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes — begins dehydroxylation at 500–600°C; structure destroyed by ~750°C. Products depend on composition: Mg-rich → forsterite + enstatite + spinel; Fe-rich → fayalite + ferrosilite + magnetite.
- **Breaks down in light?** No.
- **Dissolves in water?** Very poorly soluble in neutral water. Slightly soluble in acidic water (pH < 5), releasing Mg²⁺, Al³⁺, Cr³⁺, and silica.
- **Dissolves in acid?** Yes — dissolves readily in HCl and other strong acids. Note: chromian varieties release Cr³⁺ (relatively low toxicity) but if oxidized to Cr⁶⁺ (chromate), the acid leachate becomes hazardous.
- **Oxidizes?** Yes — Fe²⁺ in the structure oxidizes to Fe³⁺, changing color and optical properties. This is common in weathered chlorites. Cr³⁺ remains stable (Cr³⁺ is the most stable oxidation state under most Earth-surface conditions).
- **Dehydrates?** Yes — stepwise dehydration: loses interlayer and OH water between 450–700°C. Complete structural collapse by ~750°C.
- **Radiation sensitivity:** No significant radiation effects known.

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Chromite must be present and undergoing alteration to release Cr³⁺. Serpentinization must be active or recently completed to provide the alkaline, reducing, Mg-rich fluid environment. Uvarovite may precede chromian clinochlore in the same vein (see paragenetic sequence below).
- **Forms BEFORE:** Later-stage carbonates (calcite, dolomite) commonly fill remaining fracture space after chlorite-uvarovite deposition. Talc may form as an even later, lower-temperature alteration of chlorite or serpentine.
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - **In serpentinites:** Chromite (source of Cr), serpentine (lizardite, antigorite), brucite, magnetite, talc, uvarovite (often in same vein), fuchsite (Cr-muscovite), dolomite
  - **In rodingites (Ca-metasomatized ultramafics):** Uvarovite, grossular, diopside, wollastonite, vesuvianite, calcite
  - **In skarns:** Diopside, grossular, andradite, epidote, wollastonite
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene (retrograde hydrothermal in serpentinites and skarns) and supergene/weathering (oxidized chlorite forms from earlier chlorite)
- **Geological environment:** Serpentinized ultramafic rocks (ophiolites, mantle wedges), rodingites (Ca-metasomatized serpentinites), contact metamorphic skarns, chromite deposits

#### Paragenetic Sequence (Chromite → Clinochlore → Uvarovite → Chromian Clinochlore)
Based on ophiolite vein-fill studies (Moa-Baracoa, Cuba; Vourinos, Greece), the retrograde sequence in millimeter-wide fractures cutting chromitite is:

1. **Clinochlore** (Mg-Al chlorite, no Cr) — earliest, highest temperature
2. **Uvarovite** (Ca-Cr garnet) — mobilization of Ca + Cr from altering chromite
3. **Chromian clinochlore** (Cr-bearing chlorite) — retrograde reworking of earlier phases, lower temperature
4. **Rutile** (TiO₂) — late Ti mobilization
5. **Titanite** (CaTiSiO₅) — very late Ca-Ti phase
6. **Calcite** (CaCO₃) — final fracture sealing, lowest temperature

This sequence represents progressive cooling and changing fluid chemistry in a single hydrothermal vein. In the simulator, this could be modeled as a single event (`event_serpentinization`) that triggers a timed sequence of mineral appearances as temperature drops and fluid chemistry evolves.

### Famous Localities
- **Kop Krom mine, Kop Dağlari, Erzurum Province, Turkey:** The most famous kämmererite locality. Serpentinized peridotite with chromite. Produces transparent gemmy crystals of deep crimson color, up to 5–10 mm. The mine is also known for uvarovite in the same rocks. Fine specimens command premium prices.
- **Wood's Chrome Mine, Texas, Lancaster County, Pennsylvania, USA:** Classic U.S. locality for kämmererite. Historic Pennsylvania-Maryland State Line serpentinite district. Chromite-bearing serpentinite with Cr-rich chlorite.
- **Saranovskii Mine, Ural Mountains, Russia:** Type locality region for uvarovite; also produces chromian clinochlore/kämmererite in serpentinized chromitite.
- **Outokumpu, Finland:** Famous for uvarovite; chromian clinochlore occurs as an associated mineral in the same serpentinite-hosted chromite deposits.
- **Brinton's Quarry, West Chester, Chester County, Pennsylvania, USA:** Original type locality for clinochlore (not specifically chromian, but historically significant).
- **New Idria District, Diablo Range, San Benito County, California, USA:** Serpentinite belt with chromite; good chromian clinochlore specimens.
- **Jeffrey mine, Asbestos, Quebec, Canada:** Serpentinite with chromite; produces chromian clinochlore and associated minerals.
- **Notable specimens:** A 5 mm gemmy kämmererite crystal from Kop Krom is considered world-class. Due to perfect cleavage and micaceous habit, faceted gems are extraordinarily rare — most specimens are kept as crystals or thin cleavage fragments.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** YES — weakly to moderately fluorescent
- **SW (255nm) color:** Weak red or orange-red (Cr³⁺ luminescence)
- **MW (310nm) color:** Weak red
- **LW (365nm) color:** Weak red to pink (most pronounced under 365 nm excitation)
- **Phosphorescent:** Not significantly
- **Activator:** Cr³⁺ in octahedral coordination — the same ²E → ⁴A₂ transition responsible for ruby red fluorescence. However, in the chlorite structure the crystal field is weaker than in corundum, producing less intense emission.
- **Quenched by:** Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺ both strongly quench Cr³⁺ fluorescence. Fe-bearing varieties show very weak or no fluorescence. Pure Mg-Cr varieties fluoresce most strongly.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** None — all chemistry can be modeled from existing trace elements. However, a `serpentinization_index` or `ultramafic_host` flag would be useful for triggering the correct environment.
- **New events needed:** `event_chromite_alteration` — triggers when chromite-bearing rock is exposed to aqueous fluid at 150–450°C. This event should release Cr into the fluid and set up the paragenetic sequence.
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF (temperature > 150°C AND temperature < 450°C) AND
   (Mg > 1000 ppm AND Al > 200 ppm AND Si > 500 ppm) AND
   (Cr > 100 ppm) AND
   (pH > 8 AND pH < 11) AND
   (Eh < -0.2 OR strongly reducing) AND
   (serpentinization_active OR chromite_present) AND
   (vug_fill < 0.9)
→ nucleate chromian clinochlore (max 3 per vug)
→ IF Cr > 300 ppm AND Mg/Al ratio > 2:
    → variety = kämmererite (pink/purple/red)
  ELSE:
    → variety = chromian clinochlore (green)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF (temperature > 150°C AND temperature < 400°C) AND
   (Mg > 500 ppm AND Al > 100 ppm AND Si > 300 ppm) AND
   (pH > 7.5 AND pH < 11) AND
   (vug_fill < 0.95)
→ grow at rate 1.5× quartz_baseline (fast for a silicate)
→ habit: scaly rosette (probability 0.6) OR pseudohexagonal plate (probability 0.4)
→ IF temperature > 300°C: favor larger plates
→ IF temperature < 200°C: favor fine scaly aggregates
→ color: green (base) with Cr³⁺ intensity mapped to Cr concentration
→ IF Cr > 300 ppm AND low Fe: shift toward purple/red (kämmererite end)
→ IF Fe > 500 ppm: darken toward black-green (chamosite transition)
```
- **Habit selection logic:** Pseudohexagonal plates favored by slow growth in static fluid at 300–400°C. Rosettes favored by moderate nucleation density at 200–300°C. Scaly aggregates favored by rapid nucleation at 150–200°C or high fluid turbulence.
- **Decomposition products:** Above 500–600°C: dehydroxylation releases H₂O; above 750°C structure collapses. In game terms, thermal destruction at 600°C could yield a "metamorphosed residue" (olivine-like mineral) + H₂O release.

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1 — Kämmererite (Crimson Leaf):** The prized Cr-rich variety. Pink to deep crimson-red pseudohexagonal plates or rosettes. Requires Cr > 300 ppm, Mg/Al > 2, low Fe (<200 ppm). Forms at 150–300°C in serpentinized chromitite. Very soft (2–2.5 Mohs). Weak red UV fluorescence. Most visually striking variant. Named for August Alexander Kämmerer.
- **Variant 2 — Chromian Green (Serpentinite Cloak):** Standard emerald-green chromian clinochlore. Scaly to foliated aggregates coating fracture walls. Requires Cr > 100 ppm but not enough for kämmererite color shift. More common. Often intergrown with serpentine and brucite. Forms in broader temperature range (150–450°C).
- **Variant 3 — Pseudomorph After Garnet (Ghost Crystal):** Chromian clinochlore pseudomorphing after earlier-formed uvarovite or grossular garnet. The clinochlore replaces the garnet from the outside in, preserving the dodecahedral crystal shape but with micaceous, soft, green material. Requires pre-existing garnet + late-stage chloritizing fluid at 200–350°C. Rare and visually distinctive — a hard garnet turned soft and green.

## Paragenetic Context: The Serpentinite Vein Sequence

Chromian clinochlore does not form in isolation. In serpentinized ultramafic rocks with chromite, the retrograde vein-fill sequence is:

| Stage | Mineral | Temperature | Chemistry | Notes |
|-------|---------|-------------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | Clinochlore (Mg-Al) | 350–450°C | Mg, Al, Si, OH | Early, high-T chlorite |
| 2 | Uvarovite | 300–550°C | Ca, Cr, Si | Garnet phase, see `research-uvarovite.md` |
| 3 | **Chromian clinochlore** | 150–350°C | Mg, Al, Si, Cr, OH | **This mineral** — retrograde Cr uptake |
| 4 | Rutile | <300°C | Ti | Late Ti mobilization |
| 5 | Titanite | <250°C | Ca, Ti, Si | Very late |
| 6 | Calcite | <200°C | Ca, CO₃ | Final fracture seal |

This sequence represents a single cooling fluid pulse in a fractured serpentinite. The simulator could model this as a `event_serpentinization` that triggers a multi-step timer: early clinochlore → uvarovite → chromian clinochlore → late oxides/carbonates, each stage unlocking as temperature drops.

## Implementation Notes for Builders
- **Priority:** Medium. No chemical blockers — Mg, Al, Si, Cr, OH all modelable. The main requirement is a serpentinite/ultramafic scenario or event.
- **Art direction:** Micaceous, flaky, leaf-like. Green base with possible violet/crimson shift. Pseudohexagonal plates. Scaly rosettes. Soft and delicate visual treatment — should look like it could flake apart.
- **Mechanics:** Very soft (2–2.5 Mohs) — should be among the easiest minerals to "damage" or dissolve if the simulator implements physical fragility. Perfect cleavage could be a visual feature (crystals shown splitting into sheets).
- **Narrative hook:** A chlorite that thinks it's a ruby. The softest mineral with the hardest color. Named for a mining director, not a nobleman (unlike uvarovite).
- **Paragenetic tie-in:** When implementing serpentinite scenarios, chromian clinochlore should appear in the same vugs as uvarovite but at later, cooler stages. The two minerals tell a time story: uvarovite = the hot beginning, chromian clinochlore = the cool aftermath.
- **Kämmererite vs chromian clinochlore:** The color boundary is fuzzy. In the game, use a simple threshold: Cr > 300 ppm + low Fe = kämmererite (purple/red); lower Cr or higher Fe = green chromian clinochlore. This is geologically approximate but gameplay-friendly.

# Research: Native Gold

## Species: Native Gold

### Identity
- **Formula:** Au — native element, commonly alloyed with Ag (electrum), Cu, Pd, Hg, Bi
- **Crystal system:** Cubic (isometric), face-centered cubic (FCC) lattice
- **Mineral group:** Native element — metal
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 2.5–3
- **Specific gravity:** 15.0–19.3 (pure gold = 19.3; natural alloys lower)
- **Cleavage:** None
- **Fracture:** Hackly (jagged, sharp) when fresh; rounded when water-worn
- **Luster:** Metallic, highly reflective

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Rich yellow to pale yellow; silvery-white when Ag-rich (electrum, >20% Ag); reddish when Cu-rich (copper-gold alloys, rare)
- **Color causes:** Intrinsic metallic bonding — no chromophore element needed. Pure gold is the reference yellow against which all other minerals are measured. Silver content shifts color toward white; copper toward red.
- **Transparency:** Opaque in all thicknesses
- **Streak:** Yellow (diagnostic — gold leaves a yellow streak even when alloyed)
- **Notable visual features:**
  - Dendritic and arborescent (tree-like) growths — among the most spectacular crystal habits in mineralogy
  - Wire gold — hair-thin filaments twisted into impossible shapes
  - Spongy and leaf gold — thin sheets that float on water
  - Hackly surface texture on freshly broken surfaces

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Octahedral {111}, cubic {100}, dodecahedral {110}; often rounded or flattened
- **Common forms/faces:** Octahedra most common; cubic crystals rarer but documented; dodecahedra intermediate
- **Twin laws:** Spinel law twins (contact twins on {111}) common; herringbone and dendritic growth forms are not true twins but oriented overgrowths
- **Varieties:**
  - **Electrum:** Native gold with >20% silver, color silvery-white to pale yellow
  - **Amalgam:** Gold-mercury alloy, rare, forms near mercury mineralization
  - **Porpezite:** Gold-palladium alloy (Au,Pd), very rare, Brazil
  - **Rhodite:** Gold-rhodium alloy, extremely rare
- **Special morphologies:**
  - **Wire gold:** Hair-thin filaments, often twisted or braided, up to several cm long
  - **Dendritic gold:** Branching, tree-like aggregates on rock surfaces
  - **Leaf gold:** Thin sheets, sometimes large enough to cover a hand
  - **Spongy gold:** Porous, coral-like masses
  - **Nugget gold:** Water-worn, rounded masses from placer deposits; can be enormous (the Welcome Stranger nugget: 72 kg)
  - **Crystallized gold:** Euhedral octahedra, cubes, combinations — rare and highly prized by collectors

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 50–700°C (extremely wide — the widest of any native element)
  - Epithermal: 150–300°C
  - Mesothermal: 250–450°C
  - Magmatic-hydrothermal: 400–700°C
  - Supergene: 20–80°C
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 200–350°C for high-grade hydrothermal veins; lower for supergene enrichment
- **Decomposition temperature:** Does not decompose — gold melts at 1064°C, far above any vug scenario. The FCC lattice is stable to melting.
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - Higher temperatures (>350°C): equant grains, disseminated, less crystalline
  - Moderate temperatures (200–350°C): well-formed octahedra, wire growth in open spaces
  - Lower temperatures (<200°C): dendritic, leaf, and wire forms dominate; high surface area reflects lower energy crystallization
  - Supergene (<80°C): films, coatings, delicate arborescences

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Au (minimum ~0.1–1 ppm for visible deposition from hydrothermal fluids)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - **Ag:** Forms electrum; stabilizes gold in lower-temperature fluids; shifts color toward white
  - **Te:** Enables gold-telluride paragenesis (calaverite, sylvanite, petzite) that precedes or accompanies native gold
  - **S:** Essential for Au(HS)₂⁻ transport in reducing, sulfide-rich fluids; Au solubility 0.1 ppb–1 ppm at 250–350°C in equilibrium with pyrite
  - **Cl:** Enables AuCl₂⁻ transport in oxidized, saline fluids; important in porphyry and high-sulfidation systems
  - **Fe:** Pyrite and pyrrhotite buffer sulfur fugacity, controlling gold solubility
  - **As:** Arsenian pyrite can host invisible gold (nanoparticles) that remobilizes to form native gold
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - **Pb, Zn:** Compete for sulfur; high base metal content can suppress gold saturation
  - **Cu:** Forms chalcopyrite instead of gold in Cu-rich systems; gold becomes secondary
  - **Sb:** In high concentrations, stabilizes aurostibite (AuSb₂) instead of native gold
- **Required pH range:** Highly variable — gold deposits across the full spectrum:
  - Acidic (pH 2–4): high-sulfidation epithermal, porphyry
  - Neutral (pH 6–7): mesothermal orogenic, low-sulfidation epithermal
  - Alkaline (pH 8–10): some alkaline intrusion-hosted systems
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing to mildly oxidizing — gold is most mobile as Au(I) in reduced sulfur-rich fluids (Au(HS)₂⁻); precipitates when:
  - Fluid boils (CO₂, H₂S loss → destabilizes bisulfide complex)
  - Fluid mixes with oxidized meteoric water
  - Temperature drops below solubility threshold
  - pH shifts (acidification from sulfide oxidation, or neutralization from wall-rock reaction)
- **Required O₂ range:** Low O₂ for transport (reduced sulfur complexes); higher O₂ for precipitation (oxidation destroys ligand)

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No — native gold is a zero-valent element. Precipitation removes Au⁰ from solution without byproducts.
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** In telluride systems, decomposition of calaverite (AuTe₂) or sylvanite ((Au,Ag)Te₂) releases Te and produces native gold as a secondary mineral
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Gold does not dissolve in water, weak acids, or air. Aqua regia (HCl + HNO₃) dissolves it, producing AuCl₄⁻ and NO₂. Cyanide leaching produces Au(CN)₂⁻ (industrial extraction). In the vug, gold does not decompose — it is the terminal mineral in its paragenetic sequence.

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Extremely slow — among the slowest minerals in the vug. Gold deposits at rates of nanometers to micrometers per year in nature.
- **Maximum crystal size in nature:** 2 cm crystals (octahedra, cubes) known; wire gold to 15 cm; nuggets to 72 kg (Welcome Stranger, 1869, Australia)
- **Typical crystal size in vug:** Micrometers to millimeters in disseminated deposits; up to 1 cm in high-grade epithermal bonanza zones
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — retrograde behavior: gold solubility increases with temperature in most hydrothermal systems (positive temperature dependence), so cooling promotes precipitation. Rapid cooling (boiling, fluid mixing) produces fine-grained gold; slow cooling produces coarse crystals.
- **Competes with:**
  - Pyrite, arsenopyrite, pyrrhotite (for S, Fe, As)
  - Calaverite, sylvanite, petzite (for Au, Te — these are earlier in paragenesis)
  - Electrum (for Au, Ag — same mineral, different ratio)
  - Chalcopyrite, galena, sphalerite (for space in vein; gold is usually last)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** No — melts at 1064°C. No thermal decomposition. In the vug, gold survives any temperature.
- **Breaks down in light?** No — completely photostable. Gold does not tarnish, oxidize, or change color in UV or visible light.
- **Dissolves in water?** Absolutely insoluble.
- **Dissolves in acid?** Resistant to all single acids (HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃). Dissolves only in aqua regia (3:1 HCl:HNO₃) and cyanide solutions.
- **Oxidizes?** No — gold is the definition of nobility. It does not oxidize under any natural Earth-surface conditions.
- **Dehydrates?** N/A — anhydrous.
- **Radiation sensitivity:** None — gold does not darken or change under radiation. Used in radiation shielding.

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:**
  - Early sulfides: pyrite, arsenopyrite, pyrrhotite (these set the sulfur fugacity)
  - Base metal sulfides: chalcopyrite, galena, sphalerite, tetrahedrite
  - Gold-tellurides: calaverite (AuTe₂), sylvanite ((Au,Ag)Te₂), petzite (Ag₃AuTe₂), krennerite — in telluride deposits, native gold is the terminal phase, forming from telluride decomposition or fluid evolution
  - Quartz — gold often fills fractures in or coats earlier quartz
- **Forms BEFORE:** Nothing — native gold is paragenetically terminal in almost all sequences. It can be remobilized in supergene environments, but this is dissolution-reprecipitation, not breakdown.
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - Quartz (universal gangue)
  - Pyrite, arsenopyrite (invisible gold host → remobilization)
  - Chalcopyrite, galena, sphalerite (base metal sulfide stage)
  - Calaverite, sylvanite, petzite (telluride stage, earlier)
  - Carbonates: calcite, dolomite, ankerite
  - Adularia (low-sulfidation epithermal indicator)
  - Chlorite, sericite (alteration minerals)
  - Tourmaline (some mesothermal deposits)
  - Hematite, goethite (oxidation zone, supergene)
- **Zone:**
  - Primary/hypogene: mesothermal quartz veins, epithermal bonanza shoots, porphyry stockwork, intrusion-hosted
  - Supergene: lateritic and saprolitic profiles, oxidation zones above primary sulfides
  - Placer: secondary, surficial — not a vug mineral
- **Geological environment:**
  - **Orogenic gold deposits:** Quartz-carbonate-sulfide veins in greenstone belts and slate belts (Mother Lode, Bendigo, Kalgoorlie)
  - **Epithermal low-sulfidation:** Bonanza gold-silver veins with adularia-sericite alteration (Comstock Lode, Hishikari)
  - **Epithermal high-sulfidation:** Vuggy silica, alunite, enargite-gold association (Goldfield, Nevada; Yanacocha, Peru)
  - **Porphyry Cu-Au:** Stockwork and breccia, disseminated gold with chalcopyrite, bornite (Bingham Canyon, Grasberg)
  - **Intrusion-related:** Skarns, reduced intrusion-related gold systems (Fort Knox, Alaska)
  - **Telluride deposits:** Alkaline intrusive complexes, late-stage Te-Au-Ag veins (Cripple Creek, Colorado)
  - **Supergene enrichment:** Oxidized portions of all the above — gold is remobilized and concentrated in the weathering profile

### Famous Localities
- **Mother Lode, California, USA:** The gold that built the American West. Crystallized gold from the Sierra Nevada foothills — Eagle's Nest Mine, Harvard Mine, Red Ledge Mine, Sixteen-to-One Mine. Octahedra, wire gold, dendrites in quartz. The California gold rush of 1849 was born here.
- **Witwatersrand, South Africa:** The largest gold deposit on Earth. Not a vug deposit — paleoplacer conglomerates holding 40% of all gold ever mined. But the Witwatersrand has taught us more about gold concentration than any other locality. The "banket" ore: pebbles of quartz and pyrite in a matrix of pyrite, carbon, and microscopic gold.
- **Kalgoorlie (Golden Mile), Western Australia:** The Super Pit. Orogenic gold in Archean greenstones. The Welcome Stranger nugget (72 kg, 1869) came from nearby Moliagul. The Golden Mile has produced over 60 million ounces.
- **Cripple Creek, Colorado, USA:** The world's premier telluride gold deposit. Calaverite, sylvanite, krennerite, petzite, and native gold in an Oligocene alkaline diatreme complex. Cresson Mine produced bonanza ore — some of the finest gold-telluride specimens known.
- **Hishikari, Japan:** The highest-grade gold mine in the world. Low-sulfidation epithermal veins averaging 40 g/t Au. Adularia-quartz veins with electrum and rare selenide minerals. A modern bonanza.
- **Larder Lake, Ontario, Canada:** The "Golden Highway." Orogenic gold in altered komatiites and Timiskaming sediments. High-grade veins with visible gold.
- **Kolar Gold Fields, India:** One of the deepest mines on Earth (3.2 km). Archean greenstone-hosted gold. Mined for over 120 years.
- **Notable specimens:**
  - The **Ironstone Nugget** (California): 44 kg, the largest crystallized gold specimen ever found. Not a nugget — a mass of intergrown crystals.
  - The **Fricot Nugget** (California): 201 troy ounces (6.25 kg), crystallized, from the American River
  - The **Welcome Stranger** (Australia): 72 kg nugget, largest ever found, 1869
  - The **Hand of Faith** (Australia): 27.2 kg nugget, still the largest modern nugget find, 1980
  - Crystals to 2 cm from Eagle's Nest Mine, California — among the finest crystallized gold in the world

### Flavor Text

> **The Metal That Outlasts Empires**
>
> Gold is the exception to every rule. It does not oxidize, does not tarnish, does not dissolve in water or single acids, does not decompose in heat, does not react with oxygen, does not corrode in salt. It is the most noble of the noble metals — the element that chemistry forgot how to touch.
>
> And yet it forms in the most volatile places: hydrothermal veins boiling at 300°C, fluids carrying gold as a bisulfide complex so tenuous that a shift in pH, a loss of pressure, a breath of cooler water is enough to drop it from solution as metallic Au⁰. The same fluid that dissolved it precipitates it. The same event that creates the vug fills it with gold.
>
> Gold is patient in a way no other mineral is. It does not compete for elements — it waits until everything else has taken what it needs, and then it claims the remainder. In a telluride vein, calaverite and sylvanite crystallize first, binding gold in telluride lattices. Only when the tellurium is exhausted, or the temperature drops, or the fluid evolves, does the gold appear — native, free, the terminal mineral of the paragenesis.
>
> The finest crystallized gold comes from open spaces: octahedra of impossible perfection, wire filaments thinner than hair and longer than fingers, dendrites that branch like frozen lightning. These are not the rounded nuggets of placer deposits, worn smooth by rivers. These are the shapes gold chooses for itself when it has room and time.
>
> In the vug, gold would be the last mineral to appear — and the first the collector would see. Its yellow against quartz white, its metallic luster against matte sulfide, its weight in the hand a physical fact that needs no explanation. Every civilization has agreed on its value. Not because it is rare — copper is rarer in the crust. Because it does not change. Gold is the only mineral that remembers itself exactly, across all time, all chemistry, all history. The pyramids have lost their capstones. The gold inside them has not.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** Au as a trace element (currently not in the 30-trace system)
- **New events needed:**
  - `event_gold_boiling` — fluid boiling event that destabilizes Au(HS)₂⁻, precipitating native gold
  - `event_telluride_decomp` — calaverite/sylvanite decomposition releasing Au to form native gold
  - `event_supergene_gold` — oxidation zone remobilization and concentration
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF Au > 0.1 ppm AND (
  (temperature_drop AND S_present AND reduced) OR
  (fluid_mixing AND oxidation_increase) OR
  (telluride_decomp AND Te_exhausted) OR
  (pH_shift AND Au_solubility_threshold)
) → nucleate native_gold

IF pyrite_present AND arsenopyrite_present → invisible_gold_in_pyrite (not visible in vug)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
IF Au > 0.05 ppm AND temperature < 400°C AND open_space_available → grow at rate 0.1×quartz_baseline
IF temperature_falling → growth_accelerated (retrograde solubility)
IF fluid_stagnant AND Au_gradient → dendritic_wire_mode (increase surface_area)
IF supersaturation_low → equant_grains (compact, low_surface_area)
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - High supersaturation + rapid cooling/opening → dendritic/wire/leaf
  - Moderate supersaturation + steady conditions → octahedral crystals
  - Low supersaturation + restricted space → disseminated grains, coatings
  - Supergene remobilization → films, arborescences on existing mineral surfaces
- **Decomposition products:** None — gold is terminal. In extreme vug conditions (>1064°C), it would melt (liquid gold, not a mineral). In aqua regia events (not a natural vug process), it would dissolve.

### Variants for Game

#### Variant 1: Crystallized Gold
- **What makes it different:** Well-formed octahedral or cubic crystals, metallic luster, sharp edges
- **Conditions:** Slow cooling, open vug space, moderate Au supersaturation
- **Visual:** Bright yellow octahedra on white quartz — the classic museum specimen
- **Collector value:** Very high — crystallized gold is the most prized native element specimen

#### Variant 2: Wire Gold
- **What makes it different:** Hair-thin filaments, often twisted or braided, growing into open space
- **Conditions:** Rapid deposition from boiling fluids, extreme open space, high gradient
- **Visual:** Yellow threads weaving through quartz vug, catching light like metallic spider silk
- **Collector value:** Extremely high — wire gold is the most spectacular gold habit

#### Variant 3: Electrum
- **What makes it different:** Silver-gold alloy, color pale yellow to silvery-white, often richer in Ag than Au
- **Conditions:** High Ag:Au ratio in fluid (epithermal systems), lower temperature
- **Visual:** Pale, almost white metallic mineral; harder than pure gold
- **Collector value:** Moderate — common in some deposits, less vivid color than pure gold

#### Variant 4: Supergene Gold Film
- **What makes it different:** Thin, delicate films and arborescences coating oxidized minerals (goethite, hematite)
- **Conditions:** Oxidation zone, acid-generating pyrite weathering, remobilized Au precipitating on Fe-oxides
- **Visual:** Gold leaf on rust-red matrix — ephemeral, delicate, beautiful
- **Collector value:** High for delicate specimens; easily damaged

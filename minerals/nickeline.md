# Research: Nickeline (NiAs) — Vugg Simulator

**Date:** 2026-05-09
**Researcher:** 🪨✍️ (cron expansion)
**Status:** Complete

Nickeline is the primary nickel arsenide and the anchor of the Co-Ni-As paragenetic sequence. This research covers nickeline and its closely linked partners: breithauptite (NiSb), skutterudite (CoAs₃), and nickel-skutterudite — all sharing the same hydrothermal arsenide vein environments.

---

## Species: Nickeline

### Identity
- **Formula:** NiAs
- **Crystal system:** Hexagonal (dihexagonal dipyramidal, 6/mmm)
- **Space group:** P6₃/mmc
- **Unit cell:** a = 3.602 Å, c = 5.009 Å; Z = 2
- **Mineral group:** Arsenide (nickeline group)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 5–5.5
- **Specific gravity:** 7.8 (very dense — one of the densest common ore minerals)
- **Cleavage:** Imperfect on {1010}, imperfect on {0001}
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal
- **Tenacity:** Brittle
- **Luster:** Metallic
- **Fusibility:** 2 (readily fusible)
- **Other:** Garlic odor on heating (arsenic volatilization — diagnostic but toxic)

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Pale copper-red to rose-gold; tarnishes to blackish or gray-black
- **Color causes:** Intrinsic metallic bonding; NiAs structure absorbs light across the visible spectrum, reflecting the red-copper tones
- **Transparency:** Opaque
- **Streak:** Brownish-black
- **Diaphaneity:** Opaque
- **Pleochroism:** Strong in reflected light (from pale yellowish-pink to deep rose)
- **Notable visual features:** Distinct rose-gold color distinguishes it from pyrite (brass-yellow) and copper (orange-red). Polished sections show strong anisotropy. "Plumose" or feathery textures common in massive ore.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Massive, columnar to reniform; rarely as distorted crystals
- **Common forms/faces:** When crystallized: horizontally striated, {1011} terminated crystals (rare); usually too massive to show good crystal forms
- **Twin laws:** Twinning on {1011} producing fourlings (cross-shaped twins) — rare but distinctive
- **Varieties:** None formally recognized; composition varies with Sb, Co, Fe, S substitutions
- **Special morphologies:**
  - **Plumose/feathery:** Radiating needle-like aggregates giving a feathery texture — classic for Cobalt district ore
  - **Reniform:** Kidney-shaped massive aggregates
  - **Massive columnar:** Bundles of parallel columns
  - **Crusts and patches:** In host rock, often intergrown with other arsenides

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 150–550°C (moderate- to high-temperature hydrothermal)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 250–400°C (sweet spot for well-crystallized arsenide veins)
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~800°C (melts incongruently)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - Higher T (400–550°C): coarser, more massive, better intergrowths with skutterudite
  - Lower T (150–250°C): finer-grained, more likely to show plumose texture

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Ni (≥100 ppm), As (≥100 ppm)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - Co (substitutes for Ni up to several percent)
  - Fe (minor substitution, up to ~2%)
  - Sb (substitutes for As → grades into breithauptite)
  - S (minor, up to a few percent — grades into gersdorffite NiAsS)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High S (favors pentlandite/millerite instead); high O₂ (oxidizes to annabergite); high Cu (favors chalcopyrite)
- **Required pH range:** Near-neutral to slightly acidic (5–7)
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing to mildly reducing (arsenide stability field)
- **Required O₂ range:** Very low — arsenides are extremely oxygen-sensitive

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Consumes Ni²⁺ and As³⁻ from solution; may release minor H⁺ if forming from arsenite
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** None significant
- **Byproducts of dissolution/oxidation:** Ni²⁺ + AsO₄³⁻ (arsenate) + H⁺. The oxidation of nickeline is the primary source of Ni and As for supergene annabergite.

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate — faster than cobaltite, slower than sulfides like galena
- **Maximum crystal size:** Crystals to ~5 cm (rare); usually massive or cm-scale aggregates
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 0.5–3 cm masses or patches
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — faster at higher T but more massive/less crystalline
- **Competes with:** Breithauptite (if Sb present), gersdorffite (if S present), skutterudite (for As), cobaltite (for Co if Co present), millerite (if S-rich)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Melts incongruently above ~800°C
- **Breaks down in light?** No direct effect, but surface tarnishes in air over time
- **Dissolves in water?** Very slowly in deoxygenated water; rapidly in oxidizing acidic conditions
- **Dissolves in acid?** Readily in HNO₃; slowly in HCl
- **Oxidizes?** Yes — the classic supergene sequence: NiAs + O₂ + H₂O → Ni²⁺ + H₃AsO₃/AsO₄³⁻ → Ni₃(AsO₄)₂·8H₂O (annabergite). The "nickel bloom" green crusts on weathered nickeline are annabergite.
- **Dehydrates?** No (anhydrous mineral)
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Minimal

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Early pyrite, arsenopyrite (if present in same system); sometimes forms simultaneously with skutterudite
- **Forms BEFORE:** Breithauptite (if Sb arrives later), supergene annabergite (oxidation product)
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - **Arsenides:** Skutterudite, cobaltite, safflorite, rammelsbergite, maucherite, loellingite
  - **Sulfides:** Chalcopyrite, galena, sphalerite, pyrite, millerite
  - **Silver minerals:** Native silver, dyscrasite, proustite, pyrargyrite (Cobalt district signature)
  - **Supergene:** Annabergite, erythrite (if Co present), pharmacolite
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene — moderate-temperature hydrothermal veins in mafic/ultramafic host rocks, or silver-cobalt-nickel arsenide deposits
- **Geological environment:**
  - Hydrothermal veins in Proterozoic sedimentary rocks (Cobalt-Gowganda type, Ontario)
  - Serpentinite-hosted asbestos mines (where ultramafic rocks release Ni)
  - Contact metamorphic zones around mafic intrusions
  - Some medium-temperature sulfide deposits with As enrichment

### Famous Localities
- **Cobalt-Gowganda District, Ontario, Canada:** The world's most famous nickeline locality. Silver-cobalt-nickel arsenide veins in Proterozoic rocks. Specimens show rose-gold nickeline with native silver wires, skutterudite, and cobaltite. The classic "plumose ore."
- **Mansfeld, Saxony-Anhalt, Germany:** Historical European type locality. Massive ore with cobaltite and silver.
- **Schneeberg, Saxony, Germany:** Fine crystallized specimens with safflorite and skutterudite.
- **Richelsdorf, Hesse, Germany:** Crystallized nickeline — rare well-formed crystals.
- **Saarland, Germany:** Associated with coal measures.
- **Sparta, Illinois, USA:** Unusual sedimentary occurrence (pyrite suns district)
- **Notable specimens:** Cobalt district specimens are iconic — rose-gold masses with embedded silver wires, sometimes perched on calcite. The Royal Ontario Museum holds exceptional examples.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** No (metallic mineral)
- **All UV bands:** None
- **Phosphorescent?** No
- **Activator:** N/A
- **Quenched by:** N/A

### Flavor Text
> Nickeline carries the color of a fading sunset — pale copper-rose, warm and metallic, nothing like the brassy flash of pyrite that fooled the prospectors. The old German miners called it *kupfernickel* — "copper demon" — because it looked like ore but yielded no copper. The name stuck, and eventually the "nickel" part became the element itself. Nickeline grows in arsenic-rich hydrothermal veins where nickel and arsenic find each other in the dark, stitching together the NiAs structure one hexagonal layer at a time. When oxygen finally reaches it, the transformation is dramatic: the rose-gold metal weathers to apple-green annabergite — the "nickel bloom" that coats abandoned mine dumps like verdigris on a forgotten statue. Cobalt, Ontario is the shrine: plumose masses of nickeline threaded with native silver wires, the classic silver-cobalt-nickel arsenide assemblage that built a town and a thousand mineral collections.

## Species: Breithauptite (NiSb)

### Identity
- **Formula:** NiSb
- **Crystal system:** Hexagonal (same as nickeline — isomorphous series)
- **Space group:** P6₃/mmc
- **Mineral group:** Antimonide (nickeline group)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 5.5
- **Specific gravity:** ~8.2 (even denser than nickeline — antimony is heavy)
- **Luster:** Metallic
- **Color:** Copper-red to violet-copper, tarnishing dark

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Copper-red with a violet tinge, darker than nickeline
- **Transparency:** Opaque
- **Streak:** Blackish
- **Notable visual features:** Slightly darker and more violet than nickeline. Often intimately intergrown with nickeline as Sb-As zonation.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Massive to reniform; tabular crystals rare
- **Twin laws:** Same {1011} fourling twins as nickeline
- **Special morphologies:** Usually massive in ore; crystals rare and small

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 200–500°C
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 250–400°C (similar to nickeline)
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~850°C

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Ni (≥100 ppm), Sb (≥50 ppm)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** As (substitutes for Sb → grades into nickeline)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High As favors nickeline; high S favors sulfides
- **Required pH range:** Near-neutral to slightly acidic (5–7)
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing (antimonide stability)

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Similar to nickeline
- **Maximum crystal size:** Small — usually massive or granular
- **Competes with:** Nickeline (the As/Sb competition); stibnite (if S dominates)

#### Stability
- **Oxidizes?** Yes → Ni²⁺ + Sb₂O₅ (antimony is less mobile than arsenic in oxidation)
- **Dissolves in acid?** Readily in aqua regia; slowly in HCl

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Nickeline (if Sb arrives in a later fluid pulse)
- **Forms BEFORE:** Supergene Ni-Sb oxides (rare)
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Nickeline (intimately — the two form a solid solution series), skutterudite, safflorite, native silver
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene — same arsenide-antimonide veins as nickeline
- **Geological environment:** Cobalt district (minor component of nickeline ore), some antimony-rich nickel deposits

### Famous Localities
- **Cobalt-Gowganda District, Ontario:** Minor component in nickeline ore (up to 6.5 wt.% Sb in some analyses)
- **Siegen, Germany:** Type locality
- **Notable specimens:** Usually not collected separately — occurs as Sb-rich zones within nickeline masses

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** No (metallic)

### Flavor Text
> Breithauptite is nickeline's antimony twin — same hexagonal skeleton, heavier heart. Where nickeline is rose-gold, breithauptite carries a violet dusk, the antimony lending its weight and its darkness. The two minerals grade into each other so seamlessly that a single hand specimen might be nickeline on one side and breithauptite on the other, the As-to-Sb ratio shifting like a tide. It was named for Friedrich Breithaupt, the mineralogist who gave us the very concept of paragenesis — the idea that minerals arrive in sequences, not alone. Fitting, then, that breithauptite never arrives alone either. It shares the vein with its arsenic sibling, both waiting in the dark for a prospector's lamp.

## Species: Skutterudite (CoAs₃)

### Identity
- **Formula:** CoAs₃ (note: some references give As subscript 2–3; the mineral is often slightly As-deficient)
- **Crystal system:** Cubic (isometric)
- **Space group:** Im3̄
- **Mineral group:** Arsenide (skutterudite group)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 5.5–6
- **Specific gravity:** ~6.1–6.5
- **Luster:** Metallic
- **Color:** Tin-white to silver-white, tarnishing to gray or iridescent

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Tin-white, silver-gray; fresh surfaces bright metallic
- **Transparency:** Opaque
- **Streak:** Black
- **Notable visual features:** Often shows iridescent tarnish. Cubic crystals are distinctive. Sometimes forms bizarre "skeleton" or hopper crystals.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Cubic {100} or octahedral {111}
- **Common forms/faces:** Cube, octahedron, dodecahedron; combinations common
- **Twin laws:** None notable
- **Special morphologies:**
  - **Hopper/skeleton crystals:** Hollowed-out cubes with stepped faces — classic for skutterudite
  - **Granular massive:** Common in ore
  - **Coating/crusts:** On other arsenides

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 300–550°C (medium- to high-temperature hydrothermal)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 350–450°C
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~900°C

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Co (≥100 ppm), As (≥150 ppm — higher As requirement than cobaltite)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Ni (substitutes for Co → nickel-skutterudite, previously "chloanthite"); Fe (minor substitution)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High S (favors cobaltite CoAsS or pyrite instead); high O₂
- **Required pH range:** Near-neutral to slightly acidic (5–7)
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate — slower than nickeline
- **Maximum crystal size:** Crystals to ~10 cm (rare); typically 1–3 cm
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 0.5–2 cm cubes or masses
- **Competes with:** Cobaltite (if S is present — CoAsS vs CoAs₃); nickeline (for As); erythrite (if oxidizing)

#### Stability
- **Oxidizes?** Yes → Co²⁺ + AsO₄³⁻ → erythrite (Co₃(AsO₄)₂·8H₂O)
- **Dissolves in acid?** Slowly in HCl; readily in HNO₃

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Early pyrite, arsenopyrite; often co-precipitates with nickeline
- **Forms BEFORE:** Supergene erythrite (oxidation product)
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Nickeline, cobaltite, safflorite, rammelsbergite, loellingite, native silver, calcite, dolomite
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene — medium- to high-T hydrothermal veins
- **Geological environment:** Same Co-Ni-As vein systems as nickeline; also contact metamorphic; some pegmatite occurrences

### Famous Localities
- **Skutterud, Modum, Norway:** Type locality
- **Cobalt-Gowganda District, Ontario:** Fine cubic crystals with nickeline
- **Schneeberg, Saxony, Germany:** Classic European locality
- **Bou Azzer, Morocco:** Excellent cubic crystals — modern classic
- **Notable specimens:** Bou Azzer skutterudites are modern collector favorites — bright metallic cubes, sometimes with hopper development, on matrix.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** No (metallic)

### Flavor Text
> Skutterudite is the cubic imposter in a hexagonal family — while nickeline and breithauptite stack their atoms in honeycomb columns, skutterudite builds cages. The CoAs₃ structure is famous in materials science for its thermoelectric properties, but in the vug it's famous for something simpler: hopper cubes. The crystal grows so fast at the edges that the faces hollow out, leaving a metallic box with stepped windows. Type locality Skutterud, Norway gave it a name; Bou Azzer, Morocco gave it fame — bright tin-white cubes perched on pink dolomite, some perfectly solid, some skeletonized like an architect's model. Where cobaltite carries sulfur's shadow and nickeline wears nickel's rose, skutterudite is pure arsenic ambition: more As than Co, the cobalt drowned in a sea of metalloid neighbors.

## Species: Nickel-Skutterudite (NiAs₃, previously "Chloanthite")

### Identity
- **Formula:** NiAs₃ (or (Ni,Co)As₃)
- **Crystal system:** Cubic (isometric)
- **Space group:** Im3̄ (same as skutterudite)
- **Mineral group:** Arsenide (skutterudite group)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 5.5–6
- **Specific gravity:** ~6.5–6.8
- **Luster:** Metallic
- **Color:** Tin-white, slightly darker than skutterudite

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Tin-white to pale violet-gray
- **Transparency:** Opaque
- **Streak:** Black
- **Notable visual features:** Very similar to skutterudite; often indistinguishable without analysis

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Cubic, massive
- **Special morphologies:** Same hopper/skeleton forms as skutterudite

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 300–500°C
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 350–450°C

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Ni (≥100 ppm), As (≥150 ppm)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Co (substitutes for Ni → grades into skutterudite)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High S; high O₂

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Similar to skutterudite
- **Competes with:** Skutterudite (Co/Ni competition for the same structure); nickeline (for Ni and As)

#### Stability
- **Oxidizes?** Yes → Ni²⁺ + AsO₄³⁻ → annabergite

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Early arsenides; often co-precipitates with skutterudite
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Skutterudite, nickeline, cobaltite, native silver
- **Zone:** Primary/hypogene

### Famous Localities
- **Cobalt-Gowganda District, Ontario:** In Ni-rich portions of Co-As veins
- **Schneeberg, Saxony, Germany:** Historical locality
- **Notable specimens:** Usually not distinguished from skutterudite in hand specimen

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** No (metallic)

### Flavor Text
> Nickel-skutterudite is the ghost name — officially "chloanthite" until modern analysis proved it was just skutterudite's nickel twin, same cubic cage, different metal at the center. Collectors rarely distinguish it by eye; the Ni and Co versions look identical, tin-white and metallic, maybe a shade darker where nickel replaces cobalt. The old name faded from labels but the mineral didn't fade from veins. It sits in the same arsenide sequence, the nickel variant growing where cobalt ran scarce and nickel ran deep. A reminder that naming is human business — the crystal doesn't care what we call it.

## The Co-Ni-As Paragenetic Sequence

### Complete Reaction Chain

**Primary (hypogene) stage — reducing, moderate T:**
1. **Pyrite/arsenopyrite** (early, high T)
2. **Nickeline (NiAs) + skutterudite (CoAs₃)** — main arsenide precipitation
3. **Cobaltite (CoAsS)** — if S available
4. **Breithauptite (NiSb)** — if Sb arrives later
5. **Safflorite (CoAs₂), rammelsbergite (NiAs₂)** — diarsenides, lower T
6. **Native silver** — often precipitates with/reducing event after arsenides

**Supergene (oxidation) stage — oxidizing, low T:**
- **Nickeline → annabergite** (Ni₃(AsO₄)₂·8H₂O) — apple-green "nickel bloom"
- **Skutterudite/cobaltite → erythrite** (Co₃(AsO₄)₂·8H₂O) — crimson "cobalt bloom"
- **Breithauptite → Ni-Sb oxide/hydroxide** (no famous secondary mineral)

### Key Simulator Events

**Event: Arsenide Vein Pulse**
```
IF temperature 200–500°C AND Eh < -0.1 V AND Ni+Co > 100 ppm AND As > 100 ppm:
  nucleate NICKELINE (if Ni > Co)
  nucleate SKUTTERUDITE (if Co > Ni AND S < 50 ppm)
  nucleate COBALTITE (if Co > Ni AND S > 50 ppm)
```

**Event: Antimony Enrichment**
```
IF Sb > 50 ppm AND Ni > 100 ppm AND temperature < 400°C:
  nucleate BREITHAUPTITE (may replace/zoned nickeline)
```

**Event: Supergene Oxidation (Arsenide Weathering)**
```
IF O₂ increases AND temperature < 50°C AND pH 5–8:
  existing NICKELINE → dissolve, release Ni²⁺ + AsO₄³⁻
  nucleate ANNABERGITE (if Ni²⁺ + AsO₄³⁻ + H₂O available)
  existing SKUTTERUDITE/COBALTITE → dissolve, release Co²⁺ + AsO₄³⁻
  nucleate ERYTHRITE (if Co²⁺ + AsO₄³⁻ + H₂O available)
```

## Simulator Implementation Notes

### New Parameters Needed
- **Sb (antimony) broth variable:** Needed for breithauptite; currently not tracked in the 30-trace set. Could be added as trace element #31, or breithauptite could be excluded until Sb is added.
- **Nickeline-skutterudite partitioning:** Ni/Co ratio determines which arsenide nucleates when As is abundant and S is low.

### Nucleation Rule Pseudocode

**Nickeline:**
```
IF Ni ≥ 100 ppm AND As ≥ 100 ppm AND pH 4–8 AND Eh < 0 AND T 150–550°C:
  IF Ni > Co:
    nucleate NICKELINE
  ELIF Ni ≈ Co AND random() < 0.3:
    nucleate NICKELINE  // mixed Co-Ni arsenide veins
```

**Breithauptite:**
```
IF Sb is available AND Ni ≥ 100 ppm AND Sb ≥ 50 ppm AND pH 4–8 AND Eh < 0 AND T 200–500°C:
  IF Sb > As * 0.5:
    nucleate BREITHAUPTITE
  ELIF Sb present but As dominant:
    nucleate NICKELINE with Sb substitution (zoned)
```

**Skutterudite:**
```
IF Co ≥ 100 ppm AND As ≥ 150 ppm AND S < 50 ppm AND pH 4–8 AND Eh < 0 AND T 300–550°C:
  IF Co > Ni:
    nucleate SKUTTERUDITE
  ELIF Ni > Co AND S < 50 ppm:
    nucleate NICKEL-SKUTTERUDITE
```

### Growth Rule Pseudocode

**Nickeline:**
```
rate = base_rate * (Ni * As) / (1 + S_competition + O₂_inhibition)
habit: massive/reniform by default; columnar at higher T; plumose at moderate T with oscillatory growth
IF Sb present: zoned growth (As-rich core → Sb-rich rim possible)
```

**Breithauptite:**
```
rate = base_rate * (Ni * Sb) / (1 + As_competition)
habit: massive, reniform; tabular rare
IF intergrown with nickeline: banded/zoned texture
```

**Skutterudite:**
```
rate = base_rate * (Co * As^1.5) / (1 + S_inhibition)  // As excess favored
habit: cubic {100} by default; hopper at very high supersaturation
IF σ > 5: hopper/skeleton morphology
```

### Decomposition Products
- **Nickeline at 800°C+:** melts incongruently (not a simple decomposition)
- **Nickeline oxidation:** Ni²⁺ + AsO₄³⁻ → annabergite (if hydrated, low T)
- **Skutterudite oxidation:** Co²⁺ + AsO₄³⁻ → erythrite
- **Breithauptite oxidation:** Ni²⁺ + Sb₂O₅ (antimony pentoxide — no famous mineral)

### Variants for Game

**Nickeline:**
1. **Plumose nickeline** — moderate T, moderate supersaturation. Feathery radiating aggregates. Classic Cobalt district look.
2. **Massive columnar nickeline** — higher T, slower growth. Bundles of parallel columns.
3. **Nickeline with silver** — requires native silver event. Rose-gold nickeline with silver wires embedded. The Cobalt signature.

**Breithauptite:**
1. **Massive breithauptite** — Sb-rich fluid pulse. Darker, more violet than nickeline.
2. **Zoned NiAs-NiSb** — oscillating As/Sb availability. Banded texture, each layer slightly different in color.

**Skutterudite:**
1. **Cubic skutterudite** — moderate supersaturation, moderate T. Bright tin-white cubes.
2. **Hopper skutterudite** — very high supersaturation. Hollowed cubes with stepped faces. The "skeleton crystal."
3. **Nickel-skutterudite** — Ni-dominant broth. Visually identical but the label says Ni.

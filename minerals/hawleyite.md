# Research: Hawleyite (CdS, cubic)

## Species: Hawleyite

### Identity
- **Formula:** CdS
- **Crystal system:** Cubic
- **Mineral group:** Sulfide — sphalerite group
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 2.5–3
- **Specific gravity:** 4.87
- **Cleavage:** None observed (massive/powdery)
- **Fracture:** Earthy
- **Luster:** Metallic (powdery masses may appear dull to metallic)

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Bright yellow, orange-yellow, sulfur yellow
- **Color causes:** Cd-S charge transfer; particle size affects perceived color — fine-grained powdery masses appear brighter and more intensely yellow than coarse crystals
- **Transparency:** Translucent to opaque (powdery masses are opaque)
- **Streak:** Light yellow
- **Notable visual features:** Fine-grained, earthy powdery coatings on fracture surfaces; visually indistinguishable from greenockite in hand specimen. Both dimorphs appear as identical bright yellow dustings on sphalerite or siderite.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Powdery massive, earthy coatings
- **Common forms/faces:** Not observed as euhedral crystals in nature; synthetic cubic CdS crystallizes as octahedra and tetrahedra
- **Twin laws:** None reported for natural material
- **Varieties:** None recognized; all natural hawleyite is fine-grained and massive
- **Special morphologies:** Fine-grained powdery encrustations on fracture surfaces and vug linings; yellow dusting on sphalerite; coating on siderite

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 25–200°C (supergene/low-temperature hydrothermal)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 25–100°C (lower than greenockite)
- **Decomposition temperature:** ~980°C (melts); oxidizes to CdO + SO₂ above ~600°C in air
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Low T favors hawleyite (cubic) over greenockite (hexagonal). The hexagonal/cubic dimorph boundary is kinetic — greenockite is thermodynamically stable at all temperatures under most conditions, but hawleyite forms metastably at low temperatures, especially from dilute meteoric waters. Above ~300°C in synthetic systems, greenockite is exclusively stable; hawleyite never forms.

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Cd²⁺ (cadmium), S²⁻ (sulfide)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Zn²⁺ (stabilizes sphalerite-type structure via solid solution), Fe²⁺ (may substitute in sphalerite group structure)
- **Inhibiting elements:** Excess O₂ (oxidizes CdS to CdO), high T (>300°C thermodynamically excludes hawleyite)
- **Required pH range:** Neutral to slightly acidic (5–7), same as greenockite
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing to mildly oxidizing (supergene); meteoric waters carrying Cd²⁺ encounter residual sulfide
- **Required O₂ range:** Low — supergene but still reducing enough to retain S as S²⁻

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Consumes Cd²⁺ and S²⁻ from solution. If forming from sphalerite oxidation, Zn²⁺ is left in solution.
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** None directly. Consumes trace Cd.
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Cd²⁺ (toxic) + S or SO₄²⁻ depending on conditions. CdO if thermally decomposed in air.

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Fast when Cd available — Ksp so low that supersaturation is easy to achieve
- **Maximum crystal size:** Not observed as discrete crystals in nature; all known material is microscopic powdery coatings
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** Sub-micron to microscopic; never forms visible crystals
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Not strongly — solubility is low at all temperatures
- **Competes with:** Greenockite (dimorph — thermodynamically favored at all T), smithsonite (for Cd²⁺ if oxidizing), otavite (CdCO₃ if CO₃ available)

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes, above ~600°C oxidizes to CdO + SO₂. Melts at ~980°C.
- **Breaks down in light?** Darkens with prolonged UV exposure (photoreactive CdS semiconductor)
- **Dissolves in water?** Very slightly — Ksp ~10⁻²⁸, essentially insoluble
- **Dissolves in acid?** Dissolves in strong mineral acids (HCl, HNO₃). Dilute acids slow. Oxidizing acids (HNO₃) dissolve readily.
- **Oxidizes?** Yes — in oxidizing supergene conditions, CdS → Cd²⁺ (mobile, toxic) + SO₄²⁻. Otavite (CdCO₃) may form if CO₃²⁻ available.
- **Dehydrates?** No (anhydrous)
- **Radiation sensitivity:** CdS is a semiconductor; radiation induces color changes (darkening) via defect formation

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Sphalerite (Cd source — Cd substitutes for Zn in sphalerite up to ~1%), galena
- **Forms BEFORE:** Otavite (CdCO₃, further oxidation), cadmium oxides
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Sphalerite (as substrate), siderite (as substrate), galena, smithsonite, anglesite, cerussite, greenockite (same environment, higher T or different kinetic pathway)
- **Zone:** Supergene/oxidation zone of Cd-bearing Zn-Pb deposits; meteoric water alteration
- **Geological environment:** Oxidation zones of zinc-lead deposits; vugs and fractures in sphalerite-bearing ore; late-stage meteoric water alteration

### Famous Localities
- **Classic locality 1:** Hector-Calumet mine, Keno-Galena Hill area, Yukon Territory, Canada — type locality (1955). Discovered by R.J. Traill and R.W. Boyle. Named for James Edwin Hawley (1897–1965), professor of mineralogy at Queen's University, Kingston, Ontario. Fine-grained yellow coatings on sphalerite and siderite in vugs and along late fractures.
- **Classic locality 2:** Various Zn-Pb oxidation zone localities worldwide — much material historically identified as greenockite is actually hawleyite. Common wherever sphalerite weathers in presence of meteoric water.
- **Classic locality 3:** Tsumeb, Namibia — yellow CdS coatings on sphalerite in oxidation zone (may be hawleyite or greenockite; requires XRD to distinguish)
- **Notable specimens:** No exceptional crystal specimens exist — all material is powdery/massive. Type specimens at Queen's University and Canadian Museum of Nature.

### Fluorescence
- **Fluorescent under UV?** Yes, when Zn-bearing (same as greenockite)
- **SW (255nm) color:** Yellow-orange (weak)
- **MW (310nm) color:** Yellow-orange
- **LW (365nm) color:** Bright yellow-orange to carmine-red (Zn-bearing material)
- **Phosphorescent?** Not typically
- **Activator:** Zn substitution (CdS:Zn is a known phosphor). Pure CdS is a semiconductor with bandgap ~2.42 eV (512 nm).
- **Quenched by:** Fe, excess Cd vacancies

### Flavor Text

> Hawleyite is the ghost of greenockite — the same bright yellow cadmium sulfide, but crystallized in the dark geometry of cubes instead of hexagons. You cannot tell them apart without X-rays. Both arrive as sulfur-yellow dustings on weathered sphalerite, both fluoresce orange when zinc keeps them company, both carry cadmium's toxic signature. But hawleyite forms closer to the surface, where meteoric water seeps through fractures and re-deposits what the earth has already dissolved once. It was not recognized as its own species until 1955, when Canadian mineralogists peered into the crystal structure of yellow powder from a Yukon mine and found the sphalerite lattice instead of wurtzite. James Hawley, the man it was named for, spent his life studying sulfide ores. He would have appreciated the irony: his mineral exists only as microscopic grains, hiding in plain sight among coatings everyone assumed were something else. In the game, hawleyite and greenockite are two faces of the same truth — one crystallizes hot, one cold, and neither cares whether you can tell the difference.

### Simulator Implementation Notes
- **New parameters needed:** None beyond trace_Cd (already needed for greenockite)
- **New events needed:** Sphalerite oxidation releases Cd²⁺ (shared with greenockite)
- **Nucleation rule pseudocode:**
```
IF trace_Cd > 0.1 ppm AND S_total > 1 ppm AND T < 150°C AND Eh < 0.4V → nucleate hawleyite OR greenockite
IF T < 100°C AND fluid_source == "meteoric" → prefer hawleyite (kinetic selection)
IF T > 200°C → prefer greenockite (thermodynamic selection)
```
- **Growth rule pseudocode:**
```
σ = (Cd × S) / Ksp_hawleyite  [Ksp ~10⁻²⁸, same as greenockite]
IF σ > 1 → grow at rate proportional to log(σ)
Rate: fast (easy supersaturation)
Max crystals: small (micro — nucleation cheap, Cd supply limited)
Habit: always powdery/massive (no euhedral crystals)
```
- **Habit selection logic:**
  - Hawleyite: ALWAYS powdery/earthy coating (no crystal habit — natural material never forms visible crystals)
  - Greenockite: T > 100°C, high Cd → euhedral hexagonal barrels; T < 100°C, low Cd → powdery coating
- **Decomposition products:** Oxidation → Cd²⁺ (dissolved) + SO₄²⁻. If CO₃ available → otavite (CdCO₃).

### Variants for Game
- **Variant 1: Meteoric hawleyite** — Bright yellow powdery coating on sphalerite, deposited by meteoric water. Low T (<100°C), dilute Cd. Most common form. Identical appearance to powdery greenockite.
- **Variant 2: Siderite-hosted hawleyite** — Yellow coating on siderite (FeCO₃) instead of sphalerite. Forms where Cd-bearing fluids encounter siderite fractures. Same chemistry, different substrate.
- **Variant 3: Zn-bearing hawleyite** — Paler yellow, fluorescent yellow-orange under LW UV. Zn substitution in sphalerite-type lattice. Requires Zn-rich environment. The glow-in-the-dark variant — same phosphor chemistry as greenockite.

### Dimorph Note
Hawleyite (cubic CdS, sphalerite structure) and greenockite (hexagonal CdS, wurtzite structure) are dimorphs. Greenockite is thermodynamically stable at all temperatures under most geological conditions; hawleyite forms as a metastable low-temperature product from dilute meteoric waters. In practice, the two are visually identical and require XRD or electron diffraction to distinguish. In the simulator, they compete for the same Cd and S resources, with temperature and fluid source determining which dimorph nucleates. See `memory/research-greenockite.md` for the hexagonal partner.

## Linked Paragenetic Sequence: Cadmium Oxidation Zone

Hawleyite and greenockite are the cadmium sulfide pair in a broader oxidation zone sequence:

**Sphalerite** (Zn,Cd)S → primary source of Cd (Cd substitutes for Zn up to ~1%)
  ↓ oxidation, weathering
**Hawleyite/Greenockite** CdS — Cd re-encounters residual sulfide, precipitates as CdS
  ↓ further oxidation, carbonation
**Otavite** CdCO₃ — Cd²⁺ + CO₃²⁻ in oxidizing, carbonate-bearing waters
  ↓ extreme oxidation
**CdO / Cd(OH)₂** — terminal oxidation products (rare as minerals)

This sequence shares formation conditions with the zinc oxidation zone (smithsonite, hydrozincite) and lead oxidation zone (cerussite, anglesite). Cadmium tracks zinc geochemically but precipitates earlier due to lower solubility product. In the simulator, Cd released from sphalerite oxidation can either re-precipitate as CdS (if S²⁻ remains) or migrate to form otavite (if CO₃²⁻ dominates).

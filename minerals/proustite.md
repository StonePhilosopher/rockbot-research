## Species: Proustite

### Identity
- **Formula:** Ag₃AsS₃
- **Crystal system:** Trigonal (rhombohedral)
- **Mineral group:** Sulfosalt (sulfarsenite subgroup)
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 2–2.5
- **Specific gravity:** 5.57–5.64
- **Cleavage:** Distinct on {1011}; poor on {0112}
- **Fracture:** Conchoidal to uneven
- **Luster:** Adamantine to sub-metallic; crystals approach metallic on some faces
- **Space group:** R3c (no. 161)
- **IMA symbol:** Prs

### Color & Appearance
- **Typical color:** Scarlet, vermilion, deep ruby-red; can appear brownish-red in thick masses
- **Color causes:** Charge transfer between silver and the (AsS₃)³⁻ pyramidal groups in the crystal structure; the trigonal framework creates the intense red hue
- **Transparency:** Transparent to translucent in thin splinters and small crystals; opaque in thick masses
- **Streak:** Scarlet-red (vermilion) — this is the diagnostic feature distinguishing proustite from pyrargyrite
- **Notable visual features:** Small crystals and thin splinters glow ruby-red in transmitted light — the origin of the name "ruby silver." Tapering scalenohedral terminations resemble dogtooth calcite in form but not in color.

### Crystal Habits
- **Primary habit:** Prismatic crystals elongated along [001], striated parallel to length; rhombohedra with dominant {1011} or {0112}; scalenohedra with prominent {2131}
- **Common forms/faces:** {1010} prisms, {1011} rhombohedra, {0221} scalenohedra
- **Twin laws:** On {1014} to produce trillings (three-fold cyclic twins); also common on {1011} and {0112}
- **Varieties:** None significant; the proustite-pyrargyrite series has few intermediate members — most specimens are near end-member compositions
- **Special morphologies:** Massive granular, compact; rarely botryoidal or incrustation

### Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

#### Temperature
- **Nucleation temperature range:** 100–350°C
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 180–250°C ("sweet spot" for best crystals)
- **Decomposition temperature:** Melting point ~480°C; begins thermal degradation well before melting
- **Temperature-dependent habits:** Higher temperatures (200–300°C) favor prismatic crystals with well-developed terminations; lower temperatures favor massive or granular forms

#### Chemistry Required
- **Required elements in broth:** Ag (minimum ~2000 ppm), As (minimum ~500 ppm), S (minimum ~500 ppm)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:** Sb (traces stabilize structure; too much Sb routes to pyrargyrite); Cu (associates in epithermal veins but competes for Ag)
- **Inhibiting elements:** High Fe favors pyrite/arsenopyrite; high Pb pushes toward galena; high Zn toward sphalerite; high Cu toward chalcopyrite/enargite; excess Sb (>50% of As+Sb) suppresses proustite in favor of pyrargyrite
- **Required pH range:** Mildly acidic to neutral, pH 4–7
- **Required Eh range:** Reducing to mildly reducing — sulfosalts require reduced sulfur species
- **Required O₂ range:** Low — oxidizing conditions decompose sulfosalts

#### Secondary Chemistry Release
- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** Consumes Ag⁺, As³⁺, and S²⁻ from fluid; no significant byproducts
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** Depletes Ag from fluid — can suppress later native silver precipitation
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Light/heat decomposition releases native silver (metallic Ag) and acanthite (Ag₂S) internally; arsenic may oxidize to scorodite (FeAsO₄·2H₂O) if Fe and O₂ present

#### Growth Characteristics
- **Relative growth rate:** Moderate (2.5× baseline in game) — soft minerals with low lattice energy grow readily
- **Maximum crystal size:** Up to 8 cm in exceptional specimens (Saxony, Germany)
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–5 mm; thumbnail specimens common
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Growth accelerates at higher temperatures within stability window; retrograde behavior possible in epithermal systems
- **Competes with:** Pyrargyrite (Sb-analog), acanthite, native silver, tetrahedrite/tennantite, galena, arsenopyrite

#### Stability
- **Breaks down in heat?** Yes — internal silver migration begins at modest temperatures; thermal degradation produces acanthite inclusions
- **Breaks down in light?** **YES — severely light-sensitive.** Direct sunlight causes darkening within hours to days. The mechanism: photon energy mobilizes Ag⁺ ions in the structure, which migrate and nucleate microscopic acanthite (Ag₂S) crystals internally. The acanthite inclusions scatter light, turning the scarlet crystal black from the inside out. This is irreversible.
- **Dissolves in water?** Very low solubility
- **Dissolves in acid?** Soluble in nitric acid; attacked by cyanide solutions (relevant for historic silver extraction)
- **Oxidizes?** Yes — oxidizes to scorodite + acanthite in weathering zone
- **Dehydrates?** No hydrates
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Not particularly radiation-sensitive

### Paragenesis
- **Forms AFTER:** Acanthite, native silver (in some sequences); arsenopyrite (in higher-T stages); early sulfides like pyrite, chalcopyrite
- **Forms BEFORE:** Native silver (in other sequences — late-stage proustite dissolution can release Ag); cerussite, anglesite in oxidation zone; calcite, barite, quartz as late vug fillings
- **Commonly associated minerals:** Acanthite, native silver, pyrargyrite, galena, sphalerite, chalcopyrite, tetrahedrite, arsenopyrite, stibnite, calcite, barite, quartz
- **Zone:** Epithermal / low-temperature hydrothermal; supergene enrichment zone; oxidation zone of silver deposits
- **Geological environment:** Low-to-moderate temperature epithermal silver veins; supergene enrichment blankets above primary sulfide zones; volcanic-hosted precious metal systems

### Famous Localities
- **Chanarcillo, Atacama, Chile:** The most famous historic source — "Sangre de Toro" (Blood of the Bull). Late 1800s specimens are legendary for color intensity and crystal size. The Dolores Terera mine produced the best clusters.
- **Freiberg District, Saxony, Germany:** Classic European source of sharp, deep-red scalenohedral crystals on calcareous matrix. Specimens from the Andreasberg area reached 5 cm+. Historic mining district dating to the 12th century.
- **Fresnillo, Zacatecas, Mexico:** Modern source of fine proustite in epithermal silver veins. The Veta Grande district nearby also produced notable specimens.
- **Notable specimens:** The Chanarcillo specimens in major museums (Harvard, Smithsonian, Natural History Museum London) are benchmarks for the species. The Colorado solid proustite nodule (Fall River District, Clear Creek County) — a 5-inch mass of solid red proustite with acanthite "skin" — is unique in mineralogy.

### Flavor Text
> Proustite is the color of arterial blood in crystal form — scarlet, vermilion, alive. Hold a thin splinter to lamplight and it glows ruby-red from within, which is why miners called it "ruby silver" and Chanarcillo prospectors named it Sangre de Toro, Blood of the Bull. But light is its enemy. Leave a specimen on a sunny windowsill and within days the scarlet dies, bleeding inward to black as silver ions migrate through the lattice and nucleate acanthite in the heart of the crystal. The darkening is permanent — an internal wound that cannot heal. Joseph Proust, who gave his name to this mineral, discovered that chemical compounds always hold their elements in fixed proportions. Proustite honors that law absolutely: three silvers, one arsenic, three sulfurs, locked in trigonal symmetry. Until the light breaks the lock.

## Paragenetic Sequence Notes (Proustite ↔ Pyrargyrite)

Proustite and pyrargyrite are isostructural (isomorphous) — they share the same trigonal R3c structure with identical atomic positions, differing only in whether the pyramidal group is (AsS₃)³⁻ or (SbS₃)³⁻. They form a solid solution series, but natural specimens cluster near the end-members. Intermediate compositions are rare.

The X_As = As/(As+Sb) ratio controls which mineral nucleates:
- **X_As > 0.5** → proustite field (As-dominant)
- **X_As < 0.5** → pyrargyrite field (Sb-dominant)
- **Sweet spots:** X_As > 0.7 for pure proustite; X_As < 0.3 for pure pyrargyrite

Geologically, pyrargyrite is more common than proustite because antimony is generally more abundant than arsenic in epithermal silver systems. Proustite forms in the relatively rare case where arsenic dominates over antimony in the fluid.

Both minerals are late-stage, low-temperature precipitates in the epithermal paragenetic sequence. They typically form after the major sulfides (galena, sphalerite, chalcopyrite) and often intergrow with or replace earlier acanthite and native silver. In the supergene zone, they may dissolve and redeposit as secondary enrichment phases.

Both share the **light-sensitive decomposition** mechanic — a photodegradation pathway unique among common sulfosalts. This makes them "curator's nightmares" — beautiful but demanding perpetual darkness.

## Identity

- **Formula:** KAlSi₃O₈ (microcline) with trace Pb²⁺ substituting for K⁺
- **Crystal system:** Triclinic (microcline polymorph of K-feldspar)
- **Mineral group:** Tectosilicate / feldspar group
- **Hardness (Mohs):** 6–6.5
- **Specific gravity:** 2.56–2.58
- **Cleavage:** Perfect {001} and good {010} (feldspar characteristic, nearly 90°)
- **Fracture:** Uneven to conchoidal
- **Luster:** Vitreous to pearly

### Color & Appearance

- **Typical color:** Bluish-green to greenish-blue, rarely pale green, white, or colorless
- **Color causes:** Pb²⁺ substituting for K⁺ in the feldspar structure (up to 1.2% PbO, Arnaudov et al. 1967; up to 3.8 mol% PbAl₂Si₂O₈ in extreme cases). Pb²⁺ creates a color center that absorbs in the red-yellow region, leaving blue-green transmitted light. Some studies suggest Pb²⁺ + H₂O interaction is required.
- **Transparency:** Translucent to opaque (gem-quality translucent)
- **Streak:** White
- **Notable visual features:** Cross-hatched twinning (albite + pericline combined — diagnostic for microcline). Color often interrupted by white quartz or feldspar streaks/inclusions. Large crystals common in pegmatites.

### Crystal Habits

- **Primary habit:** Blocky, tabular, or prismatic crystals
- **Common forms/faces:** {001}, {010}, {110} — typical feldspar forms
- **Twin laws:** Albite law (polysynthetic, fine parallel striations), Pericline law (polysynthetic, lath-like individuals), Cross-hatched (albite + pericline combined — diagnostic for microcline)
- **Varieties:** None within amazonite itself, but color grades from pale pastel to vivid saturated blue-green
- **Special morphologies:** Large blocky crystals in pegmatites; massive in granites

## Formation Conditions (SIMULATOR PARAMETERS)

### Temperature

- **Nucleation temperature range:** 300–500°C (pegmatite crystallization)
- **Optimal growth temperature:** 300–400°C (microcline stability field — lower than orthoclase)
- **Decomposition temperature:** 1100°C (melts incongruently)
- **Temperature-dependent habits:**
  - >500°C → orthoclase (monoclinic, no cross-hatching)
  - 400–500°C → microcline (triclinic, cross-hatched twinning develops on slow cooling)
  - <400°C → maximum microcline (fully ordered Al/Si, strongest cross-hatching)
  - Amazonite color requires microcline polymorph + Pb in fluid

### Chemistry Required

- **Required elements in broth:**
  - K: dominant (as K⁺, >1000 ppm)
  - Al: framework component (required for feldspar structure)
  - Si: framework component (as H₄SiO₄, >100 ppm)
  - Pb: trace colorant (10–1000 ppm for color; higher concentrations possible)
- **Optional/enhancing elements:**
  - Na: forms perthite exsolution on cooling (Na-rich albite lamellae in K-feldspar host)
  - H₂O: some studies suggest Pb²⁺-H₂O interaction enhances color
- **Inhibiting elements:**
  - High Ca → favors plagioclase instead of K-feldspar
  - High Fe³⁺ → creates pink orthoclase, competes with green
  - Very high Pb → destabilizes feldspar structure
- **Required pH range:** 7–9 (mildly alkaline, typical for pegmatite fluids)
- **Required Eh range:** Mildly oxidizing (Pb²⁺ must be stable; reducing conditions precipitate galena instead)
- **Required O₂ range:** Moderate (enough for Pb²⁺, not so much that Fe oxidizes and colors pink)

### Secondary Chemistry Release

- **Does it release any chemicals when forming?** No significant byproducts
- **Byproducts of nucleation:** None
- **Byproducts of dissolution/decomposition:** Releases K⁺, Al³⁺, SiO₂ into solution; Pb²⁺ if color centers break down

### Growth Characteristics

- **Relative growth rate:** Slow to moderate (feldspar growth is slower than quartz)
- **Maximum crystal size in nature:** Massive crystals up to 1 meter in pegmatites (Amelia Court House, VA)
- **Typical crystal size in vugs:** 1–10 cm
- **Does growth rate change with temperature?** Yes — slower cooling favors larger crystals and better microcline ordering
- **Competes with:** Albite (Na-feldspar), orthoclase (same chemistry, higher-T polymorph), quartz, mica

### Stability

- **Breaks down in heat?** Incongruent melting at ~1100°C; above 500°C converts to orthoclase (loses cross-hatching, may lose color if Pb mobilizes)
- **Breaks down in light?** No — color is stable in light (unlike realgar, kunzite)
- **Dissolves in water?** Very low solubility; attacked by strong acids
- **Dissolves in acid?** Slowly attacked by HF; resistant to most other acids
- **Oxidizes?** Pb²⁺ is already oxidized (Pb⁰ → Pb²⁺ is the relevant transition for color formation)
- **Dehydrates?** N/A (anhydrous, though some color models involve structural H₂O)
- **Radiation sensitivity:** Generally stable; nearby radioactive minerals could cause smoky coloration (same mechanism as smoky quartz)

## Paragenesis

- **Forms AFTER:** Early-formed minerals in pegmatite (garnet, tourmaline, apatite)
- **Forms BEFORE:** Late-stage pegmatite minerals (lepidolite, spodumene, beryl, columbite)
- **Commonly associated minerals:**
  - Quartz (white streaks in amazonite)
  - Albite (perthite exsolution)
  - Muscovite, lepidolite (mica group)
  - Tourmaline (black schorl, especially in pegmatites)
  - Garnet, beryl, spodumene (pegmatite suite)
  - Galena (Pb source for color)
- **Zone:** Pegmatite core zone (coarse-grained, K-rich)
- **Geological environment:**
  - Granitic pegmatites (primary source of gem amazonite)
  - Granite cavities and miaroles
  - Hydrothermal veins (rare)

## Famous Localities

- **Amelia Court House, Virginia, USA:** Most famous US locality. Massive blue-green crystals up to 30 cm. First mined 1827, still producing. Type locality for "amazonite" in American mineralogy.
- **Pikes Peak area, Colorado, USA:** Large crystals with smoky quartz, albite, microcline, goethite. Associated with Pikes Peak granite.
- **Kola Peninsula, Russia:** Exceptional crystals from granitic pegmatites.
- **Minas Gerais, Brazil:** Pegmatite deposits with gem-quality material.
- **Madagascar:** Fine gem material, often with perthite patterns.
- **Notable specimens:** The "Amazonite Queen" from Colorado — massive crystal cluster with smoky quartz, albite, and goethite. Smithsonian has exceptional specimens.

## Simulator Implementation Notes

### Game Positioning

Amazonite is a **color variety of microcline feldspar**, not a distinct species. However, it deserves its own entry because:
1. It has a unique color mechanism (Pb²⁺ substitution) tied to a specific trace element
2. It requires the microcline polymorph (low-temperature K-feldspar)
3. It teaches players about element-specific coloration in feldspars
4. It connects to galena (Pb source) in a beautiful geological circuit

### New Parameters Needed

- **Pb tracking:** The simulator currently doesn't track Pb separately from other metals. Amazonite requires this.
- **Polymorphism:** The feldspar system already handles orthoclase/microcline/albite/sanidine. Amazonite is a microcline variant.

### Nucleation Rule Pseudocode

```
IF mineral == microcline AND
   K > 1000 ppm AND
   Al > 100 ppm AND
   Si > 100 ppm AND
   Pb > 10 ppm AND
   T < 500°C AND       // Must be microcline stability field
   T > 300°C AND       // Not too cold
   pH 7-9 AND
   Eh mildly oxidizing    // Pb²⁺ stable
THEN
   nucleate amazonite (microcline with Pb color center)
```

### Growth Rule Pseudocode

```
IF amazonite exists AND
   T < 500°C AND
   Pb remains in broth
THEN
   grow at feldspar rate (slower than quartz)
   IF slow cooling event THEN
      develop cross-hatched twinning (microcline ordering)
   IF Pb depleted THEN
      continue as colorless microcline
ELSE IF T > 500°C
   convert to orthoclase (loses cross-hatching, may lose color if Pb mobilizes)
```

### Habit Selection Logic

- **Blocky/tabular:** Default for pegmatite amazonite
- **Cross-hatched:** Develops on slow cooling (<10°C/hour)
- **Perthite pattern:** If Na present, exsolve albite lamellae on cooling

### Decomposition Products

- **Heat >500°C:** Converts to orthoclase (monoclinic), may lose Pb and color
- **Strong acid:** Dissolves as K⁺, Al³⁺, SiO₂, Pb²⁺ in solution
- **Weathering:** Alters to clay minerals (kaolinite), releases K and Pb

## Variants for Game

- **Variant 1: Pegmatite Amazonite**
  - Large blocky crystals, cross-hatched twinning
  - Conditions: slow-cooled pegmatite, Pb present, K-rich
  - Associated with quartz, mica, tourmaline

- **Variant 2: Granite Cavity Amazonite**
  - Smaller crystals in miaroles (gas cavities in granite)
  - Conditions: miarole environment, vapor-phase crystallization
  - Often associated with smoky quartz

- **Variant 3: Colorless Microcline**
  - Same chemistry, no Pb
  - Conditions: Pb-depleted or high-T orthoclase that cooled to microcline
  - White to pale pink, no blue-green color

## Key Teaching Points for Game

1. **Color is element-specific** — Pb²⁺ creates blue-green, Fe³⁺ creates pink (orthoclase)
2. **Polymorphism matters** — same chemistry, different structure, different color response
3. **Trace elements can be beautiful** — "impurities" create gem varieties
4. **Paragenesis is real** — galena dissolution → Pb release → amazonite coloration
5. **Cooling rate affects texture** — slow cooling = cross-hatched twinning = diagnostic microcline
